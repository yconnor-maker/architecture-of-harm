# Chapter 7: Pregnancy Behind Bars

---

Consider two possible endings for the patient in the preceding chapter — the pregnant woman whose partner loaded a gun on her, whose partner jumped her, whose partner burned their ultrasound pictures.

In the first ending, she follows the physician's safety plan. She hides her documents. She leaves the area. She delivers her baby somewhere else. She survives.

In the second ending, he escalates. She defends herself. She uses a weapon — the same weapon he loaded on her. He is injured or killed. She is arrested. She is charged with assault or homicide. She is pregnant at the time of her arrest. She enters the correctional system.

In the second ending, the system does not see a victim. It sees a perpetrator. She enters a facility where there is a 62.3 percent chance [^1] she will not be pregnancy-tested. Where she may labor in shackles. Where her baby will be separated from her within 72 hours. [^2] Where, if her sentence exceeds 15 months, [^3] the Adoption and Safe Families Act will begin the clock toward permanent termination of her parental rights — and she will lose the child she carried through the violence.

Ninety-four percent of incarcerated women have experienced domestic violence. [^4] Seventy-four percent of women imprisoned for murder or manslaughter had experienced intimate partner violence within a year of the offense that led to their conviction. [^4] The system that failed to protect them then punishes them for surviving.

---

## The System Before Dobbs

Between 1980 and 2023, the number of incarcerated women in the United States grew by more than 600 percent — from 26,326 to 186,244 — a rate of growth twice that of men. [^5] The drivers were the War on Drugs, mandatory minimum sentencing, and the criminalization of poverty. Women in state prisons are incarcerated for drug or property offenses at rates substantially higher than men — the proportion convicted of drug offenses rose from 12 percent in 1986 to 25 percent by 2021. [^6] Sixty percent of incarcerated women lived below the federal poverty line before arrest. [^5] By 2023, over one million women were under some form of criminal justice supervision — prison, jail, probation, or parole — and the female incarcerated population rebounded 22 percent in a single post-pandemic year. [^7]

This growth was a racial project. Between 1986 and 1991 — a single five-year period — incarceration of Black women in state prisons for drug offenses surged 828 percent. [^6] Despite a 69 percent decline in Black women's imprisonment rate since 2000, Black women remain imprisoned at 68 per 100,000 — 1.7 times the white women's rate of 41 per 100,000. [^6] In the same period, white women's imprisonment rate actually rose 18 percent, [^6] narrowing the disparity ratio while disguising the underlying expansion of the carceral apparatus into all women's lives. Latina women are imprisoned at 51 per 100,000, 1.2 times the white rate. [^6] American Indian and Alaska Native women experience an incarceration rate 4.3 times higher than white women — the highest disparity of any group [^8] — driven by the intersection of federal jurisdiction on tribal lands, chronic underfunding of Indian Health Service, and historical trauma.

The geography matters as much as the demography. Idaho imprisons women at 152 per 100,000 — the highest rate in the nation. Rhode Island imprisons women at 6 per 100,000 — the lowest. [^9] That is a twenty-five-fold variation. [^9] A pregnant woman's exposure to every harm documented in this chapter — prenatal care gaps, shackling, postpartum separation, Medicaid disruption, and family severance — is determined not by clinical need or individual risk but by which side of a state line she was standing on when she was arrested.

## The Abuse-to-Incarceration Pipeline

Over 80 percent of incarcerated women have histories of physical or sexual abuse [^9] — many are imprisoned for defending themselves against their abusers. The abuse routinely continues in custody. The December 2024 permanent closure of FCI Dublin—following a systemic sexual abuse scandal resulting in a $116 million settlement for over 100 survivors and a February 2025 consent decree mandating independent monitoring—demonstrates that facilities themselves function as sites of continuous victimization [^33]. The cases of Cyntoia Brown-Long and Marissa Alexander are the ones that became nationally visible, but the structural pattern they represent is routine: self-defense standards built around a male model of combat — the requirements of imminent threat and proportional force — systematically fail women who use a weapon against an unarmed attacker twice their size, or who strike preemptively after years of escalating violence. Black women are disproportionately arrested in domestic violence incidents even when they are the victims, and receive longer sentences than white women in comparable self-defense cases. Stand Your Ground laws, written to protect the person with the gun in the confrontation, have not functioned equivalently for Black women.

The pipeline begins even earlier. Black girls are 14 percent of the female youth population but 39 percent of girls in juvenile residential facilities. [^10] Native girls are confined at 39 per 100,000 — four times the rate of white girls. Black girls are confined at 29 per 100,000 — three times the rate. [^11] Between 40 and 81 percent of justice-involved girls report histories of sexual abuse, compared to 20 percent in the general population, [^10] and girls are nearly twice as likely as boys to report five or more distinct forms of abuse. [^10] Forty percent of youth placed for status offenses — truancy, curfew violations, running away — are girls, despite representing only 15 percent of the total incarcerated youth population. [^11] The pipeline is widening: girls' share of all youth arrests rose from 19 percent in 1985 to 31 percent by 2024 [^34]. These offenses are survival behaviors. Running away from a home where you are being raped is not a criminal act. But it is treated as one, and it is the first contact point in a system that will follow these girls into adult incarceration, adult pregnancy, and adult loss.

## The Pregnancy Testing Gap and the Data Void

Only 37.7 percent of U.S. jails conduct pregnancy tests upon entry — a number that comes from the only national-scale jail dataset in the field, the 2016–2017 Johns Hopkins Pregnancy in Prison Statistics study. [^1] Most facilities rely on self-reporting, which misses pregnancies where women don't yet know they're pregnant, fear disclosure due to potential loss of autonomy, or don't trust correctional healthcare providers. Pregnancies are routinely discovered late, missing critical windows for prenatal care initiation and, where legal, abortion access.

The federal picture is marginally better but appallingly recent. The first comprehensive federal data on pregnancy in prisons — the BJS Maternal Health Supplement — was not collected until 2024 and was published in a landmark 2025 report covering calendar year 2023. In the 47 jurisdictions reporting, 88 percent of female admissions were tested for pregnancy, and 2 percent tested positive. [^12] On a single day — December 31, 2023 — 328 pregnant women were in state or federal prisons, representing 0.5 percent of the female prison population. [^13] Approximately 58,000 pregnant people enter U.S. jails and prisons annually. [^14] That the federal government did not begin counting them until 2024 is not an oversight. It is a design feature of a system that does not want to be measured.

The BJS data recorded 727 pregnancy outcomes across 49 jurisdictions in 2023: 665 live births (91.5 percent), 47 miscarriages (6.5 percent), and 15 abortions (2.1 percent). [^15] These numbers are a beginning, but they are not accountability. The BJS report excludes jails, private facilities, and the metrics that would actually permit evaluation of care quality — preterm birth rates, low birth weight, NICU admissions, and maternal mortality. The 2025 BJS report itself omitted jails, private facilities, and critical metrics [^35]. A 2024 Government Accountability Office review concluded that comprehensive national data on pregnancy outcomes and care quality in correctional facilities does not exist. [^16] Without it, tracking maternal or infant mortality in custody, identifying systemic failures in obstetric care, or comparing outcomes across jurisdictions is structurally impossible. The data gap itself prevents racial disaggregation — you cannot document a disparity you refuse to measure.

Among the pregnant women whose race was recorded in the 2023 BJS data, 60 percent were white, 20 percent were Black, 9 percent were Hispanic, and 4 percent were American Indian or Alaska Native. [^14] ACOG's Committee Opinion 830 states that incarcerated pregnant people must receive care equivalent to non-incarcerated patients [^17] — comprehensive prenatal care, high-risk screening, twenty-four-hour emergency access, breastfeeding support. These are professional recommendations. No enforcement mechanism exists. No facility is required to follow them.

Prison diets are designed for adult men. Pregnant women require 300+ additional calories per day, increased protein, and specific micronutrients. [^17] Many incarcerated pregnant women report being unable to modify their diet, having no access to prenatal vitamins without paying a copay, and supplementing from commissary with wages of $0.13 to $0.52 per hour. [^18]

## Shackling: The Practice That Won't Die

As of 2025, approximately 40 states, D.C., and the federal government — through the 2018 First Step Act — prohibit the use of restraints on incarcerated individuals during labor and delivery. [^19] Only three states — New York, Illinois, and California — completely ban shackling of pregnant women. [^19] The remaining jurisdictions include exceptions for "extraordinary circumstances" or subjective "security risk" assessments — loopholes that are routinely exploited. Roughly ten states have no anti-shackling legislation at all, though 2026 legislative actions in Utah and Virginia signal ongoing state-level battles [^36]. Missouri's HB 495, signed in March 2025, extended protections to county jails, prohibiting restraints during the third trimester, labor, and for 48 hours postpartum [^20] — an expansion that underscores how recently even basic protections have been absent at the local level.

The laws do not work. A 2019 study of perinatal nurses found 83 percent reported that their patients were shackled "sometimes" to "all of the time." Twelve percent reported patients were always shackled. [^21] In one-third of cases, the only justification was adherence to facility protocol — not any individualized assessment of risk. Even in New York — which banned shackling in 2009 — a 2015 compliance audit found 85 percent of interviewed incarcerated women had been shackled while pregnant. [^22] The law had been in effect for six years. The audit relied on facility self-reporting. There is no independent monitoring mechanism, no penalty for violation, no mandatory data collection on restraint use, and no sanctions regime.

ACOG, AMA, the American Nurses Association, the American Public Health Association, and the National Commission on Correctional Health Care unanimously condemn shackling as medically dangerous. The clinical case is unambiguous: shackling interferes with emergency obstetric interventions including cesarean sections, increases fall and injury risk, impedes fetal monitoring, raises the risk of deep vein thrombosis, and causes severe pain and psychological trauma during labor. Jurisdictions that prohibit it report zero escapes and zero threats to staff from pregnant prisoners. [^23] The practice persists because enforcement is absent, correctional staff training is inconsistent, and facility-level protocols routinely contradict state law — particularly during transport to outside medical facilities.

## Abortion Access at 2.1 Percent

Prior to Dobbs, incarcerated women retained a constitutional right to abortion under the Fourteenth Amendment. The access reality was a different matter. A 2021 study found only half of state prisons surveyed allowed abortion in both first and second trimesters. Fourteen percent prohibited it entirely. Thirty-two percent had no written policy at all. Two-thirds of prisons required women to self-pay for abortions. [^24] The Hyde Amendment bars federal Medicaid from covering abortion. Incarcerated women earn pennies per hour.

The 2023 BJS data captured 15 abortions across 49 jurisdictions — a prison abortion rate of 2.1 percent of all pregnancy outcomes, against a national abortion ratio of 18 percent. [^15] The barriers — financial, logistical, informational, and discretionary — made the legal right functionally inaccessible for most women.

Post-Dobbs, that legal right no longer exists in the states where the majority of women's prisons are located. A woman incarcerated in Alabama, Texas, Mississippi, or Georgia cannot access abortion at any price, for any reason, at any gestational age below the state's threshold — and prison transport does not extend across state lines for elective procedures. The constitutional framework that once provided at least a theoretical backstop is gone.

## The Criminalization of Pregnancy Outcomes

Alabama's chemical endangerment statute — §26-15-3.2, originally a 2006 law targeting methamphetamine lab exposure — was expanded by the Alabama Supreme Court in 2013 and 2014 to include "viable unborn children." In 2018, Alabama enshrined fetal personhood in its state constitution. The result: hundreds of women faced pregnancy-related criminal charges in the two-year period between June 2022 and June 2024. [^25] A woman whose fetus is exposed to a controlled substance faces a Class C felony carrying one to ten years in prison. If the pregnancy ends in death — miscarriage, stillbirth — the charge escalates to a Class A felony. Prosecutors have applied the law aggressively, and its impact falls disproportionately on women of color in low-income communities. [^25]

The expansion of fetal personhood frameworks — the same legal architecture that underlies post-Dobbs abortion criminalization — is being applied to substance use cases in states with aggressive prosecutors. As this report's chapter on substance use documents, the 1,396 arrests, 79 percent in five Southern states, 46.5 percent in Alabama alone, [^25] are not abstract policy. They are the intake pipeline for every harm documented in this chapter. A pregnant woman arrested on a felony chemical endangerment charge enters a correctional system with no mandatory prenatal care standards, a 62.3 percent chance [^1] of not being pregnancy-tested, an 83 percent chance of being shackled during labor, and near-zero access to medication-assisted treatment. [^1] The same administration that pardoned 23 individuals convicted of violating the FACE Act is presiding over an expansion of the carceral apparatus targeting pregnant women.

## Mother-Child Separation and the Destruction of Bonds

In the vast majority of U.S. correctional facilities, newborns are separated from their incarcerated mothers within 24 to 72 hours of birth. [^2] There is no national policy governing this separation. Only 11 state prison systems and the federal Bureau of Prisons operate nursery programs, and their scale is negligible: at the end of 2023, just 86 women were participating — against 665 live births that year. [^18] Eligibility is typically restricted to nonviolent offenses with near-term release dates, meaning the women with the longest separations are systematically excluded. No federal mandate requires states to offer nursery programs. No national standards exist for their operation.

Sixty-two percent of women in state prisons have a child under 18. Incarcerated mothers are five times more likely than incarcerated fathers to have children enter foster care. [^3] The Adoption and Safe Families Act of 1997 requires states to file termination-of-parental-rights petitions when a child has been in foster care for 15 of the most recent 22 months. [^3] ASFA includes no exception for incarceration. For mothers serving sentences longer than 15 months — the majority — this law all but guarantees permanent severance of the parent-child relationship, regardless of the mother's capacity to parent upon release. Incarcerated parents face structural barriers to completing court-ordered reunification services — parenting classes, substance abuse treatment — while behind bars. Maintaining "significant contact" is nearly impossible when phone calls cost a dollar a minute, prisons are geographically remote from courts and communities, and visitation policies are restrictive. The law treats incarceration as evidence of abandonment. Approximately one in eight incarcerated parents with children in foster care faces termination of parental rights. [^3]

The psychological toll is measured. Incarcerated postpartum women experience postpartum depression at rates of 35 to 80 percent — compared to 12 to 20 percent in the general population. [^26] The primary driver is not pre-existing mental illness. It is forced separation during the critical attachment window. Separation within hours of birth causes an acute grief reaction, compounding pre-existing trauma histories and the near-total absence of mental health services in correctional facilities. There is no federal requirement for postpartum mental health screening in correctional settings. The children, in turn, face cascading consequences: sons are 25 percent more likely to drop out of school, and across the population, children of incarcerated mothers experience higher rates of migraines, asthma, anxiety, aggressive behavior, and involvement in the juvenile justice system. They are more likely to be adopted and less likely to be reunified with their parents. [^7]

## Reentry: The Coverage Gap as a Kill Zone

A structural win occurred effective January 1, 2026: the Consolidated Appropriations Act of 2024 mandated that all states must suspend — not terminate — Medicaid for incarcerated adults, eliminating the re-enrollment gap that previously left formerly incarcerated women without coverage for weeks to months after release. [^27] Separately, 19 states have been approved for Section 1115 Medicaid reentry waivers allowing pre-release services — including case management, medication-assisted treatment, and a 30-day medication supply — for up to 90 days before discharge. [^28] For incarcerated youth, mandatory Medicaid screening and case management 30 days pre-release took effect a year earlier, on January 1, 2025. [^29]

This matters. But it is being enacted in the same administrative period that is proposing $400 to $880 billion in cuts to the Medicaid program itself, [^27] and in the same states where the coverage a woman returns to may no longer include the behavioral health services, MAT access, and postpartum care that would allow her to survive reentry. Nineteen states have waivers. Thirty-one do not. For a pregnant woman released from a facility in a non-waiver state, the coverage gap remains a period of maximum medical vulnerability — the weeks after release when prenatal care lapses, prescriptions go unfilled, and the stressors of reentry converge with the physiological demands of pregnancy or postpartum recovery. Without a coverage bridge, women face delayed prenatal care, missed postpartum visits, interrupted MAT, and medication nonadherence — conditions that directly increase maternal mortality risk.

The Black Maternal Health Momnibus Act, introduced multiple sessions by the Congressional Black Caucus but not yet enacted, would begin to address this intersection. Among its provisions: federal funding for doula programs in correctional facilities, a federal ban on shackling at all stages of pregnancy, and commissioned studies on incarcerated maternal mortality with an explicit racial disparity focus. [^30] It remains proposed legislation. In the meantime, programs that exist at the margins have shown what is possible. The Minnesota Prison Doula Project has demonstrated high rates of vaginal deliveries and healthy birthweight. New York City's Growing HOPE program received a $2 million expansion grant in 2024. Mount Sinai's Arnhold Institute published its "Born Inside" report in 2026, documenting birth experiences during incarceration. [^31] These programs provide emotional, physical, and educational support through pregnancy, labor, and postpartum — increasing parenting confidence and partially addressing separation trauma. They are recognized as key interventions for maternal mortality disparities among incarcerated people of color. They remain exceptions, not systems.

## The Pipeline, Measured

The data documented in this report's chapter on substance use — 1,396 arrests, 79 percent in five Southern states, 46.5 percent in Alabama alone [^25] — connects directly to these incarceration outcomes. A pregnant woman arrested on a felony chemical endangerment charge enters a correctional system with no mandatory prenatal care standards, a 62.3 percent chance [^1] of not being pregnancy-tested, an 83 percent chance of being shackled during labor, and near-zero access to MAT. [^1] If she delivers in custody, her infant is separated within 72 hours. If her sentence exceeds 15 months, [^3] ASFA timelines begin running toward the permanent termination of her parental rights. Her child enters the foster care system and begins accumulating the Adverse Childhood Experiences that, as this report's chapter on perinatal mental health documents, triple the risk of preeclampsia in that child's own future pregnancy.

The pipeline is not theoretical. Every link has been measured. And every link is being strengthened, not broken, by the current policy trajectory.
