# Chapter 13: The Demographic Collapse

## The Proof That It Backfired

The stated rationale for overturning Roe v. Wade, for the proliferation of state-level abortion bans, and for the broader pro-natalist agenda of the conservative movement is that restricting abortion will produce more births, strengthen the nuclear family, and reverse the demographic decline of the United States. The data says the opposite is happening. Every measured indicator of reproductive behavior since the Dobbs decision shows Americans — particularly young Americans — choosing fewer children, not more, and choosing permanent measures to ensure it.

If the goal was more babies, the policy has failed on its own terms. If the goal was something else — the enforcement of patriarchal control over female bodies, the punishment of sexual autonomy, the consolidation of a political coalition organized around the subordination of women — then it is succeeding. But it is not producing the demographic outcome its architects claim to want.

## The Fertility Rate Is at an All-Time Low

The U.S. total fertility rate has plummeted to 1.6 children per woman[^1] — 24 percent below the 2.1 replacement rate required to sustain population without immigration. Provisional 2025 data from the CDC's National Center for Health Statistics shows a general fertility rate of just 53.1 per 1,000 women aged 15 to 44[^2], continuing the free fall. More than 52 percent[^20] of women aged 20 to 39 are childless[^3]. The marriage rate has fallen to 46.4 percent — the lowest since the Census Bureau began tracking it in 1940.

These are not new trends. The fertility rate has been declining for decades, driven by expanded educational and economic opportunity for women, the rising cost of childcare, and the structural impossibility of raising children on wages that have been stagnant in real terms since the 1970s. The cost of raising a child through age 17 now exceeds $310,000[^4]. What Dobbs has done is not reverse these trends — it has accelerated them, by adding fear to the list of reasons women choose not to become mothers.

The acceleration is measurable in intention, not just outcome. According to Pew Research Center, 47 percent[^96] of childless adults under 50[^5] now say they are unlikely to ever have children — a 10-percentage-point surge from 37 percent in 2018[^6]. When asked why, 57 percent say the same thing[^7]: "I just don't want to." This is not ambivalence. It is a generation that has watched the political system weaponize pregnancy, surveill miscarriage, and prosecute medical decisions, and concluded that the safest reproductive plan is no reproduction at all.

## The Economics of Not Having Children

The decision not to have children is not purely ideological. It is, for millions of women, arithmetic. Childcare costs have surged past pre-pandemic levels, reaching a national average of $16,000 per year per child in 2025[^8] — a figure that exceeds in-state college tuition in many states and consumes 35 percent of single-parent median income[^9]. The federal government's own affordability benchmark — that families should spend no more than 7 percent of household income on childcare[^10] — is unmet in every single state for infant care. 46 percent of children under six live in licensed childcare deserts—a crisis that worsened in 2025 following the expiration of pandemic stabilization funds, costing the economy $172 billion annually.[^11]

And childcare is only the first bill. Insured patients pay $2,563 to $3,071 out of pocket for delivery alone[^12]; uninsured women face $7,000 to $30,000 or more[^13]. New mothers are twice as likely as their non-birthing peers to carry medical debt[^14] exceeding $250. According to the Peterson-KFF Health System Tracker, 46 percent of Black adults with medical debt report credit score damage[^15] — compared to 32 percent[^75] of White adults[^16] — meaning that the act of giving birth can lock a Black woman out of housing, employment, and mainstream credit for years afterward.

The downstream economics are equally punishing. Women denied abortion experience a 40 percent income decline over five years[^17] compared to women who obtained one, according to the landmark UCSF Turnaway Study. The denied women face three times higher odds of poverty[^18] and a $9,260 annual income gap[^19] that compounds across a lifetime. For Black women who are denied, the income decline is 52 percent[^20]. These are not projections. These are observed outcomes, rigorously controlled, from one of the most important longitudinal studies in reproductive health economics. And Dobbs has scaled the Turnaway penalty from a clinical research finding to a population-level economic event affecting millions of women in 21 ban states[^21].

The cascading costs are staggering. Unintended pregnancies cost federal and state governments $21 billion annually[^22], primarily through Medicaid-funded births and infant care. Post-Dobbs birth increases in ban states are projected to cost Medicaid an additional $4.6 billion from 2023 to 2026[^23] alone — 42,000 additional Medicaid-funded births[^24] per year in states that stripped women of the ability to choose. Meanwhile, the Institute for Women's Policy Research calculates that state abortion restrictions cost the U.S. economy $133 billion annually[^25] and suppress 556,000 women from the labor force entirely[^26]. Goldman Sachs projects that ban states will lose $105 billion in cumulative GDP over the next decade through reduced female labor force participation, healthcare cost increases, and talent relocation. The pro-natalist policy is not just failing to produce babies. It is producing poverty.

## The Motherhood Penalty and the Fatherhood Bonus

The economic architecture punishing motherhood operates at every stage. Mothers face a 4 to 7 percent wage penalty[^27] per child, while fathers receive a measurable earnings boost — a dynamic so well-documented in labor economics it has its own name: the fatherhood bonus[^28]. The Pregnant Workers Fairness Act, which took effect in June 2023, was the first federal law to require employers to provide reasonable accommodations for pregnancy — meaning that for the entirety of American history before that date, a woman could be fired for asking for a bathroom break during a high-risk pregnancy.

The United States remains the only wealthy nation without federally mandated paid parental leave. Seventy-five percent of private-sector workers lack access to any paid leave[^29]. One in four American mothers returns to work within two weeks[^30] of giving birth — not because she has recovered, but because she cannot afford to stay home. One in three fathers takes no leave at all[^31]. The maternity leave gap alone costs women $37 billion in lifetime earnings[^32]. The absence of paid leave does not merely reflect a policy gap — it reflects a political choice to make motherhood a financial catastrophe for everyone except the wealthy.

Women perform 2.6 hours per day of unpaid domestic work[^33] compared to 1.9 hours for men[^34], according to the Bureau of Labor Statistics. In dual-earner households, women still perform 73 percent of childcare[^35]. The caregiving crisis has forced more than 455,000 women out of the workforce between January and August 2025 alone, with 42 percent citing caregiving costs[^36] as the reason. Unpaid care work performed by women is valued at $1.1 trillion annually[^37] — an economic contribution that appears in no GDP calculation and earns no Social Security credit. The lifetime earnings loss for a mother averages $300,000[^38[^95]]. The system does not merely fail to support mothers. It extracts from them.

## The Sterilization Surge

Prior to Dobbs, surgical sterilization rates had been declining since the mid-1970s, steadily replaced by Long-Acting Reversible Contraception like IUDs. The 2022 decision violently reversed a 40-year clinical trend.

In August 2022 alone[^39] — the first full month after the Dobbs decision — tubal sterilizations among young adults aged 19 to 26 leapt 70 percent[^40] compared to pre-decision baselines. Vasectomies among the same age group surged 95 percent[^41]. By September 2023, tubal ligations had approximately doubled and vasectomies tripled[^42] compared to pre-Dobbs levels. These are not fluctuations. They are young people — many of them nulliparous, meaning they have never had a child — choosing permanent, irreversible solutions because they do not trust the legal environment to allow them to control their fertility through any reversible means.

The sustained data is equally stark. Tubal sterilizations in states with abortion bans rose approximately 3 percent every month[^43] following Dobbs — a compounding trajectory that shows no sign of leveling off. Vasectomies among privately insured men rose 26 percent overall[^44]. In Texas, urologists report a massive, sustained surge in vasectomy requests, with cash-pay clinics heavily marketing the procedure at $500 to $900[^45] as a direct response to the state's political climate.

This is the clinical signature of a population that has concluded its government cannot be trusted with its reproductive future.

## The Texas Paradox

Texas provides the clearest illustration of the backfire. Despite enacting one of the most restrictive abortion bans in the country — a total ban with no rape or incest exception — Texas's fertility rate dropped 2.07 percent in 2023[^46] to a new state low of 60.6 births per 1,000[^47] women of reproductive age. The law designed to force birth is producing fewer births, because the women subject to it are choosing sterilization, leaving the state, or avoiding pregnancy entirely.

The initial post-Dobbs data showed a brief birth spike — approximately 32,000 excess births annually[^48] in ban states in the first year (documented in this report's chapter on children). But that spike represents women who were already pregnant at the time of the decision and could not access abortion. The sustained trajectory is the opposite: a population actively suppressing its own fertility in response to state coercion.

The economic consequences in Texas are visible in the clinical data. Texas hospitals saw a 50 percent increase in sepsis[^49] from second-trimester pregnancy loss management after the ban — the direct clinical cost of physicians delaying standard-of-care intervention out of fear of prosecution. Pregnancy-related hospitalization costs in ban states rose 22 percent from 2022 to 2025[^50], driven by increased complexity and delayed care presentation. Emergency room visits for reproductive health complaints surged 18 percent[^51] in ban states, generating $2.3 billion in additional emergency care[^52] spending. The policy is not merely failing to produce more births. It is producing more complications, more near-deaths, and more bills — paid by the same taxpayers whose elected officials promised this would strengthen families.

## The Intimacy Crisis

The demographic collapse extends beyond contraception to the fundamental architecture of human connection. Seventy percent of couples[^53] in states with abortion bans changed their contraceptive practices after Dobbs. One in five women[^54] in ban states reports avoiding sex entirely due to pregnancy fear — a direct chilling effect of the legal risk now attached to sexual activity. The anxiety surrounding accidental pregnancy in states with total bans has severely hindered spontaneous intimacy and driven a massive surge in demand for LARCs among young, childless individuals who view dating in their state as legally hazardous.

The broader sex recession predates Dobbs but has accelerated in its wake. The percentage of young adults aged 18 to 29 reporting no sexual activity in the past year has doubled, from 12 percent in 2010 to 24 percent in 2024[^55]. One in three young men[^56] reports no partnered sex at all. Weekly sex among adults 18 to 64 declined from 55 percent in 1990 to 37 percent in 2024[^57]. The rate of young adults living with a partner fell from 42 percent in 2014 to 32 percent[^75] in 2024[^58] — a collapse in cohabitation that directly maps to declining births.

## The Gender Chasm

The 51-point gender gap[^59] among young voters in the 2024 election reflects a generational rupture that extends well beyond electoral politics. Young women overwhelmingly prioritize reproductive autonomy and view the conservative movement as an existential threat to their physical safety and economic trajectory. Young men are drifting toward the right-wing reactionary channels that frame female empowerment as a zero-sum loss. The resulting ideological chasm between men and women under 30 is producing measurable changes in relationship formation, cohabitation rates, and the willingness of women to enter partnerships with men whose political identity signals danger.

According to Pew and YouGov, 25 percent of Americans would not date someone[^60] with opposing political views. Political homophily in marriages has increased from 50 percent in 1960 to 77 percent in 2023[^61]. Thirty-five percent of Gen Z women[^62] say they would not date a Republican-voting man. OKCupid data shows that liberal-conservative match rates fell 30 percent since 2020[^63]. The policy environment has not merely chilled sexual behavior — it has fractured the ideological conditions under which heterosexual relationships form in the first place.

The 4B Movement — a South Korean feminist framework of no dating, no sex, no marriage, and no childbirth — surged virally in the United States after the 2024 election, adopted by American women as a form of reproductive protest. Women are not merely choosing fewer children. Some are choosing to exit heterosexual partnership entirely, as a conscious political act in a political environment they experience as hostile to their survival.

Gen Z is 40 percent more likely to be single[^64] than Millennials were at the same age. Roughly 30 percent of Gen Z adults[^65] have never been in a romantic relationship. Forty-five percent of adults aged 18 to 34[^66] report recent "situationship" experience — intimate contact without commitment, driven by economic precarity and a deliberate avoidance of the entanglements that once preceded parenthood. The median time to relationship exclusivity has doubled since 2014[^67]. The dating app infrastructure that was supposed to facilitate connection is instead generating fatigue: 79 percent of users report frustration[^68], and 48 percent say apps make it harder[^69] to find a partner than meeting someone in person.

Meanwhile, 25 percent of young men[^70] aged 15 to 35 report feeling lonely "a lot," and 15 percent of American men now report having zero close friends[^71] — up from 3 percent in 1990[^72]. The friendship recession is feeding a male loneliness crisis that the manosphere exploits and the policy establishment ignores. The men who cannot form friendships become the men who cannot form partnerships, who become the men who resent the women who will not partner with them, who become the electoral base for the politicians who restrict women's rights — completing a cycle that produces fewer families at every turn.

## The Loneliness Epidemic

The Surgeon General declared loneliness a public health epidemic — and noted that its health effects are equivalent to smoking 15 cigarettes per day. Social isolation increases the risk of premature death by 29 percent[^74][^73], coronary heart disease by 29 percent[^74][^73], stroke by 32 percent[^75], and dementia by 50 percent in older adults[^76]. The economic toll is equally measurable: $6.7 billion[^77] in additional annual Medicare spending attributable to social isolation alone.

Women bear a disproportionate burden: they report higher rates of social isolation, carry the weight of caregiving that fragments social networks, and now, in ban states, face the additional isolation of navigating reproductive decisions in a surveillance environment where disclosing a pregnancy can trigger investigation. The collapse of what sociologists call "third places" — churches, civic organizations, bars, union halls — has eliminated the infrastructure where adults once formed the relationships that preceded family formation. Church membership dropped below 50 percent for the first time[^78] in recorded polling in 2020, down from 76 percent in 1976[^79]. Civic organization participation has fallen more than 30 percent since the 1990s[^80]. The bar and tavern count in America dropped from 190,000 in 1970 to fewer than 70,000 by 2020[^81]. The places where people once met, courted, and built the social trust that underlies family formation are gone.

The policy architecture is creating the loneliness it claims to cure. The "childless cat lady" rhetoric that defined the 2024 Republican campaign explicitly frames childlessness as moral failure and civic deficiency — while the policies of the same political movement make childbearing financially devastating, medically dangerous, and legally surveilled. Women are not choosing loneliness. They are choosing survival in a system that has made motherhood the riskier option.

## The International Lesson

The United States is not the first country to attempt to legislate its way out of demographic decline. It is merely the latest to discover that coercion does not produce babies — it produces resistance. Hungary, under Viktor Orbán, has spent more than 5 percent of its GDP on pro-natalist subsidies — tax breaks, housing grants, loan forgiveness for mothers of three or more — and its fertility rate remains below replacement. South Korea spends $270 billion[^82] on fertility incentives and recorded a fertility rate of 0.72 in 2023[^83], the lowest on earth. Japan has thrown subsidies, workplace reforms, and dating programs at its demographic crisis for two decades and still cannot move the needle above 1.2[^84]. Poland's aggressive pro-natalist policy produced a brief uptick in births that reversed within three years.

The consistent finding across every country that has tried is this: women do not have more children because governments tell them to. They have more children when they have affordable childcare, paid leave, economic security, affordable housing, and confidence that the healthcare system will not kill them in childbirth. The United States offers none of these. It offers instead the world's highest maternal mortality rate among wealthy nations — 32.9 per 100,000[^85] — a cost of raising a child that exceeds a quarter million dollars[^86], zero weeks of federally mandated paid leave, and a political movement that responds to women's refusal to reproduce by calling them selfish. The international evidence is unambiguous: you cannot punish women into motherhood. You can only support them into it. And the United States has chosen punishment.

## The Workforce Spiral

The demographic collapse is not merely a social phenomenon. It is an economic death spiral. Fewer births today mean fewer workers in twenty years — fewer taxpayers, fewer Social Security contributors, fewer consumers, fewer caregivers for the aging population that the current political class will, by then, have become.

Abortion access within 50 miles is associated with an 8 percent increase[^87] in women's labor force participation and a $4,500 annual earnings[^88] gain, according to research published in the Journal of Health Economics. The effect is strongest for Black women, who see a 12 percent increase[^89] in labor force participation when a nearby clinic opens. Every clinic that closes, every ban that takes effect, every provider who flees a ban state for a jurisdiction where practicing medicine is not a felony — each one subtracts women from the workforce, subtracts earnings from the economy, and subtracts tax revenue from the states that imposed the bans. Ban states are already showing a divergent trajectory: female labor force participation is declining 3.2 percent[^90] in ban states while rising 1.1 percent[^91] in non-ban states.

The workforce implications extend beyond the women directly affected. The caregiving crisis forces women out of paid work to perform unpaid care. The childcare crisis makes paid work financially irrational for many mothers. The student debt crisis — women hold approximately 65 percent[^92] of the $1.77 trillion[^93] in outstanding student loans — delays partnership and parenthood by years. Each $10,000 in student debt reduces marriage probability by 1.4 percent[^94]. The housing affordability crisis pushes the average first-time homebuyer age to a record high of 38[^95], and 47 percent[^96] of young adults cite housing costs as the reason they have delayed committing to a serious relationship. These are not separate crises. They are the same crisis, expressed in different ledgers — and they all terminate in the same outcome: fewer families, fewer children, a smaller tax base, and a nation that is eating its own demographic future because it would rather control women than support them.

## What This Means for the Trump Administration's Legacy

The pro-natalist agenda has produced an anti-natalist outcome. The administration that positioned itself as the champion of the American family has presided over the lowest fertility rate in American history, the highest sterilization rates since the introduction of modern contraception, a collapse in marriage rates, and a generational gender divide that is reshaping the electorate.

Whatever they thought Dobbs was going to accomplish — it has accomplished the opposite. You cannot simultaneously make pregnancy more dangerous, more expensive, more surveilled, and more legally precarious, while cutting the safety net that supports parents, dismantling the childcare infrastructure, and criminalizing the medical decisions of pregnant women — and expect women to have more children. You cannot propose $8 billion[^97] in reproductive health funding cuts, threaten to eliminate Medicaid coverage for 9 million women[^98] through block grants, and defund the family planning programs that prevent the unintended pregnancies you claim to oppose — and call yourself pro-family. You cannot slash the teen pregnancy prevention programs that serve 120,000 adolescents[^99], let the ACA premium subsidies expire so that maternity coverage becomes unaffordable, and propose excluding Planned Parenthood from Medicaid for the 2.8 million patients[^100] who have no other provider — and pretend the resulting babies were the plan.

The women have answered. The answer is no.
