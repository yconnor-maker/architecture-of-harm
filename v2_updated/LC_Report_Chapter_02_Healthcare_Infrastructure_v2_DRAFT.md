# Chapter 2: Healthcare Infrastructure — The Hospitals, the Workforce, the Clinics

*What is left to provide the care.*

---

A patient delivers in the wrong place. Not a planned home birth, not a birth center—she delivers somewhere that is not the labor and delivery unit because the system around her is breaking down in real time. The unit cannot admit her properly. The documentation system has no field for what just happened. A nurse cannot log into the computer because someone swapped her phone without updating her credentials. Another nurse prints lab labels on a printer that produces blank stickers—no ink—and walks the floor looking for one that works. They are seriously lacking in people. There is no provider on the unit until the assigned physician arrives. This is the second time recently that a patient has been walked up from the emergency department without proper triage or transfer, because the system does not have the staff or process to receive her correctly. 

The team manages. They always manage. They manage the way understaffed clinical teams always manage—with ad hoc workarounds, texted favors, and improvised solutions. Every day I am surprised more accidents don't happen, because the healthcare system is kept running through sheer force of will and the ingenuity of charge nurses doing unpaid, unrecognized work to hold the patchwork together.

The absurdity of this environment is best captured by a single image: a junior resident, a grown physician with two decades of education and training, sitting at a counter-height desk in a well-resourced hospital. The chair he was given was broken; it couldn't rise to the height of the desk. He looked like a toddler trying to use an office desk to write his notes. That is modern medicine. We waste immense amounts of money on inefficiency, but no one takes a second to pause and say, *this isn't working.* Nobody wants to take the time to reorganize, so we just keep stacking patches on top of patches. Before you know it, the mess you've created is ten times worse than if you had just paused and fixed the root problem.

You see the strain in the operating room, too. A cesarean section on a woman with extensive adhesions—thick bands of scar tissue—from a prior surgery. The procedure is complicated. It requires blunt dissection through the scar tissue, electrocautery to control bleeding, and an O'Leary stitch (ligation of the uterine artery) to manage hemorrhage from uterine atony. The fascia is thickened. Space is insufficient. Additional adhesiolysis (breaking down more scar tissue) is required just to reach the uterus. The baby is delivered. The placenta is adherent. The uterus will not contract. 

And through all of it, the anesthesia is inadequate. The patient feels the surgery. Not discomfort—actual pain. The spinal does not work well enough. The anesthesia team provides additional medication that offers "a moderate amount" of improvement. Local anesthetic is injected at the end. My primary postoperative concern isn't infection or surgical complications. It's pain control—because the infrastructure has failed to provide the most basic prerequisite for major abdominal surgery: the patient not feeling it.

These are not catastrophes. No one died. These are simply the baseline conditions of American obstetric care in a system built on workarounds.

## Before

More than 330 labor and delivery units have closed in the United States since 2011.[^1] The closures are concentrated in rural areas and low-income communities. Over one-third of all U.S. counties — 1,104 counties — are now maternity care deserts[^2]: counties with no hospital offering obstetric services, no birth center, and no OB-GYN. Women in these counties who go into labor drive two to four hours to deliver. Women in active labor, in a car, on a highway, for hours. Some of them do not make it. 2.3 million women of childbearing age and 150,000 babies born every year[^3] have essentially no local maternity care. Since 2010, over 500 hospitals have closed their OB units; 5.5 million women now live in counties with no or limited maternity care.[^4]

Maternity units close because they are the most expensive service line in a hospital: 24/7 coverage, specialized staff, high liability insurance costs, and equipment that sits idle between deliveries. Rural hospitals operating on thin margins cut the service that costs the most to maintain. The math is straightforward. The consequence is that an entire community loses not just delivery services but prenatal care, contraceptive counseling, STI screening, and cancer screening — because these services were typically co-located with the maternity unit. When the unit closes, the providers leave. Everything goes.

The pace of closures has been accelerating: 18 in 2020, 34 in 2023, 21 in 2024, and 27 in 2025 year-to-date — 116 or more rural labor and delivery unit closures since 2020 alone.[^5] The scale of surviving infrastructure is equally alarming: of the nation's approximately 2,396 rural hospitals, only 950 — representing 41% — still offer labor and delivery services.[^6] In twelve states, fewer than one-third of rural hospitals continue to provide maternity care. An additional 127 rural hospitals are currently designated "at risk"[^7] due to negative total margins and sustained losses on patient services over the past two fiscal years. Rural facilities are uniquely vulnerable because they lack the alternative revenue streams available to large urban health systems, making 24/7 obstetric coverage financially untenable. Three forces are converging simultaneously on these hospitals: declining birth volumes, acute workforce shortages, and persistent financial insolvency — and there is no federal intervention on the horizon to address any of the three.

The geographic consequence for pregnant patients is direct and measurable. Approximately 70% of pregnant individuals in rural areas must now travel more than 30 minutes to reach a delivery facility[^8]; 20% must travel more than 50 minutes. These travel times are not inconveniences. In active labor, with a ruptured placenta, with a cord prolapse, with eclamptic seizures, transport time is the difference between life and death. Beyond the delivery crisis, these closures have produced a secondary catastrophe: the "deskilling" of rural emergency departments. When a hospital closes its maternity unit, the specialized staff leave. Over time, even the local emergency department loses the obstetric competency to stabilize a maternal emergency — meaning that when a crisis occurs, even the closest emergency room may no longer have the staff or equipment to keep a woman alive long enough to reach a facility that can treat her. The infrastructure collapse transforms manageable clinical emergencies into fatal ones.

The OB-GYN workforce shortage was already estimated at 8,000 physicians before the current crisis, projected to reach 22,000 by 2050.[^9] Forty percent of practicing OB-GYNs are over 55 years old. Only 19% are under 40. Approximately 15,000 baby boomer obstetricians are approaching retirement. Training a replacement takes 12 years: four years of medical school, four years of residency, and the early-career years before full independent practice. There is no way to accelerate this. There is no shortcut. As early as 2004, active OB-GYN numbers were declining or failing to match population growth. By 2024, projections warned of a 5,000-physician deficit by 2030. The retirement wave was coming regardless of politics. Politics turned a slow-moving workforce crisis into an acute emergency. The nursing workforce is similarly strained: more than 193,000 registered nurse openings are projected annually[^10] through 2032.

Title X served 2.8 million patients at $102 per patient per year[^11] — the most cost-efficient healthcare delivery in the federal government. Ten states still had not expanded Medicaid as of 2025: Alabama, Florida, Georgia, Kansas, Mississippi, South Carolina, Tennessee, Texas, Wisconsin, and Wyoming. This left 1.4 to 1.9 million adults in a coverage gap — earning too much for Medicaid, too little for ACA subsidies. The Hyde Amendment had banned federal Medicaid funds from covering abortion since 1976.

## The Rural Geography of Death

The maternity care desert is not a metaphor. It is a map of who lives and who dies based on where they were born.

According to CDC National Center for Health Statistics data, rural counties have a maternal mortality rate of 25.9 per 100,000 live births — 42% higher than the 18.2 rate in large metropolitan counties.[^12] The excess deaths are not mysterious. Hemorrhage and hypertensive emergencies account for approximately 60% of them[^13], and both are conditions where minutes determine outcomes. Rural women are 65% more likely to die from postpartum hemorrhage[^14] than their urban counterparts, driven by blood product access limitations — rural hospitals have limited blood bank capacity, smaller transfusion volumes that shorten shelf life, and no dedicated hemorrhage response teams — and by delayed recognition at low-volume facilities where staff see these emergencies too infrequently to respond with automaticity.

The workforce maldistribution is staggering. Only 4.1% of all OB-GYNs practice in rural areas, despite 10.2% of childbearing-age women living there.[^15] Sixty-four percent of rural counties have no practicing OB-GYN at all. According to a KFF analysis of HRSA data, provider density in rural areas is one-third that of metropolitan counties — 13 OB-GYNs per 100,000 women compared to 41.[^16] Over 10 million women live in counties with no OB-GYN whatsoever.[^17] The HRSA Health Workforce Simulation Model projects rural workforce adequacy declining from 57.1% in 2025 to 51.4% by 2037[^18], while metropolitan adequacy slides from 97.9% to 85.1%. The gap is widening, not closing.

Fifty-four percent of rural hospitals with obstetric units deliver fewer than 100 births per year[^19] — below established quality-safety thresholds. Twenty-nine percent deliver fewer than 50. Low-volume units are associated with lower provider skill maintenance, higher cesarean rates, and worse outcomes when emergencies occur. This creates a lethal paradox: the rural hospitals that remain open are often too small to safely deliver babies, but closing them means no delivery facility at all for a hundred miles.

The measurable health consequences cascade beyond the delivery room. Research published in JAMA Network Open found that rural obstetric unit closure is associated with a 9% increase in preterm births and a 7% increase in low birthweight[^20] in the surrounding county within two years — effects that persist for three years or more. Rural women are 2.3 times more likely to have an unplanned out-of-hospital birth[^21] than urban women. Unplanned out-of-hospital births carry four times the neonatal mortality of planned facility births. Rural infants have a 27% higher overall mortality rate — 6.45 per 1,000 live births compared to 5.06 in large metropolitan counties[^22] — and the gap has been widening since 2010. Only 16.9% of rural birth hospitals offer higher-level neonatal care[^23], compared to 74.4% of urban facilities. Fifty-five percent of rural counties have no NICU at any level. Rural premature infants face 25% longer transport times to reach intensive care.

The prenatal care pipeline is eroding in parallel. Rural women are 40% less likely to initiate prenatal care in the first trimester[^24] compared to urban women, with the largest disparities concentrated in the rural South. Among rural Black women, first-trimester initiation drops to 52% — compared to 71% for rural white women. Among rural American Indian and Alaska Native women, it is 49%. Nationally, first-trimester prenatal care has declined from 78.3% in 2021 to 75.5% in 2024[^25], while late or no prenatal care has risen from 6.3% to 7.3%. In maternity care deserts specifically, the inadequate prenatal care rate reaches 15.8%, and the rate of pre-pregnancy hypertension is 1.3 times higher than in full-access counties — meaning the communities with the least care also carry the highest risk.

## The States Where Infrastructure Has Collapsed

The crisis is national, but its most severe expression is regional — concentrated in the South, the Plains, and Appalachia, where rural poverty, Medicaid non-expansion, and now abortion bans converge.

The March of Dimes identifies the states with the highest percentage of counties classified as maternity care deserts: North Dakota at 73.6%, South Dakota at 57.6%[^26], Oklahoma at 51.9%, Missouri at 51.6%, Nebraska at 51.3%, and Arkansas at 50.7%. In these states, half or more of all counties have no birthing facility and no obstetric clinician.

Alabama has undergone a four-decade collapse. In 1980, 45 of Alabama's 55 rural counties had obstetric services. By 2024, only 15 to 16 remain[^27] — a 64 to 67% decline. Lawrence Medical Center in Moulton ended emergency and inpatient services in February 2025. Thomasville Regional halted obstetric care in 2024. Twenty-eight additional rural hospitals remain at risk. The destruction is concentrated in the Black Belt counties, where the population is predominantly Black and the infrastructure was already threadbare.

Mississippi has a maternal mortality rate of 39.1 per 100,000 live births, with Black women dying at 4.5 times the rate of white women.[^28] Nearly a quarter of Mississippi women live more than 30 minutes from an obstetric hospital. According to the Chartis Center for Rural Health, 47% of Mississippi's rural hospitals are vulnerable to closure.[^29] Georgia's pregnancy-related mortality rate reaches 37.9 per 100,000, with 87% of those deaths deemed preventable[^30] — cardiovascular conditions, mental health crises, hemorrhage, embolism — all conditions treatable with timely access to care that does not exist.

Texas has more rural hospitals at highest closure risk than any state in the nation — 47 facilities. CapRock Hospital in Bryan closed in December 2024.[^31] Texas is a non-expansion state with the largest uninsured population in the country, and Hispanic and Black Texans are disproportionately served by the at-risk facilities. In Missouri, maternal mortality in maternity care desert counties is 36% higher[^32] than in counties with full obstetric access.

The Chartis 2025 rural hospital vulnerability index identifies 432 rural hospitals at risk of closure[^33] nationwide. Forty-six percent of all rural hospitals operate with negative margins.[^34] In non-expansion states, the figure rises to 53%.[^35] The states with the highest proportion of at-risk rural hospitals — Arkansas at 50%, Mississippi at 47%, Kansas at 47% — overlap almost perfectly with the states that have refused Medicaid expansion and, in most cases, banned abortion.

This is not coincidence. An analysis found that states with the combined burden of rural provider shortage, abortion restriction, and Medicaid non-expansion have 3.4 times higher preventable maternal mortality than states with none of these conditions.[^36] The effects are multiplicative. Each condition independently increases mortality; in combination, they create near-complete reproductive healthcare deserts where ectopic emergency care delays, untreated hypertension, and absent prenatal care drive preventable death. Rural Black and American Indian women bear the disproportionate burden — and the states with the highest minority populations are overrepresented in this triple-burden category.

## The Emergency Surgery Gap

Thirty-two percent of rural counties lack a hospital with 24/7 emergency cesarean section capability[^37] within 30 minutes of travel. This statistic is not about scheduling inconvenience. It is a clinical reality codified in obstetric guidelines: emergency cesarean section requires a decision-to-incision time of 30 minutes or less. When a placental abruption occurs, when a cord prolapse is diagnosed, when fetal heart tracings show acute distress — the clock starts. If there is no anesthesiologist in the building because the hospital cut staffing, if the nearest surgical team is 45 minutes away by ambulance, if the rural emergency department closed its obstetric unit and the remaining staff have not managed a crash cesarean in two years — women and babies die in that interval.

The CMS Rural Emergency Hospital designation, launched in January 2023, was designed to stabilize struggling rural hospitals by allowing them to convert to emergency-and-outpatient-only facilities with enhanced reimbursement — $285,626 per month in facility payments and 105% of outpatient payment rates. By early 2026, 42 to 44 hospitals across 18 to 20 states had converted.[^38] But REH designation, by definition, eliminates inpatient services. It eliminates maternity care. Every REH conversion is another community where a pregnant woman in crisis has no local surgical option.

## Trump 1.0

The Gag Rule stripped approximately 1,000 clinics from Title X.[^39] Each closed clinic was a node in the safety net. When the node went dark, the women it served had nowhere to go — and the services they lost were not limited to contraception. Co-located prenatal care, cancer screening, and STI testing were gone with the clinic. From 2010 to 2018, Title X had already been cut by $31 million, resulting in 1.2 million fewer patients served. The Gag Rule finished the job. In rural areas, the damage was especially concentrated: 28% of rural Title X family planning sites closed or reduced services between 2019 and 2021, leaving 1.6 million rural patients without a primary family planning provider.[^40]

## The Pandemic

Eighteen rural maternity units closed in 2020. The healthcare workforce hit a breaking point — burnout, moral injury, PTSD. Providers left the profession. The U.S. lost more than 100,000 registered nurses between 2020 and 2021[^41] — the largest single-year decline on record. L&D units were particularly devastated because obstetric nursing requires specialized training that cannot be quickly backfilled. By 2022, 53% of OB-GYNs reported burnout[^42], a 13-percentage-point increase from pre-pandemic levels. Among OB-GYN residents, a national survey found 64.8% reporting burnout, 57.2% depression, and 70.9% anxiety. Rural hospitals, already operating on razor-thin margins, lost the financial reserves that might have sustained them through the next crisis. Nothing was replaced. No surge capacity. No rebuilding.

## What Began to Recover

Biden restored Title X. Some clinics reopened. The overall pace of closures slowed temporarily. The policy infrastructure stabilized — but the structural forces driving closures (workforce aging, rural hospital financial fragility, malpractice costs) were not resolved.

## Dobbs

Idaho lost 114 OB-GYNs[^43] — 43% of its workforce. Maternal-fetal medicine specialists went from 9 to fewer than 5. Complete labor and delivery unit closures followed. Idaho's Attorney General attempted to legally block doctors from even referring patients to out-of-state clinics.

Residency applications for OB-GYN programs dropped 10.5% across ban states[^44] overall. Alabama saw a 21.2% plummet. Missouri: a 25% drop. Texas SB 8 triggered a 16% decline in applications to Texas programs alone, with the majority of OB-GYN residents considering leaving the state entirely. A Commonwealth Fund analysis found a 6.7% overall decrease in residency applicants to ban states[^45] — trainees actively avoiding states where they cannot receive comprehensive clinical training in miscarriage management, ectopic pregnancy treatment, and emergency obstetric intervention. Sixty percent of physicians in total ban states considered leaving — 11% actually did.[^46] Forty-two percent of abortion providers have relocated out of ban states. Practices that once had three or four doctors collapsed to one or zero. Retired physicians are being pulled out of retirement to staff hospitals that would otherwise close their maternity units entirely.

Sixty-one percent of OB-GYNs practicing in ban states report concerns about their legal risk.[^47] The Health Resources and Services Administration projects the U.S. will meet only 82% of anticipated OB-GYN demand by 2037 — and that projection was made before accounting for the Dobbs exodus. Malpractice premiums increased 30-40% in ban states.[^48] Twenty-eight percent of women of reproductive age with young children report difficulty accessing maternity care nationally; in states with abortion bans, the figure is 34%.

Closures accelerated: 34 in 2023, 21 in 2024, 27 in 2025 year-to-date — 116+ rural labor and delivery unit closures since 2020 alone. The following 2025 closures are documented and named:

These are not abstractions. They are specific hospitals in specific communities where a woman can no longer deliver a baby without driving — in some cases for hours.

Physicians in ban states now write medical charts as legal defense briefs — documenting in statutory language why each intervention is required to prevent "substantial and irreversible impairment." If a patient presents bleeding after a self-managed abortion, clinical strategy dictates treating the symptoms as a spontaneous miscarriage, minimizing documented inquiry into the cause to prevent triggering mandatory reporting laws. This is not medicine. This is survival. The legal ambiguity — what counts as a "life-threatening" exception, when exactly does a physician have legal clearance to intervene — is not an abstract policy question. It is a real-time clinical calculation that physicians are making while a patient is hemorrhaging or septic on a gurney. In that ambiguity, women die.

## The Travel Burden

Before Dobbs, the average driving distance to the nearest abortion facility was 46.8 miles. After Dobbs, in ban states, it rose to 279.7 miles — a six-fold increase.[^49] In 2024, 155,000 patients traveled across state lines[^50] for abortion care, nearly double the 81,000 pre-Dobbs baseline. Texas alone generated more than 28,000 interstate travelers. Illinois served as the top destination, absorbing 35,470 out-of-state patients from the South and Midwest.

The rural dimensions are worse. Post-Dobbs, rural women in ban states travel a median of 75 or more miles for abortion care.[^51] One in five rural women lives more than 200 miles from the nearest provider. Rural Black women in ban states report inability to access abortion at nearly twice the rate of rural white women — 34% compared to 18%. For rural Latinas, the figure is 29%. These are women without public transportation, without paid leave, without childcare options — women for whom a 200-mile journey is not a road trip but an impossibility. Three-quarters of abortion patients are at or below 200% of the federal poverty level. The distance is the ban.

Meanwhile, a parallel geography of misinformation has emerged. Rural areas have 2.3 times more crisis pregnancy centers per reproductive-age woman[^52] than actual abortion providers. In state after state, CPCs cluster near the sites of closed abortion clinics — offering medically inaccurate information to women who have no alternative provider within reach. Thirty states fund these centers through "Alternatives to Abortion" programs.

## The Midwifery Bottleneck

The midwifery model works. It produces equivalent or better outcomes for low-risk pregnancies at lower cost. Rural Federally Qualified Health Centers with integrated certified nurse-midwives show 40% higher prenatal care initiation rates and 28% better birth outcomes[^53] than rural counties without CNM-integrated care. Midwives are the most scalable answer to the OB-GYN shortage in rural America.

But 23 states have policies restricting midwives from practicing to the full extent of their training — scope-of-practice restrictions requiring physician supervision or collaborative agreements that are impossible to establish in counties where there is no physician to collaborate with. The restriction creates a circular trap: the midwife cannot practice without a supervising physician, but the supervising physician has already left or retired, so the community gets no provider at all.

Progress is slow but measurable. Twenty-six states and the District of Columbia now allow independent CNM practice[^54], up from 24 in 2023, with Wisconsin, Illinois, Florida, and North Carolina expanding scope between 2023 and 2025. But every year of delay in the remaining 23 states is another year in which rural communities that could have midwifery care go without any care.

Home births have increased from 29,592 in 2016 to 46,918 in 2023.[^55] Black women's home births tripled: from 823 to 2,733. This is not a lifestyle choice. It is a response to a failing hospital system — hospitals closing, providers leaving, and a system where Black women are three and a half times more likely to die. Expansion of midwifery is undermined by scope-of-practice restrictions, insurance barriers, and Medicaid reimbursement at a punitive 50-85% of hospital rates.

## The Medicaid Architecture

Medicaid finances 47% of all births in rural areas[^56] — nearly half. In maternity care deserts, Medicaid covers close to 50% of all births compared to 40.1% in full-access counties. This means that any federal Medicaid spending cut hits rural maternity care first and hardest.

The data on Medicaid expansion and hospital survival is unambiguous. Approximately 69% of rural hospital closures between 2014 and 2024 occurred in states that had not expanded Medicaid[^57] at the time of closure. An analysis from the Chartis Center for Rural Health found that critical access hospitals in non-expansion states operate on margins 40% thinner[^58] than their peers in expansion states. ACA Medicaid expansion reduced rural uninsured rates by 41% in expansion states compared to just 15% in non-expansion states, with rural reproductive-age women experiencing the greatest coverage gain.

Federally Qualified Health Centers have become the critical safety net. FQHCs served 4.7 million rural patients for reproductive healthcare in 2022.[^59] Thirty percent of rural Americans depend on FQHCs as their primary reproductive health access point. FQHCs provided prenatal care to 566,000 patients and care for one in six U.S. births. They accept Medicaid at roughly 90%, compared to 77% for private OB-GYN practices. They are the last line.

During the 2023 Medicaid unwinding, rural states had 22% higher disenrollment rates[^60] than urban areas, with reproductive-age women accounting for 31% of all disenrollments — lost to paperwork failures at understaffed rural Medicaid offices, mail renewal failures at rural addresses, and a digital divide that limited online renewal. Four-point-six million children lost Medicaid coverage, 70% due to paperwork alone.[^61]

## Trump 2.0

Title X frozen — $65.8 million, as of March 31, 2025.[^62] The Office of Population Affairs went from approximately 50 staff to one officer; 30 received RIF notices on October 10, 2025. 879 clinics across 23 states have been affected. 834,000 women are projected to lose access.[^63] Planned Parenthood defunded from Medicaid in 39 states, effective July 2025 — $45 million per month in unreimbursed care. Twenty-three Planned Parenthood clinics have closed since July 2025; 32 more closures announced across 11 states. 16 of 86 Title X family planning grantees have had funding frozen. In many of these communities, there is no replacement program and no other provider.

HHS reduced from 82,000 to 62,000 employees. Over 4,800 federal health workers fired.[^64] The Indian Health Service targeted for $900 million in cuts; 12 IHS facilities closed since March 2025 — removing healthcare infrastructure for Native American communities where maternal mortality is 4.9 times the white rate.[^65] American Indian and Alaska Native women experience rural maternal mortality rates 3.2 times higher than rural white women and twice the rate of urban AI/AN women — driven by IHS chronic underfunding, geographic isolation on reservations, and historical trauma. In Alaska, the median distance to obstetric care is 2.5 hours. The only federal healthcare system serving these communities is being dismantled.

On July 4, 2025, the One Big Beautiful Bill Act was signed into law, projecting $900 billion in Medicaid and CHIP cuts over ten years.[^66] The CBO projects 10.5 million coverage losses. New caps on state-directed payments will limit the supplemental funding that has kept many rural hospitals solvent. Medicaid work requirements of 80 hours per month take effect January 1, 2027 — imposing administrative barriers on the same rural, low-income populations that already face the most severe healthcare access gaps. For the 432 rural hospitals already vulnerable to closure, for the 46% of rural hospitals already operating with negative margins, this legislation does not merely fail to solve the crisis. It accelerates it.

Meanwhile, enhanced ACA premium tax credits expired on December 31, 2025. An estimated 3.8 million people are projected to become newly uninsured in 2026. Twenty-two million marketplace enrollees face an average 114% premium increase.[^67] The national uninsured rate, currently 8%, will climb. The coverage losses compound every structural failure documented in this chapter.

Private equity consolidation of hospital systems produces its own casualties. When PE firms acquire and consolidate hospitals, they reduce staffing. In obstetric emergencies — emergency cesarean sections — the decision-to-incision time is a critical safety metric. When there is no anesthesiologist in the building because the hospital cut staffing to improve margins, that time increases. Women and babies die in that interval.

## The Parallel Collapse: Behavioral Health Workforce

The healthcare infrastructure crisis extends beyond obstetric providers. The behavioral health workforce — the psychiatrists, psychologists, addiction counselors, and social workers who treat the conditions that are now the leading medical killers of pregnant women — is collapsing in parallel.

There are fewer than 500 reproductive psychiatrists in the United States[^68] for approximately 3.6 million births per year. Perinatal mood and anxiety disorders affect 1 in 5 women, yet 75% receive no treatment. Maternal suicide is now the leading cause of death in the first year postpartum. Ninety-six percent of birthing-aged women live in maternal mental health professional shortage areas. Fifty-eight percent of rural counties have no licensed perinatal mental health specialist whatsoever, and rural women are 42% more likely to experience untreated perinatal depression than their urban counterparts. By 2025, approximately 150 "Dark Zone" counties have been identified[^69] — counties with both high maternal mental health risk and severe provider shortage. The number of severe-risk counties has tripled, from 24 to 92. Ninety-three percent of behavioral health professionals reported burnout in a 2023 workforce survey, with 33% annual clinician turnover during the pandemic period.

The addiction treatment workforce faces a projected shortfall of 77,050 counselors by 2038.[^70] Overdose is the leading medical cause of maternal death. Rural opioid-involved maternal mortality is 43% higher than urban[^71], and rural pregnant women with opioid use disorder are 28% less likely to receive medication-assisted treatment — blocked by geographic barriers to addiction treatment, limited buprenorphine-waivered providers, transportation barriers to methadone clinics that require daily in-person dosing, and rural stigma that punishes help-seeking. SAMHSA grants — $2 billion in addiction treatment and mental health funding — were terminated. The programs that train and deploy the providers who treat pregnant women with substance use disorders are being defunded at the exact moment when fentanyl-xylazine is producing the worst overdose crisis in American history.

The cascading effect is compounding: when a rural hospital closes its maternity unit, the co-located behavioral health services disappear too. The woman who needs both prenatal care and addiction treatment now has nowhere to go for either. The OB-GYN shortage and the behavioral health shortage are not two separate crises. They are the same crisis — a systematic withdrawal of the workforce that keeps pregnant women alive — playing out across two interlinked clinical domains.
