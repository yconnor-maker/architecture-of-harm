# Chapter 1: Maternal Mortality — Who Is Dying and Why

*The defining measure of a nation's commitment to its women.*

---

A young mother, pregnant with her second child, comes in for a fever and flank pain. She has a history of recurrent infections that were never properly worked up—a failure of primary care spanning a decade. Her blood pressure is dangerously high, spiking to 170. She had been seen the week before for the same issue, but no medication was started and she was sent home. Now she has headaches and is seeing spots. The facility where she is sitting does not have a neonatal intensive care unit equipped to handle the extremely premature delivery that is about to become necessary. While the medical team starts magnesium sulfate to prevent seizures and scrambles to arrange a transfer to a hospital that can actually keep her two-pound baby alive, the real reason she hasn't been able to manage her health comes out.

Her partner had loaded a gun on her. He had jumped her while she was pregnant, then sent other people to jump her again days later. He had burned all of their ultrasound pictures. She lives alone with her older child. She has no family support, no safe place to go, and her rent is about to double. When I tell her that the number one cause of death for pregnant women is murder—more than hemorrhage, more than preeclampsia—she is not surprised. She already knows. The failing infrastructure isn't just the lack of a NICU. It's the absolute absence of a safety net.

The hospital's social work department cannot help her. "You just don't have great success with our social workers," I tell the medical team later. Instead, I text my own nonprofit contacts. I print out local domestic violence hotline numbers. I counsel the patient to hide her documents—passport, birth certificates, cash—at a friend's house. To set aside ten dollars a week. To tell someone. The clinical note becomes a safety plan. The prescription pad becomes a resource list. And then the next patient is waiting.

These are not outliers. This is a Monday morning.

---

## The Number That Reframes Everything

The leading cause of pregnancy-associated death in the United States is not hemorrhage. It is not hypertension. It is not infection. It is homicide — at a rate of 3.62 per 100,000 live births, exceeding every obstetric cause of maternal death combined by more than two times.[^1] Sixty-eight percent involve firearms.[^2] The killer is almost always a male intimate partner. Black women aged 18 to 24 die from pregnancy-associated homicide at 8 per 100,000 — four times the national average.[^3]

The leading *medical* cause — surpassing hemorrhage, preeclampsia, and sepsis since 2020 — is overdose. Overdose deaths among pregnant and postpartum women increased 81 percent between 2017 and 2020.[^4] Mental health conditions account for 23 percent of all pregnancy-related deaths.[^5] Together, violence, overdose, and mental health — none of which are treated by the obstetric interventions that dominate federal maternal health policy — kill more pregnant women than every clinical cause combined.

This is not a failure of obstetric medicine. It is a failure of the entire system surrounding the woman — the partner who kills her, the substance use treatment she cannot access, the mental health infrastructure that does not exist, the economic trap that prevents her from leaving, and the policies that have made every one of those failures worse.

## Before

The United States has the highest maternal mortality rate of any high-income country in the world. This has been true for decades. While peer nations were reducing maternal death to low single digits — Sweden at 4 per 100,000, Norway at 2, Japan at 3[^6] — the United States was going the wrong direction. Between 2000 and 2014, the U.S. maternal mortality ratio increased 26.6%, from 18.8 to 23.8 deaths per 100,000 live births.[^7] No other developed nation was experiencing increases. The United States was alone.

The United States spends more on healthcare than any country in the world — over $4.5 trillion per year, approximately $13,500 per person.[^8] No other high-income nation comes close. The average among OECD countries is roughly half that. And for this investment — the largest healthcare expenditure in human history — the United States produces the worst maternal mortality, the worst infant mortality, and the lowest life expectancy of any wealthy democracy. The Commonwealth Fund's international comparison, updated regularly, consistently ranks the United States last or near-last among high-income countries in healthcare access, equity, and outcomes. Americans die younger. American babies die more often. American mothers die at rates that other wealthy nations eliminated a generation ago. And Americans go bankrupt doing it.

The average total healthcare cost of pregnancy and childbirth in the United States is $18,865 — $26,200 for a cesarean section.[^9] Employees with employer-sponsored insurance still pay roughly $2,854 to $3,200 out of pocket. Without insurance, the range is $9,000 to $25,000. The United States is the most expensive country in the world in which to have a baby. It is also the most dangerous wealthy country in which to have a baby. Those two facts are not unrelated. They are the same failure expressed in different currencies.

The United States remains the only industrialized wealthy nation on earth without a guaranteed federal paid family leave policy. It is the only wealthy nation where women can lose their jobs for taking time to recover from childbirth. It is the only wealthy nation where the decision to have a child is, for millions of women, a calculation about whether they can afford to survive the experience.

Women hold 64% of all U.S. student loan debt[^10] — between $833 billion and $929 billion. Black women carry an average of $58,252 in educational debt versus $29,323 for white women.[^11] After twelve years of repayment, the average Black borrower still owes 112% of their original loan.[^12] This debt suppresses homeownership, delays family formation, and compounds every other economic barrier that pregnancy and childbirth impose.

By 2021, the national maternal mortality rate had reached 32.9 per 100,000[^13], driven upward by the pandemic. It came down to 22.3 in 2022, then 18.6 in 2023. Provisional 2024 data shows the national rate at approximately 17.9 — 649 women died.[^14] The CDC's own maternal mortality review committees have found that 84% to 93% of maternal deaths in the United States are preventable.[^15] Preventable does not mean "unfortunate." It means the system failed. The medicine existed. The protocols existed. Something in the chain between the woman and the intervention broke down — a missed diagnosis, a delayed referral, a closed unit, a missing provider, a transportation gap, an insurance lapse, a language barrier, a provider who did not listen.

**The Racial Chasm.** National averages conceal a stratification by race that is among the most severe health disparities documented in modern medicine. In 2023, Black non-Hispanic women died at 50.3 per 100,000 live births — 3.5 times the rate for white non-Hispanic women at 14.5.[^16] In 2024, provisional data shows Black women at 44.8 per 100,000 versus white women at 14.2 — a ratio of 3.2 to 1.[^17] Between 2021 and 2023, the Black-to-white maternal mortality ratio widened from 2.6x to 3.5x. The gap is not closing.

Native American and Alaska Native women have a maternal mortality rate of 118.7 per 100,000 — 4.9 times the white rate.[^18] The Indian Health Service, the only federal healthcare system serving these communities, was already chronically underfunded before 2025.

**The Racial Wealth Gap as Structural Foundation.** The racial disparity in maternal death does not exist in a vacuum. It sits on top of a wealth gap that has been functionally stagnant for three decades: for every $100 that white households hold, Black households hold $15.[^19] The mean racial wealth gap grew 38 percent between 2019 and 2022 alone — from $842,000 to $1.15 million.[^20] In 1992, the gap was 86 percent. In 2022, it was 85 percent.[^21] Thirty years of economic policy produced zero structural change. This is the economic architecture beneath the mortality disparity: women who cannot afford to leave violent partners, who cannot afford the $2,854 out-of-pocket cost of insured childbirth, who carry $58,252 in average educational debt (versus $29,323 for white women), who after twelve years of repayment still owe 112 percent of their original loans. The wealth gap does not explain the mortality gap by itself — Black women with household incomes above $100,000 still die at higher rates than white women below the poverty line — but it compounds every other barrier the system places between a woman and survival.

These disparities are not explained by individual behavior, insurance status, or prenatal care utilization in isolation. They are explained by structural racism — by a healthcare system that systematically undertreats Black women's pain, dismisses their symptoms, delays their diagnoses, and provides them lower-quality care at every stage of pregnancy and postpartum recovery. Black women are up to 50% more likely to experience severe maternal morbidity.[^22] They face higher rates of emergency cesarean sections, the highest rates of preterm birth, and are overwhelmingly more likely to receive delayed or absent prenatal care. 54% of Black women report feeling the effects of racial and gender bias[^23] in the healthcare system. One in five say a provider did not take their pain seriously because of their race. A failure of provider active-listening has been repeatedly cited as the core clinical trigger for delayed diagnosis of conditions like preeclampsia in Black and Brown patients.

In 2023, leading clinical voices formalized a mandate to abandon "race-based medicine" — which assumes biological racial differences drive outcomes — in favor of "race-conscious medicine," a framework that identifies racism and discriminatory care structures as the primary drivers of disparity. The disparities persist even when controlling for every variable except the system itself. The system is the variable.


**A Note on Data Reliability.** The National Center for Health Statistics stopped producing national maternal mortality rates for approximately a decade — citing concerns over the accuracy of the "pregnancy checkbox" on death certificates — only resuming publication in 2018. This history of data volatility is not a footnote. It is the context in which all current "stabilizations" must be read: some portion of apparent improvement may reflect shifting reporting methodology rather than improved clinical outcomes. Provisional figures are routinely revised upward as late death records are submitted and as inclusion guidelines shift. The NCHS and leading public health researchers explicitly caution that recent apparent stabilization is statistically insignificant — a plateau masking deep structural rot rather than a meaningful clinical improvement. The aggregate figure of 649 maternal deaths in 2024 compared to 669 in 2023 represents a plateau in a system that remains catastrophically behind peer nations where maternal mortality ratios are often three to four times lower than those found in the United States.

**Age and the Mortality Gradient.** The age-stratified picture is largely absent from national discourse. For women aged 40 and older, the maternal mortality rate in 2024 stood at 62.3 per 100,000[^24] live births — nearly five times the rate for women younger than 25, which remained at 13.7. This age-related disparity intensified sharply during the pandemic, when the mortality rate for women 40 and older reached 106.3 per 100,000 at peak — a figure that would constitute a declared public health emergency in any peer nation. The mechanisms driving these age-related disparities include higher rates of underlying cardiovascular conditions, greater likelihood of multiple gestation, and the compounding effects of inadequate housing, environmental hazards, and decades of disparate access to quality prenatal care. These social drivers — not individual clinical choices — form the true foundation of the crisis beneath the clinical surface.

## Trump 1.0 (2016–2020)

The first Trump administration was the decisive instrument of a fifty-year conservative legal strategy — the Federalist Society, founded in 1982, building the pipeline of conservative legal minds at every level of the federal judiciary; the Heritage Foundation and allied organizations producing model legislation, TRAP laws, waiting periods, and trigger bans written and placed in state legislatures across the country for decades, staged and waiting. By the time the Supreme Court was ready, 13 states had trigger bans on the books[^25], written and waiting for the day Roe fell. This was not reactive. It was strategic.

Three Supreme Court vacancies in four years — Neil Gorsuch (2017), Brett Kavanaugh (2018), Amy Coney Barrett (2020) — gave the conservative movement the supermajority it had been building toward for fifty years. Barrett's confirmation, eight days before the 2020 election, replacing Ruth Bader Ginsburg, was the final piece.

The Domestic Gag Rule gutted Title X, forcing approximately 1,000 clinics out of the program[^26] and eliminating not just family planning but co-located prenatal care, cancer screening, and STI testing — the upstream services that prevent the downstream emergencies that kill women. The fetal tissue research ban in 2019 disrupted clinical trials studying preeclampsia and placental development. The judicial fuse was lit.

## The Pandemic (2020–2022)

Maternal mortality surged to 32.9 per 100,000 in 2021[^27] — the highest recorded rate in modern American history. Black maternal mortality reached 69.9 per 100,000 in 2021 — a number that would constitute a public health emergency in any peer nation. Maternal deaths surged 46% in states that had refused to expand Medicaid versus 21% in expansion states.[^28] Unvaccinated pregnant women experienced catastrophic outcomes: higher rates of ICU admission, ventilation, and death from COVID-19 than any other demographic group.

The pandemic exposed what clinicians already knew: the American obstetric system operates with no surge capacity. There are no backup labor and delivery units. There are no reserve OB-GYNs. When a hospital diverts obstetric nurses to COVID wards — which happened in hospital after hospital — there is nobody to replace them. Eighteen rural maternity units closed in 2020 alone.[^29]

Prenatal care — the single most effective intervention for preventing maternal and infant death — collapsed. Women missed appointments because clinics closed, because they were afraid of COVID exposure, because they could not arrange childcare, because they lost their jobs and their insurance in the same week. The shift to telehealth worked for women with smartphones, reliable internet, and private spaces to talk. It did not work for women without any of those things. Every missed prenatal visit was a missed screening: undetected preeclampsia progresses to seizures, stroke, and death; undetected gestational diabetes produces macrosomic babies, birth injuries, and neonatal ICU admissions; undetected syphilis during pregnancy produces congenital syphilis in the infant — blindness, bone deformity, organ damage, death.

## What Began to Recover

Under the Biden administration, 48 states extended postpartum Medicaid coverage from 60 days to 12 months — a critical policy change adopted even in conservative states under pressure from their own mortality data. HIPAA reproductive privacy protections were updated. EMTALA guidance was clarified to require emergency abortion care. Title X funding was restored. Fetal tissue research restrictions were reversed.

Overall maternal mortality came down: 32.9 (2021) → 22.3 (2022) → 18.6 (2023). Black maternal mortality: 69.9 → 49.5 → 50.3. Still catastrophic. Still 3.5 times the white rate. But the trajectory was bending — there was evidence that the system could improve if the infrastructure was supported.

## Dobbs (June 2022 Forward)

Trump's judicial appointments made Dobbs possible. Dobbs made everything worse.

When the Dobbs decision came on June 24, 2022, it did not merely reverse Roe v. Wade. It eliminated the constitutional right to abortion that had been the legal floor beneath reproductive healthcare for 49 years. Within weeks, abortion was banned or severely restricted in 21 states. Trigger bans activated. Providers fled. Hospitals closed labor and delivery units. Malpractice premiums spiked. Medical students stopped applying to residencies in states where they could not be trained in the full scope of obstetric care.

States with abortion bans now show maternal death rates 62% higher than states preserving access[^30] — 28.8 versus 17.8 per 100,000 births. Bans in 14 states have produced an estimated 22,180 additional live births and 478 additional infant deaths above what would have been expected without the bans. Black infants died at 11% higher than expected — 265 additional Black infant deaths. A total national ban would produce an estimated 140 additional maternal deaths annually: a 21% overall increase and a 33% increase for Black women.

**Texas:** A 56% increase in maternal mortality between 2019 and 2022[^31] — five times the 11% national increase over the same period. Texas maternal mortality is now 155% higher than California's. The racial breakdown destroys the assumption that this is only a racial disparity story: white women saw a 95% increase, Black women 38%, Hispanic women 30%. The single largest increase was among white women — meaning the policy itself, not the pre-existing racial health gap, is driving the deaths. The Texas six-week ban was associated with a 12.9% increase in infant and neonatal mortality. Infant deaths with congenital anomalies spiked 12.7% above expected baselines in 2022. Sepsis rates for women treated for second-trimester miscarriages jumped 50% because physicians were too afraid of prosecution to provide timely care. In Texas, clinical documentation must now read as a legal defense brief. Providers must document precisely why an intervention is required to prevent "substantial and irreversible impairment," using explicit statutory language. The Texas Attorney General has filed suits to block federal HIPAA protections regarding out-of-state medical records, creating near-total surveillance over maternal charting.

The specific clinical mechanism driving the sepsis surge has been documented by ProPublica and reviewed by dozens of maternal health experts: the most pronounced increase occurred among patients admitted while the fetus still had a detectable heartbeat — cases in which the pregnancy was no longer viable or was actively endangering the mother's life, but in which the presence of cardiac activity created legal ambiguity that physicians chose to wait out rather than risk criminal prosecution. The delay was not clinical uncertainty. It was legal fear. Case counts: 67 sepsis cases related to second-trimester pregnancy loss in 2021, rising to 99 in 2023, even as national maternal mortality rates were declining over the same period. Pre-pandemic baseline maternal hospital deaths in Texas were approximately 79; by the post-ban period of 2022-2023, that number rose to 120 — a 51.9% increase. Regional variation within Texas further documents the role of institutional protocol: Houston-area hospitals, which adopted more restrictive interpretations of the state law, saw a 63% increase in sepsis rates; Dallas-area hospitals, which modified their protocols after witnessing adverse outcomes, saw a more moderate but still alarming 29% increase. The Texas state government has declined to review maternal deaths from 2022 and 2023 — a decision that maternal health researchers characterize as a systematic effort to sever the documented causal link between legislative action and maternal fatality. When the data would prove the policy is lethal, do not produce the data.

**Idaho:** Maternal mortality surged 121.5% between 2019 and 2021[^32] leading into the ban. Between 22% and 43% of all practicing OB-GYNs left the state. Idaho's maternal-fetal medicine specialists — the physicians who manage the highest-risk pregnancies — went from 9 to fewer than 5. Complete labor and delivery unit closures followed. The Attorney General attempted to legally block doctors from even referring patients to out-of-state clinics.

**Georgia:** Rate: 48.4 per 100,000.[^33] **Florida:** The six-week ban, effective May 2024, produced an immediate 30-35% drop in total clinician-provided abortions and a catastrophic 80% decline in care from Planned Parenthood clinics — severing the entire Southeastern abortion access pipeline, since Florida had been the destination for women fleeing bans in Georgia, Alabama, Mississippi, and Louisiana.

We know the names: Amber Thurman in Georgia. Josseli Barnica. Nevaeh Crain. Candy Miller. Women who died because they could not access timely medical care in their states. In Texas, a woman experiencing a miscarriage waited 40 hours for treatment while bleeding and in pain as hospital staff consulted lawyers. In Missouri, doctors told a woman whose water broke at 18 weeks that "current Missouri law supersedes our medical judgment." In Idaho, the state's largest hospital system stopped delivering babies altogether.

**The Criminalization of Pregnancy.** In the two years following Dobbs, 412 criminal charges were filed against individuals for pregnancy outcomes[^34] — doubling from year one to year two. Alabama accounted for 192 charges. Oklahoma: 112. 75% of the defendants were low-income. 97% of charges included substance use allegations. 264 of those 412 cases originated from medical disclosure — doctors and nurses reported their own patients to law enforcement. A woman went to her doctor. The doctor reported her. She was charged with a crime. Women in border regions have reduced their prenatal visits by 40-60%. The women who most need prenatal care are avoiding it because they are afraid that seeing a doctor will result in criminal charges. Prenatal care is the single most effective intervention for preventing maternal and infant death. When women avoid it, they die.

**The Dobbs-Violence Connection.** The relationship between abortion access and violence is now quantified: a 7-10% increase in intimate partner violence in ban counties[^35], a 3.4% increase in the intimate partner homicide rate per additional restriction, and an estimated 9,000 to 14,000 additional domestic violence incidents nationally. When a woman cannot leave a pregnancy, she cannot leave the relationship. Chapter 9 documents this connection in full — from prevalence through mechanism to the geographic concentration that compounds every disparity for Black women in ban states.

**The Comstock Threat.** The Comstock Act — an 1873 law that prohibits sending "obscene" materials through the mail — is being positioned as a mechanism to ban the mailing of mifepristone and other reproductive healthcare materials without any new legislation, without any vote of Congress. Project 2025 explicitly directs the Department of Justice to weaponize this statute. Nearly 1 in 4 U.S. abortions were telehealth-delivered by late 2024[^36], with over 40,000 provided directly into ban states under the protection of shield laws enacted by 18 states. Comstock enforcement would collapse that entire infrastructure overnight.

## Trump 2.0 (January 2025 Forward)

The systematic dismantling of maternal health infrastructure accelerated rapidly in 2025 and 2026. The PRAMS team — the only people in the federal government tracking pregnancy outcomes across all 50 states for 38 years — was fired on April 1, 2025.[^37] PRAMS funding expires April 2026. Two of three branches of the CDC Division of Reproductive Health were eliminated. The Women's Health and Fertility Branch — gone. The Contraceptive Best Practices Team — gone. 

In March 2025, an executive restructuring directed by the Department of Government Efficiency (DOGE) mandated that the Health Resources and Services Administration (HRSA), SAMHSA, and several CDC programs be absorbed into a new American Health Administration (AHA) with a 10,000-person staff reduction.[^41] The Alliance for Innovation on Maternal Health (AIM) — a federal program that had successfully reached over 1,000 hospitals and reduced severe maternal morbidity by 15-20% through standardized care bundles — was slated for elimination in the FY2026 budget cuts.[^42]

The federal legislative response effectively ceased. The "Momnibus" package, intended to comprehensively address Black maternal mortality, failed for a third consecutive Congress and was not even reintroduced in the 119th Congress (2025-2026) under the Republican majority.[^43] During an April 2026 House Energy & Commerce Committee hearing, when Representative Summer Lee asked HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. how the department planned to address the Black maternal mortality crisis after instructing grant applicants to remove the word "Black" from applications, the Secretary did not deny the instruction, instead reframing the documentation of racial disparity itself as a divisive act.[^44] 

These infrastructure cuts collided with emerging clinical crises. Between 2022 and 2024, maternal syphilis rose by 28%, driving catastrophic outcomes in congenital syphilis. Simultaneously, the only safe treatment during pregnancy — Bicillin L-A — entered a severe shortage projected to last through late 2026, exacerbated by the dismantling of HHS drug supply chain monitoring.[^45]

Congress reauthorized the Preventing Maternal Deaths Act through 2030 with $113.5 million for Maternal Mortality Review Committees. So the committees that investigate maternal deaths are funded. But the surveillance system that feeds them data is being destroyed. They funded the ability to count how women die while defunding the ability to prevent those deaths. And in states like Idaho, the response to the data was even more cynical — Idaho actively tried to defund and disband its own MMRC to stop publishing data showing that its abortion ban is killing women. When the data proves your policy is lethal, destroy the data.

The NIMHD — the only federal institute specifically tasked with studying why Black women die at three to four times the white rate — had 77 grants terminated ($223.5 million), a 29.6% cut to its funding.[^38] It received 61% fewer competitive grants. The institute itself was targeted for elimination. The framing: "anti-DEI." The function: defunding the only research infrastructure specifically designed to understand the patients who are dying the most. Title X funding was frozen under the guise of reviewing grantee compliance with anti-DEI executive orders — weaponizing anti-diversity language to strip funding from the clinics that serve the women with the worst outcomes.

Even in California — the best-performing state, with a maternal mortality rate of 4.0 per 100,000[^39] and a 55% reduction achieved through the CMQCC — Black women still die at three to four times the white rate. Only 17% of California hospitals complied with a 2019 racism-in-medicine training law. Approximately 70 maternal deaths per year, roughly 60% preventable. The racial gap survives even the best systems.

In Louisiana — 58.1 per 100,000[^40], fourteen times California's rate — 59% of Black maternal deaths are preventable versus 9% of white deaths. And Louisiana's Governor Jeff Landry — dispatched by Trump as special envoy to Greenland — announced in February 2026 he was sending a Navy hospital ship to Greenland "to take care of the many people who are sick." Greenland, which has universal free healthcare, said no thank you. Both Navy hospital ships were in drydock in Alabama. Landry's own state — 73% of residents lack sufficient primary care, 86% lack dental providers, 93% lack mental health providers — has a maternal mortality rate fourteen times California's. He offered healthcare to a country that already has it while presiding over a maternal health catastrophe at home.

**Full 2024 Demographic Breakdown.** The national rate of 17.9 masks a more complex picture when disaggregated fully. The NCHS provisional 2024 data shows:

| Demographic Group | 2023 Rate | 2024 Rate | Percent Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| National Average (per 100k births) | 18.6 | 17.9 | -3.7% |
| Non-Hispanic Black Women | 49.5 | 44.8 | -9.5% |
| Non-Hispanic White Women | 14.9 | 14.2 | -4.7% |
| Hispanic Women | 12.1 | 12.1 | 0.0% |
| Non-Hispanic Asian Women | 14.5 | 18.1 | +24.8% |
| Women Aged 40 and Over | 61.2 | 62.3 | +1.8% |
| Total Maternal Deaths | 669 | 649 | -3.0% |

Three findings from this table demand attention. First, Hispanic women saw zero improvement — a flat line at 12.1 while the national rate declined. Second, Non-Hispanic Asian women saw a 24.8% increase — moving from near-white rates to significantly elevated rates in a single year. This increase is not explained by existing narratives about racial disparity and warrants immediate investigation; it does not appear in most public reporting on the 2024 data. Third, women aged 40 and over saw rates increase, not decrease, from 61.2 to 62.3. The reported national "decline" is carried almost entirely by improvements among Black and white women while other groups moved in the wrong direction.

Provisional 2024 data carries an additional caveat: the NCHS and leading researchers warn that late death records and shifts in inclusion guidelines routinely produce upward revisions of preliminary figures. 12-month rolling rates dropping to approximately 16.7 by September 2025 suggest a continued slight downward trend, but researchers caution these figures should be treated as estimates pending final reconciliation. The slight improvement in the aggregate gap masks the approaching data blackout: 2023 is the last year for which we will have reliable national data. The instrument that measured the damage was destroyed while the damage was accelerating.
