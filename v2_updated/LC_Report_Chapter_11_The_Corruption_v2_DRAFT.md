# Chapter 11: The Corruption — Where the Money Actually Went

*The money did not disappear. It was redirected.*

---

The physician knew what she was seeing.

A colleague was performing non-indicated cesarean sections. Not emergencies. Not cases where the clinical trajectory had deteriorated and the decision to operate was the only remaining option. Cases where the operation was chosen because it was faster, or because it billed better, or because no one was going to question it.

She did not report it. There was no mechanism that would make reporting effective and no protection for the person who tried. What she did was dictate her observations into a device she carried in her pocket — building a record of what medicine looked like from inside, for the day when someone might care.

"Some colleagues perform non-indicated C-sections," she said. She contrasted this with her own training, which had emphasized preventing primary cesareans — keeping the scalpel in the drawer when it wasn't needed, even when the schedule and the billing system both incentivized taking it out. "There is a disparity in care quality for marginalized groups who cannot hold institutions accountable."

That sentence is the thesis of this chapter. The corruption in American maternal health is not a scandal. It is a business model. The patients who cannot hold the system accountable — the patients on Medicaid, the patients without advocates, the patients who do not know they have alternatives — receive care calibrated to the system's convenience, not to their survival. The physician who tries to provide equitable care is penalized for it. The physician who does not is rewarded.

"I strive to provide the same quality of care to all patients, regardless of insurance status," she said. Then, with the sadness of someone who has watched from inside: "The system often does not support ethical and safe practice."

## Crisis Pregnancy Centers: Not Medical Facilities

Texas diverted $140 million from medical research to fund crisis pregnancy centers.[^1] Crisis pregnancy centers are not medical facilities. They do not have licensed providers. They have been documented providing inaccurate medical information to patients — including inaccurate information about abortion risk, fetal development, and treatment options. Dr. Connor has personally treated patients with ectopic pregnancies — a life-threatening emergency requiring immediate surgical or pharmaceutical intervention — whose treatment was delayed because they went to a crisis pregnancy center first, thinking it was a medical facility. The $140 million that went to these entities came from the same budget that should have funded the medical research infrastructure that is now gone.

The scale of this deception is now quantifiable. Approximately 2,700 CPCs operate nationally — three times the number of actual abortion clinics — receiving an estimated $700 million annually in combined federal, state, and private anti-abortion funding.[^2] The FTC issued warning letters to 15 CPCs for deceptive digital advertising[^3], purchasing Google ads under keywords like "abortion clinic" to intercept patients seeking actual medical care. CPC clients are 34% Black and 26% Hispanic.[^4] These are not counseling centers. They are delay mechanisms. In states with gestational bans, delay equals denial.

## The Consolidation of Power

Consider who is now running the American healthcare apparatus.

RFK Jr. — a man with no medical degree, no public health training, and a documented history of promoting anti-vaccine misinformation — is the Secretary of Health and Human Services, overseeing the health of 330 million Americans. He dismissed 17 standing members of the CDC's Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices. He oversaw the reduction of HHS from 82,000 to 62,000 employees.[^5] Under his authority, ACOG — the nation's leading obstetric professional organization — withdrew from the CDC's vaccine advisory process because it could no longer trust the scientific integrity of the committee.

Dr. Mehmet Oz — a television personality who built his career promoting unproven health products — is the CMS Administrator, overseeing Medicare and Medicaid. His first major action was deferring $259.5 million in Medicaid funding to Minnesota[^6] under a "fraud prevention" initiative that advocates warn could be weaponized to restrict reproductive healthcare reimbursements.

Jay Bhattacharya — a Stanford health economist who co-authored the Great Barrington Declaration opposing COVID lockdowns — has been appointed to oversee both the NIH and the CDC simultaneously (February 18, 2026).[^7] The world's two premier public health research institutions, under a single director, in the context of sweeping budget cuts and workforce reductions.

The "CRUSH" initiative — Comprehensive Regulations to Uncover Suspicious Healthcare — signals a major administrative overhaul of how Medicaid and Medicare claims are scrutinized, with potential to increase administrative burdens specifically on safety-net and reproductive care providers.

The pattern is consistent: public health infrastructure is being reconsolidated under political appointees whose qualifications are ideological alignment, not scientific expertise.

## The $1.7 Trillion Operation and the Rational Blockholder Problem

HHS controls approximately $1.7 trillion annually[^8] — the single largest discretionary and mandatory spending complex in the federal government, exceeding the defense budget when Medicaid, Medicare, NIH, CDC, FDA, HRSA, and SAMHSA are aggregated. That is the scale of what is being restructured. To put it in terms that financial analysts understand: this is not a policy disagreement. This is a hostile restructuring of a $1.7 trillion operation.

The corporate governance literature has a concept directly applicable here: the rational blockholder. A blockholder is an entity with a concentrated ownership stake large enough to influence management decisions — and uses that influence to maximize its own return, not the return of all stakeholders. Applied to healthcare: private equity's concentrated ownership of hospital systems, physician practices, and insurance intermediaries creates a structural incentive to extract value rather than generate health outcomes. The blockholder maximizes return by reducing the cost of delivering care — through workforce cuts, service-line elimination, billing optimization — regardless of patient outcomes. The rational blockholder does not care that rural obstetric units are unprofitable. It closes them. It does not care that congenital syphilis is preventable. It does not profit from prevention. It profits from treatment — specifically the commercially insured portion. Medicaid patients generate the lowest margins. They are the first to lose access.

The current administration's restructuring of HHS follows rational blockholder logic: eliminate oversight, reduce regulatory burden on high-margin operators, defund safety-net infrastructure, and consolidate decision-making under leadership aligned with the extractive model. What looks like ideology from the outside looks like portfolio optimization from the inside. The people making the decisions are not confused about the consequences. The consequences are the feature.

## AG Weaponization and the Preemption War

The state attorney general has become the single most consequential elected official for reproductive healthcare access — more determinative than any governor, more immediately dangerous than any legislator.

Anti-abortion PACs spent an estimated $43 million in 2024 state AG races[^9], according to the National Institute on Money in Politics, outpacing pro-access spending three to one. Dark money in AG races grew 340% from 2018 to 2024.[^10] The investment is rational: the AG controls enforcement of criminal penalties, determines which providers face prosecution, and decides whether to join multi-state litigation coalitions. Twelve AG races in 2024 were identified as abortion enforcement-determinative.

The weaponization is operational. At least five state AGs in ban states have asserted cross-border subpoena authority — demanding health records from providers in protection states when their residents travel for care. In 2026, Louisiana and Idaho AGs attempted interstate subpoenas on telemedicine providers in Illinois and Massachusetts.[^11] Shield-law states refused. No Supreme Court ruling exists on interstate subpoena authority for reproductive health records, and with the HIPAA reproductive privacy rule vacated, there is no federal floor.

Preemption doctrine has become a two-directional weapon. In restrictive states, legislatures use "offensive preemption" to block local governments from regulating CPCs or expanding reproductive health protections. In protective states, the New Mexico Supreme Court ruled in January 2025[^12] that local abortion-restrictive ordinances were preempted by the state's Reproductive and Gender-Affirming Health Care Freedom Act. The effect is a hardening of the legal landscape in both directions, with AGs as the primary enforcement arms. Your reproductive rights are now a function of your state capital, enforced by your state attorney general, funded by donors you will never see.

## The SCOTUS Pipeline and Judicial Capture

The judiciary is not a neutral arbiter in this system. It is the system.

The Supreme Court's demolition of voting rights protections followed a thirteen-year trajectory. Shelby County v. Holder in 2013 gutted preclearance. Brnovich v. DNC in 2021 narrowed Section 2 protections for vote denial. Louisiana v. Callais in April 2026 — a 6-3 ruling — struck down a second majority-Black congressional district[^13], requiring proof of intentional discrimination and allowing states to use partisan motives as a defense. Justice Kagan's dissent declared the ruling renders VRA Section 2 "all but a dead letter." No effective federal Voting Rights Act enforcement tool remains.

Below the Supreme Court, the pipeline is accelerating. The Trump administration confirmed 60 Article III federal judges in the first 120 days[^14] of his second term — lifetime appointments, none screened on reproductive rights. Sixth Circuit nominees are critical for abortion access in Michigan, Ohio, Kentucky, and Tennessee. These judges will adjudicate reproductive rights cases for thirty to forty years.

At the state level, judicial elections have become the primary battleground. The 2023 Wisconsin Supreme Court race drew a record $51 million[^15] in spending — five times the previous state record. The liberal victory flipped the court for the first time in fifteen years, with direct implications for Wisconsin's 1849 abortion ban. According to the Brennan Center, at least six states with determinative supreme court elections in 2026 have active abortion litigation pending, and outside spending is tracking toward $100 million.[^16] The people who decide whether your rights exist are selected by the people who profit from their elimination.

## The Gerrymandering–Reproductive Health Feedback Loop

The connection between gerrymandered maps and dead women is not metaphorical. It is statistical.

A Voting Rights Lab correlation analysis found that states with the most restrictive voting access have maternal mortality rates 2.4 times higher[^17] than states with the most expansive voting access — a relationship that persists after controlling for income and urbanicity. The states that suppress votes are the same states that restrict abortion, defund maternal health infrastructure, and refuse Medicaid expansion. It is the same policy ecology, governed by the same actors, funded by the same donors.

Callais activates this feedback loop in real time. Within days of the ruling, Tennessee's governor called a special session. The legislature passed a new map on May 7, 2026, splitting Memphis — 64% Black — across three Republican-leaning districts.[^18] Representative Steve Cohen ended his reelection campaign on May 15, citing the impossibility of winning. Tennessee's delegation is projected to shift from 8-1 Republican to 9-0.[^19] Louisiana is restructuring Representative Cleo Fields' majority-Black district around predominantly white Baton Rouge communities. Mississippi's governor plans to dismantle the state's lone majority-Black district before 2027. All four post-Callais redistricting states have near-total abortion bans. Eliminating majority-Black districts removes the representatives most likely to advocate for reproductive healthcare.

The Senate filibuster completes the trap. The Women's Health Protection Act has failed in four consecutive Congresses. The John Lewis Voting Rights Advancement Act has failed on the same procedural barrier four times. Federal voting rights preclearance has been absent since 2013. Over 1,000 discriminatory voting law changes have been implemented[^20] in formerly covered states since Shelby County, according to the Brennan Center. The communities most harmed — Black women in Southern states — cannot fix either problem through the mechanisms designed to protect them.

## The Global Dimension

The Mexico City Policy was expanded from $8 billion to over $30 billion in affected global aid, enacted January 27, 2026. Approximately 2,600 organizations worldwide are affected.[^21] The U.S. completed its withdrawal from the World Health Organization on January 26, 2026, severing $280 million in funding.[^22] The Guttmacher Institute has documented the mathematical relationship: every $10 million decrease in family planning funding produces approximately 500 additional maternal deaths globally.[^23] The U.S. is exporting its domestic reproductive policy to the developing world, where consequences are measured in deaths.

The downstream specificity of the Mexico City Policy damage is now documentable. An estimated 1,400 clinics globally have closed or suspended operations — 1,175 of those in Africa. Approximately $87 million in direct funding has been lost. An estimated 9 million people have been cut off from reproductive healthcare services.[^24] These are documented facility-by-facility closure records. The countries most affected are those with the highest baseline maternal mortality rates — the women most at risk of dying in childbirth are the ones being denied the care that would prevent it.

The administration simultaneously signed bilateral health cooperation MOUs with Panama ($22.5 million) and Burkina Faso ($147 million)[^25], establishing U.S.-led global health capabilities outside of the WHO — shifting resources away from established global reproductive health and maternal mortality frameworks into bilateral arrangements controlled by the administration.

## The Two-Tier System, Formalized

There is a data point missing from every economic analysis of Dobbs, and its absence is itself a finding: nobody is tracking the shadow economy of forced-birth adoption. As forced pregnancies increase in ban states, there is no federal tracking of the financial dynamics of the private adoption industry — the transfer of wealth from low-income birth mothers to high-income adoptive parents and the fees extracted by private agencies. The babies most likely to be adopted are white. The mothers most likely to be forced into pregnancies they cannot sustain are poor and disproportionately of color. The economic beneficiaries of this system are not the birth mothers. This is an untracked wealth transfer operating inside the Dobbs economy, and no one is counting it.

Meanwhile, WIC has been weaponized through budget politics. A lapse in WIC benefits forces families to suddenly pay the full, unsubsidized cost of specialized infant formula — redirecting limited household funds from rent and utilities into hunger, driving spikes in deep poverty. The proposed cut to WIC's Cash Value Benefit from $26/$52 to $10/$13[^26] would deepen this deliberately engineered food insecurity.

The simultaneous withdrawal of federal infrastructure and variable state response has formalized a two-tier system. In California, state investment of $140 million or more in reproductive healthcare, mandatory nurse staffing ratios, sustained MMRC operations, and decades of quality improvement have produced a maternal mortality rate of 4.0 per 100,000. In Louisiana, the rate is 58.1 per 100,000 — fourteen times California's rate.[^27] Cash-pay concierge models thrive for wealthy patients while the safety net collapses for everyone else. Startups offering $5,000 "menopause optimization" packages[^28] are flush with venture capital, while the local federally qualified health center cannot afford generic estrogen cream because their 340B drug pricing program was restricted. If you work for a Fortune 500 company, your employer covers travel costs for out-of-state reproductive care. If you work retail, you get nothing.

Simultaneously, the physical infrastructure of care has vanished for those without means. Eighty-one clinics closed post-Dobbs, leaving 22 million reproductive-age women—and disproportionately Native American women—living in abortion deserts more than 100 miles from care.[^29] In 2025 alone, full-year data showed abortion incidence reached 1,126,000, but 24% were delivered via telehealth, including 91,000 to patients in total-ban states via shield laws.[^30]

## The Economic Devastation — The $133 Billion Tax

$133 billion: the annual economic cost of abortion restrictions across 16 states (IWPR). 556,042 women suppressed from the workforce. $68 billion in annual economic drag from the 16 states with the most severe bans. $32.3 billion: the annual cost of maternal morbidity from conception through 5 years postpartum. $6.6 billion: lost productivity from maternal morbidity annually.[^31] 8 of the 10 lowest-GDP-per-capita states have abortion bans[^32]: Mississippi, West Virginia, Arkansas, Alabama, Kentucky, South Carolina, Idaho, and Louisiana. 129,000 residents fled ban states post-Dobbs.[^33] Furthermore, Black and Hispanic women now account for 49% of interstate abortion travelers despite comprising only 32% of the reproductive-age population—a structural racial disparity in post-Dobbs access.[^34] Brain drain accelerating.

The compounding nature of costs across a woman's lifetime represents a structural "woman tax": expensive out-of-pocket contraception in her twenties → prohibitive maternal care co-pays in her thirties ($18,865 average; $26,200 for a cesarean section)[^35] → high-cost menopausal symptom management in her fifties. Women aged 45-54 experiencing menopause incur healthcare costs 45-47% higher than their male or pre-menopausal peers, accompanied by an estimated $1.8 billion in annual lost workplace productivity.[^36] This is not a series of unrelated expenses. It is a lifelong economic extraction that inhibits intergenerational wealth accumulation — and it is getting worse at every stage simultaneously.

The $900 billion in total proposed Medicaid cuts[^37], the proposed 65% cut to Title X funding ($286 million annually at risk)[^38], the $223.5 million in terminated NIMHD grants, the $22 million in terminated fetal tissue research, the $280 million withdrawn from the WHO, the $45 million per month in unreimbursed Planned Parenthood care — this money is not being saved. It is being redirected. The Medicaid work requirements are designed to strip coverage; the administrative burden is the point. The system that is being built is not one that eliminates healthcare. It is one that eliminates healthcare for poor women. The wealthy will always have access. The question this administration has answered — through fifty specific, documented actions — is whether everyone else will.

## The IVF and Contraceptive Contradiction

The administration publicly embraces IVF as a "pro-family" technology while systematically undermining the conditions that make it viable. The Alabama Supreme Court's February 2024 ruling in LePage v. Mobile Infirmary[^39] — declaring frozen embryos to be "children" under state wrongful death law — temporarily shut down IVF services at three of Alabama's largest fertility clinics. The ruling exposed the legal fragility of IVF in states with fetal personhood frameworks. If a frozen embryo is a legal person, then the routine IVF process — which creates, evaluates, and discards embryos that are non-viable or carry genetic disease — becomes legally indistinguishable from homicide.

Alabama passed emergency legislation to restore IVF, but the underlying personhood logic remains embedded in the legal architecture of 11 states with active fetal personhood language. Four additional states now face active litigation threatening IVF access, with an estimated 15,000 IVF cycles annually at risk.[^40] Congressional Republicans blocked the Right to IVF Act in June 2024. The racial dimension compounds the contradiction: Black patients face 1.5 times higher infertility rates[^41] but significantly lower IVF utilization. IVF costs $15,000-$30,000 per cycle, is not covered by most insurance plans, and the FTC has opened investigations into fertility clinic pricing. The intersection of legal risk, financial inaccessibility, and racial disparity means IVF is becoming a technology available only to the wealthy, in blue states, with the right employer.

The contradiction extends beyond IVF. Following the Dobbs concurrence, four state bills have been introduced to restrict IUD access citing fetal personhood, threatening the primary long-acting contraceptive for 4.3 million users nationally.[^42] Twelve states have also eliminated or severely restricted judicial bypass for minors seeking abortion, affecting an estimated 45,000 pregnant minors annually.[^43]

## The Comstock Act: A Backdoor National Ban

The Comstock Act of 1873 — an anti-obscenity statute that has been effectively dormant for a century — is being resurrected as a mechanism for a de facto national abortion ban without requiring new legislation. The statute prohibits mailing "obscene" materials, and legal scholars aligned with the current administration argue that abortion medications (mifepristone and misoprostol) qualify as "articles for producing abortion" under the statute's original text.

The significance cannot be overstated: Comstock enforcement would ban the mailing of medication abortion nationwide — including in states where abortion remains legal — without requiring a single new law, a single Congressional vote, or a single court ruling. This would affect the 60%+ of abortions now conducted via medication. A 2026 DOJ internal memo suggests Comstock bars mailing of mifepristone and misoprostol — a reinterpretation that could terminate approximately 500,000 annual mail-order medication abortions.[^44] The 2022 OLC memorandum concluding Comstock does not apply to lawful mailings has been withdrawn. State attorneys general in several ban states have signaled intent to prosecute. The Trump DOJ has already withdrawn from 11 pending FACE Act prosecutions, signaling a non-enforcement era for clinic protections — and clinic violence has risen 346% in states where enforcement dropped.[^45] The Comstock threat exists inside this enforcement vacuum.

Meanwhile, the legal chaos surrounding EMTALA continues to cost lives. HHS documented 22 hospitals denying stabilizing abortion care in EMTALA-covered emergencies in 2025 alone, resulting in three maternal deaths and four hysterectomies while the Moyle v. US conflict remained unresolved.[^46]

## Digital Surveillance of Pregnancy

The vacating of HIPAA reproductive privacy protections in Purl v. HHS (June 2025)[^47] removed the last federal barrier to the digital surveillance of pregnancy. The implications map onto everyday digital behavior:

- Period-tracking apps: used by an estimated 50 million American women. This data can now be subpoenaed. The FTC found that Flo Health — with over 100 million users — had shared menstrual cycle data[^42] and pregnancy intentions with Facebook and Google without consent.
- Search history: queries for abortion services, clinics, and medication abortion can be obtained through standard law enforcement data requests.
- Location data: commercially available from data brokers without a warrant. Data broker Near Intelligence sold location data from nearly 600 Planned Parenthood facilities[^48] to the anti-abortion Veritas Society, enabling targeted anti-abortion ads to clinic visitors' phones. State AGs in three ban states have issued geofence warrants capturing all individuals within 100 meters of clinics.[^49]
- Pharmacy records: purchases of emergency contraception, pregnancy tests, and misoprostol can be tracked through retail pharmacy databases.
- License plate surveillance: a Texas sheriff's office used the Flock Safety ALPR network — 83,000 cameras across 6,809 networks[^50] — to track a woman suspected of self-administered abortion across state lines. The search was logged as "had an abortion, search for female."

Multiple states with abortion bans have already attempted to subpoena digital health data. The chilling effect is documented: an estimated 30% of period-tracking app users in ban states deleted their apps within six months of *Dobbs*.[^43] Women are avoiding digital health tools — which are the primary interface for prenatal care scheduling, medication reminders, and symptom tracking — because they fear their own health data will be used against them.

The convergence of Comstock, digital surveillance, and the HIPAA vacatur creates a legal environment in which a woman's pregnancy can be detected, tracked, and prosecuted through her own digital footprint — without her knowledge, without her consent, and without a warrant.

This surveillance feeds a growing criminalization apparatus: 73% of US pregnancy criminalization cases involve Black women, and the rate of prosecution is accelerating post-Dobbs, with 12 new cases filed in 2026 alone.[^51]

## The Democratic Erosion Closes the Exits

The final dimension of the corruption is the systematic closure of every democratic mechanism that could reverse it.

Voters have tried. In every state where abortion has appeared on a ballot since Dobbs — ten of ten between 2022 and 2024 — voters chose to protect reproductive rights. Kansas shocked observers in August 2022 when 59% of voters in a deep-red state rejected an anti-abortion amendment.[^52] In 2024, seven of ten states approved protections.[^53] The direct democracy strategy works. Which is precisely why it is being dismantled.

Eleven states have enacted new restrictions on initiative and referendum processes since 2022 — raising signature thresholds and imposing supermajority ratification requirements of 60% to 67%. Florida's abortion measure received 57% support in 2024 but failed the 60% threshold.[^54] The strategy is anti-majoritarian by design: abortion measures consistently win at 55-63%, so the threshold is raised above the margin of victory.[^55] Nineteen states enacted restrictions on absentee and mail voting since 2020, producing a 23% drop in absentee voting — steepest among Black and Latino voters.[^56]

Voter suppression, gerrymandered legislatures, captured courts, weaponized AGs, and procedural sabotage of direct democracy operate as a system. The same states that ban abortion suppress votes, stack courts, fund CPCs, criminalize providers, surveil patients, and raise the threshold for the ballot measures that could undo it all. Fourteen states enacted both anti-trans legislation and abortion restrictions in the same sessions between 2022 and 2026[^57] — the same sponsors authoring both. The target is not abortion alone. It is bodily autonomy as a governing principle.

The corruption is not that the system is broken. The corruption is that the system is working exactly as redesigned — extracting wealth, consolidating power, eliminating oversight, and sealing the exits so the people harmed by it cannot vote their way out.
