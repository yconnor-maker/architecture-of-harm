# Chapter 6: Perinatal Mental Health

## The Crisis No One Built Infrastructure For

---

She had figured she miscarried over the weekend. Her stomach started looking different. She came in. She drove herself. Her family was in Charlotte. She was alone.

She was young. Second miscarriage — both early, both around seven weeks. She had been throwing up every thirty minutes for three or four weeks, couldn't eat, had been to the emergency room multiple times. They gave her fluids each time and sent her home. They prescribed an antiemetic she never picked up. Nobody followed up.

The physician walked in and saw a woman who looked, in her clinical assessment, "very sad." Not sad the way people are sad after a loss. Sad in a way that makes a physician concerned. The patient had been put in foster care as a child. Her siblings were separated. Her mother's home was not safe — her younger siblings were "too bad." Her partner of four or five years had left when he found out about the pregnancy. He didn't want it. She had been raped before. She had relocated to a new town where she knew almost no one. She was living alone in a place where the toilet would not flush. She was using trash bags. She drove for Amazon and had been out of work for weeks.

The ultrasound showed retained tissue. Her white count was elevated — 12,000. The differential diagnosis was infection versus the body's normal response to loss. But the physician's primary concern was not the uterus. It was the patient's mental state.

"Tell me about this feeling of being out of it," the physician said. Then: "Who is around you that you trust?"

The physician called the patient's mother on speaker. She called her "princess." Her mother offered what she could — "You're welcome to come here" — but everyone in the room understood it was not a real option.

"I can't stay here too long," the patient said. "I still have bills to pay."

"Are you able to work in this condition?" the physician asked.

Silence.

"My friend, that's the depression talking," the physician said. "We haven't even tried anything."

She stayed with her for over an hour. She explained the difference between sadness and depression — that sadness is the appropriate response to loss, but depression changes brain chemistry in ways that willpower cannot reverse. That it is no different from telling someone to concentrate really hard on curing their infection. "You would say, 'You are a bad doctor. Give me some antibiotics.' It's the same thing." She admitted the patient for stabilization. She coordinated a psychiatric evaluation. She tried to find resources for housing, for safety, for the basic scaffolding of a life that was collapsing in real time.

Seventy-eight minutes. One patient. One visit. And the system had nothing waiting for either of them on the other side of the hospital door.

---

Mental health conditions account for approximately 23 percent of all pregnancy-related deaths in the United States.[^1] That figure — nearly one in four — makes mental health the single largest clinical category of pregnancy-related mortality, exceeding hemorrhage, hypertension, and infection individually. According to CDC Maternal Mortality Review Committee data, more than 80 percent of these deaths are judged preventable.[^2] The women who die from this do not die in delivery rooms. They die in the months afterward, in a window the system was specifically designed not to cover.

Sixty-two percent of all pregnancy-related suicides occur between 43 and 365 days postpartum[^3] — precisely the period that, until recently, fell outside the 60-day Medicaid postpartum coverage window that governed healthcare access for over 40 percent of all U.S. births. Suicide is now the leading single cause of death in the postpartum year, outpacing every obstetric complication. The insurance expired while the crisis peaked. That was not a policy oversight. It was a policy execution.

The geography of maternal suicide mirrors the geography of abandonment. Pregnancy-associated suicide rates vary four-fold across states: from 2.1 per 100,000 live births in Massachusetts to 8.7 per 100,000 in Wyoming.[^4] States without Medicaid expansion have maternal suicide rates 2.3 times higher than expansion states.[^5] The highest rates cluster in Wyoming, Alaska, and Montana — rural states with no perinatal psychiatry infrastructure and geographic isolation that makes the nearest mental health provider a three-hour drive. The lowest rates anchor in Massachusetts and Connecticut — states with integrated perinatal systems, universal Medicaid, and the PPAP model Massachusetts pioneered.

## The Doubling

In 2010, 9.4 percent of women were diagnosed with postpartum depression at delivery. By 2021, that number had nearly doubled to 19 percent[^6] — roughly one in five mothers leaving the hospital with a diagnosis. Among non-Hispanic Black women, the increase was 140 percent over the same period[^7] — the steepest racial slope in the data. Today, approximately 20 percent of all new mothers nationally[^8] meet criteria for postpartum depression, with rates reaching 35 percent among Medicaid-enrolled women.

The COVID-19 pandemic turned a building crisis into a conflagration. Perinatal depression symptoms among pregnant and postpartum women surged from 15 percent to 41 percent[^9] — a 173 percent increase. Anxiety symptoms rose from 29 percent to 72 percent.[^10] Nearly three-quarters of pregnant and postpartum women were experiencing significant anxiety during the pandemic. Among Black women, pandemic-era perinatal mood disorder rates reached 49 percent.[^11] Among Hispanic women, 47 percent.[^12] Essential worker status meant higher exposure trauma combined with continued work obligation. NICU visitor bans created maternal isolation trauma that disproportionately impacted Black mothers, who already carry elevated preterm birth rates.

The pandemic peak was supposed to be transient. It was not. Perinatal depression and anxiety rates in 2023–2024 remained 30 to 40 percent above pre-pandemic baselines[^13], driven by the collapse of informal childcare networks — grandparent COVID deaths decimated Black and Latino multigenerational childcare systems — the permanent loss of employer-based childcare, and housing costs that made single-income parenting unviable. An estimated 1.2 million mothers reduced work hours for childcare in 2023 alone[^14], each instance an independent risk factor for perinatal mood disorder. The psychiatric infrastructure to treat them did not exist.

Before the pandemic, the United States had approximately two inpatient perinatal psychiatric units for the entire country[^15] — for 4 million annual births. The United Kingdom, with fewer than 700,000 annual births, had 22 mother-baby units. State psychiatric bed capacity declined 8 percent between 2016 and 2023, to 10.8 beds per 100,000 people.[^16] Fewer than 250 board-certified reproductive psychiatrists now serve 3.7 million annual births[^17] — down from an already inadequate 500. Psychiatric residency programs include an average of 2.4 hours of perinatal psychiatry content across four years of training.[^18] Twelve fellowship programs exist nationwide.[^19] The median wait for consultation without a PPAP is 47 days[^20] — in a clinical context where postpartum psychosis can become lethal in hours. Seventy-five percent of affected women went untreated[^21] — not because they didn't need care, but because the care did not exist.

## Beyond Depression: The Anxiety and PTSD That No One Screens For

Postpartum depression dominates the public conversation, but the clinical reality is broader and more dangerous than the narrative suggests.

Perinatal anxiety disorders affect 18 percent of pregnant women and 17 percent of postpartum women.[^22] They co-occur with depression at rates of 50 to 60 percent.[^23] Yet perinatal anxiety screening rates run 30 to 40 percent lower than depression screening nationally.[^24] The Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale — the most widely used screening tool — contains an anxiety subscale in items 3, 4, and 5, but clinicians do not always score it separately. The result is that the most common perinatal mental health condition after depression is systematically under-detected. ACOG updated its clinical practice guidelines in 2024 to recommend screening for both perinatal depression and anxiety at every prenatal visit and at the 4-to-6-week postpartum visit[^25], expanding the prior recommendation of a single postpartum screen. Twelve months post-publication, an estimated 35 percent of practices remain non-compliant with the new guideline.[^26]

Perinatal PTSD is the condition the system is least equipped to see. It affects 3 to 9 percent of postpartum women overall, but the rate reaches 18 percent among Black women with histories of intimate partner violence.[^27] Among all deliveries, 4 percent produce PTSD from the birth experience itself[^28] — emergency cesarean sections, perinatal loss, NICU admissions, and what the clinical literature now terms obstetric violence: unconsented procedures, dismissal of pain, provider disrespect during labor. Sixty-eight percent of pregnant women with opioid use disorder carry documented PTSD[^29], their addiction and their trauma sharing the same upstream pipeline. Only 22 percent of women with perinatal PTSD receive PTSD-specific treatment.[^30] The condition is frequently misdiagnosed as postpartum depression because OB settings do not routinely screen for PTSD, and the symptom overlap — insomnia, emotional numbing, difficulty bonding with the infant — makes misclassification easy.

This matters clinically because the treatments are different. A woman with PTSD who is prescribed an SSRI for presumed depression will receive partial symptom relief at best. She needs trauma-focused therapy — and the infrastructure for that in the perinatal period does not exist at scale.

## The Screening-to-Treatment Chasm

Screening has improved dramatically. National perinatal depression screening rates reached 72 percent of prenatal visits and 58 percent of postpartum visits in 2024, up from 42 percent and 28 percent in 2019.[^31] Thirty-two states now mandate perinatal screening by law.[^32] The EPDS has been validated in 11 languages for U.S. clinical use and demonstrates 86 percent sensitivity at the standard cutoff of 10 or above.[^33]

But screening without treatment is a diagnostic exercise with no clinical endpoint. Treatment initiation among women who screen positive remains below 30 percent.[^34] The screen-to-care boundary is where the system fails — a positive result meets a referral void. Black women are screened at near-equivalent rates to White women (70 versus 73 percent)[^35], but they are three times more likely to screen positive and not receive follow-up.[^36] Hispanic women are screened at 64 percent.[^37] American Indian and Alaska Native women — the population with the highest pregnancy-associated suicide and overdose rates in the country — are screened at only 51 percent.[^38]

The EPDS carries a structural bias. Validated primarily in White, English-speaking populations, its sensitivity drops to 74 percent in Asian American women and 70 percent in recent African immigrant women.[^39] For Spanish-speaking Latina perinatal women, care access runs 38 percent lower than English-speaking peers[^40] — not because the EPDS lacks a validated Spanish version (it has one, with 85 percent sensitivity)[^41], but because 62 percent of perinatal mental health providers lack Spanish-speaking staff or certified interpretation.[^42] A promotora community health worker model reaches three times more Spanish-speaking women than clinic-based outreach[^43], but these programs remain exceptions, not infrastructure.

The treatment chasm is widened by insurance barriers that function as policy choices. Despite the Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act, perinatal mental health services face prior authorization denial rates three times higher than obstetric services.[^44] Commercial insurers deny perinatal psychiatry claims at 18 percent versus 6 percent for other OB services.[^45] A 2024 Department of Labor audit found non-quantitative treatment limitation violations in 34 percent of commercial plans[^46] — meaning a third of insurers were breaking the law, and the treatment was still being denied.

## The Racial Architecture

Black women experience postpartum depression at rates 38 percent higher than White women[^47], are 40 percent less likely to receive a formal diagnosis, and initiate pharmacotherapy at less than half the rate of their White peers.[^48] In rural settings, Black women's PPD rates run 80 percent higher than those of their White rural counterparts.[^49] Only 37 percent of Black and Hispanic women receive any mental health treatment for perinatal conditions, compared to 67 percent of White women.[^50] Indigenous women experience depression 87 percent more often than White women and carry a lifetime sexual violence prevalence of 56.1 percent[^51] — a rate of traumatic exposure that directly feeds the perinatal mental health crisis through the ACEs pipeline documented below.

American Indian and Alaska Native women carry the most catastrophic outcomes in the data. Their pregnancy-associated suicide and overdose rates are 4.2 times higher than White women's.[^52] The Indian Health Service — a federal treaty obligation — operates at mental health staffing 40 percent below recommended, with per-capita mental health spending of $283 versus $1,491 in non-IHS systems.[^53] Sixty-eight percent of AIAN women report unmet perinatal mental health needs.[^54]

This is not a failure of individual women to seek help. It is a system that does not employ providers who look like the patients, does not fund treatment in the geographies where the need is greatest, and does not screen with culturally appropriate instruments. The treatment gap persists at every income level — racial disparities in perinatal mental health outcomes remain statistically significant even after adjusting for socioeconomic status, indicating racism as an independent driver beyond poverty.

## The ACEs Pipeline: How Childhood Trauma Becomes Maternal Death

The pipeline from childhood trauma to maternal mortality has been quantified at every link.

At least 1 in 4 girls in the United States experiences sexual abuse before age 18.[^55] Among American Indian and Alaska Native women, 56.1 percent report lifetime contact sexual violence. Across all women, 26.8 percent — more than 33 million — report completed or attempted rape.[^56] This is the first input.

The biology of what follows is dosed and measurable. An ACE score of 4 or more produces a 7-times elevation in alcoholism risk, an 11-times elevation in intravenous drug use, and a nearly 3-times elevation in depression.[^57] At an ACE score of 6, life expectancy shortens by 20 years.[^58]

In pregnancy, 4 or more ACEs produce an odds ratio of 3.06 for preeclampsia.[^59] Every ACE beyond zero increases preterm delivery risk by 13 percent.[^60] Any ACEs increase antenatal depression risk by an odds ratio of 1.59, low birth weight by 1.27, gestational diabetes by 1.39.[^61] One in five American adults carries 4 or more ACEs. Among AIAN adults, 32.4 percent. Among Black adults, 24 percent. Among adults earning under $15,000, 24.1 percent.[^62]

The woman who arrives at her first prenatal appointment carrying 4+ ACEs is arriving with a pregnancy already marked by the accumulated biology of her history. When clinicians treat her hypertension or her gestational diabetes in isolation from that history, they treat the symptom while leaving the cause untouched.

The pipeline closes through three parallel mechanisms: ACEs produce chronic disease, which produces high-risk pregnancy and elevated maternal mortality. ACEs produce untreated mental health conditions, which produce maternal suicide and impaired bonding that accumulates new ACEs in the next generation. ACEs produce substance use disorder, which produces overdose death and the family separation pipeline documented in this report's chapters on substance use and incarceration. The loop is intergenerational.

## Where Mental Health Meets Substance Use: The Dual Crisis

Seventy-three percent of women with opioid use disorder in pregnancy have co-occurring depression or anxiety.[^63] Sixty-eight percent carry documented PTSD. These conditions share neurobiological pathways and an upstream cause in the ACEs pipeline. Yet the treatment system operates as if they were different diseases treated by different providers in different buildings.

Integrated programs that co-locate medication-assisted treatment with mental health services, peer support, and social work show 45 percent better treatment retention and dramatically lower maternal mortality — 0.8 per 1,000 compared to 2.4 per 1,000 in siloed care.[^64] The mechanism is straightforward: siloed care creates a dropout cascade where mental health treatment dropout leads to MAT dropout leads to relapse leads to death. Integration removes the fragmented care navigation burden from the patients least equipped to carry it.

Yet pregnant women face reduced access to the treatment that exists. Buprenorphine appointment access for pregnant women stands at 61.4 percent, compared to 73.9 percent for non-pregnant women.[^65] Ninety percent of specialty addiction care is concentrated in urban areas[^66], leaving rural pregnant women structurally excluded. The substance abuse counselors who staff these programs earn an average of $34,000 per year[^67] — poverty wages for work that prevents downstream death.

## The Poverty Gradient

Poverty is the single strongest social predictor of perinatal mood and anxiety disorder onset and severity. Women with household incomes below 200 percent of the federal poverty level experience postpartum depression at 28 percent — nearly double the 15 percent rate for women above 400 percent FPL.[^68] Among women who were poor before birth, 68 percent remain in poverty afterward.[^69]

The social determinants are additive. Housing instability elevates perinatal mood disorder risk by 45 percent. Food insecurity by 52 percent. Absence of childcare by 40 percent. Social isolation by 55 percent.[^70] When three or more co-occur — and one in three perinatal women reports two or more concurrent adversities — the cumulative risk is 3.2 times baseline.[^71] These adversities fall disproportionately on Black, Hispanic, and AIAN women, but racial disparities persist at equivalent income levels, confirming that racism operates as an independent driver beyond socioeconomic status.

## What Was Built, What Is Being Destroyed

All fifty states plus the District of Columbia have now implemented 12-month postpartum Medicaid coverage — a historic expansion, created by the American Rescue Plan Act of 2021, made permanent by the Consolidated Appropriations Act of 2023, and fully operational across every state as of January 2026. An estimated 1.7 million additional postpartum women per year[^72] are now eligible for extended mental health coverage beyond the old 60-day cliff. For the first time, the coverage window matches the clinical risk window.

Perinatal Psychiatry Access Programs now operate in 31 states, providing real-time telephone consultation to OB providers who lack perinatal psychiatry expertise. Where PPAPs exist, median wait times for perinatal psychiatry consultation drop from 47 days to 2.4 days.[^73] These programs function as a workforce multiplier — enabling primary OB providers to manage mild-to-moderate perinatal mood disorders without a specialist referral that would take weeks. But 40 percent of high-need rural counties remain without PPAP coverage.[^74]

Telehealth now accounts for 47 percent of all perinatal mental health visits nationally, following permanent Medicare and Medicaid telehealth reauthorization. No-show rates dropped 31 percent compared to in-person visits. But broadband infrastructure gaps reproduce geographic inequity even when policy is equitable: rural telehealth utilization stands at 28 percent versus 54 percent urban[^75], and AIAN women on tribal lands have the lowest telehealth utilization of any group because the broadband does not exist.

The 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline has experienced an 80 percent surge in demand since its launch, with monthly contacts growing from 355,000 to more than 654,000. Text message volume has increased more than 11-fold[^76], reflecting shifting communication preferences among younger populations — and accessibility of texting in unsafe or private situations. The National Maternal Mental Health Hotline (1-833-TLC-MAMA), launched in 2022 as the first dedicated federal line for perinatal mental health crises, is under threat from HHS staffing reductions.

The proposed Medicaid cuts now law through the One Big Beautiful Bill Act — $880 billion over 10 years — would undo the coverage that took a generation to build. The Medicaid work requirements taking effect in January 2027 will strip coverage from millions of women of reproductive age. The 12-month postpartum extension is meaningless if the woman loses her Medicaid eligibility before she becomes pregnant.

Dobbs is not merely a reproductive rights issue — it is a psychiatric event. Women in states with abortion restrictions show depression and anxiety rates 18 percent higher[^77] than women in non-restriction states. Thirty-nine percent of OB/GYN providers in ban states report inability to provide standard mental health referrals due to legal uncertainty.[^78] Twenty-five percent of maternal-fetal medicine specialists have left ban states, reducing perinatal psychiatry consultation availability in precisely the states adding the greatest psychiatric burden.[^79] Adolescent mothers — who experience postpartum depression at 2.5 times the rate of adult mothers, with only 19 percent initiating treatment[^80] — are increasing in high-restriction states where forced parenthood at an adolescent developmental stage has independent psychiatric consequences.

The FDA approved zuranolone (Zurzuvae) in August 2023 as the first oral treatment specifically for postpartum depression — producing 50 percent remission by day 15, compared to 30 percent for placebo.[^81] But two years after approval, fewer than 25,000 patients have received it. The $15,900 list price per 14-day course and Medicaid non-preferred formulary status in 32 states[^82] have created a two-tier system: commercially insured White women hold the majority of prescriptions, while Medicaid-enrolled Black, Hispanic, and low-income women face near-total exclusion.

The Bipartisan Safer Communities Act grants that funded mental health staff in K-12 schools — the front-line identification system for children accumulating the ACEs that will become the next generation's maternal deaths — were rescinded in April 2025. The school counselor-to-student ratio nationally sits between 385:1 and 408:1 — more than 50 percent above the recommended maximum of 250:1.[^83] HRSA projects a combined behavioral health workforce deficit exceeding 277,000 clinicians by 2038: 77,050 addiction counselors, 99,780 mental health counselors, and 99,840 psychologists short.[^84] The workforce that would deliver treatment is in structural free fall.

The administration's actions form a coherent pattern: expand the conditions that produce perinatal mental illness (ban abortion, cut safety net, reduce housing and nutrition programs, escalate immigration enforcement that suppresses care-seeking) while simultaneously reducing the capacity to treat it (cut behavioral health workforce funding, rescind school mental health grants, threaten the hotline, defund SAMHSA, maintain parity law violations without enforcement). The result is predictable, dosed, and measurable. It will be counted in maternal deaths — if anyone is left to count them.
