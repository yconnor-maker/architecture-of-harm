# Chapter 12: Research and Surveillance — Destroying the Instruments

*When you eliminate the capacity to measure a problem, you eliminate the political pressure to fix it. When you eliminate the capacity to protect the data that remains, you turn measurement into a weapon.*

## Before

The United States had built the most advanced public health surveillance infrastructure in the world. It was imperfect. It was underfunded. It was slow. But it existed, and it worked.

The Pregnancy Risk Assessment Monitoring System — PRAMS — coordinated by the CDC since 1987, was the only system of its kind: the only mechanism that systematically tracked what happens to women before, during, and after pregnancy, in every participating state, by race, geography, and risk factor. It had been running for nearly 40 years.

Maternal Mortality Review Committees in most states investigated every pregnancy-related death to determine whether it was preventable, what the contributing factors were, and what system changes could prevent the next one. By 2024, all 50 states and the District of Columbia had established MMRCs — the first nationwide infrastructure for systematic surveillance of preventable maternal deaths. Over 80 percent of maternal deaths reviewed by these committees were determined to have been preventable. The CDC's STI surveillance infrastructure tracked syphilis, gonorrhea, chlamydia, and HIV outbreak geography. The National Birth Defects Prevention Network tracked congenital anomalies. The ART surveillance system tracked assisted reproductive technology outcomes. The Safe to Sleep program educated parents about infant sleep safety. The NIMHD — the National Institute on Minority Health and Health Disparities — funded $534 million in research producing the evidence for every racial health disparity published in a medical journal.

This infrastructure did not merely observe outcomes. It produced the clinical protocols that save lives: magnesium sulfate for preeclampsia, antenatal corticosteroids for preterm birth, evidence-based hemorrhage bundles, standardized hypertension treatment algorithms. Every one of those protocols exists because someone funded the research, enrolled the patients, collected the data, and published the findings. That infrastructure took decades to build. California used it to reduce maternal mortality by 55% between 2006 and 2013 — from 16.9 to 7.3 per 100,000[^4] — through the CMQCC: evidence-based hemorrhage and preeclampsia toolkits, quality collaboratives, data transparency. That is what this infrastructure made possible.

NIH was funding 27 institutes with active clinical trials for preeclampsia, preterm birth, and placental development. Women's health research received 8.8 percent of total NIH grant spending[^5] over the prior decade — already a fraction of what the disease burden warranted. The National Academies had recommended an additional $15.7 billion over five years.[^4] And even within that underfunded infrastructure, the pipeline was structurally inequitable: a landmark 2011 study in Science documented that Black principal investigators were funded at 55 to 65 percent the rate of white investigators[^4] after controlling for education, training, and productivity — a gap that NIH's own UNITE initiative, launched in 2021, had not closed.

## Trump 1.0

Fetal tissue research restricted in 2019, disrupting placental and preeclampsia research. NIH independence challenged politically. Research investment politicized.

## What Began to Recover

Biden reversed fetal tissue restrictions in 2021. ARPA-H created ($2.5 billion). "Sprint for Women's Health" launched — $113 million to 24 projects.[^6] NIMHD supported. The research infrastructure began to stabilize.

More than stabilize. The White House Initiative on Women's Health Research (November 2023) catalyzed $200 million in new FY2025 NIH women's health funding[^7], bringing total projected spending to $5 billion — still only 10 percent of the $48 billion NIH budget,[^7] but the largest dedicated increase in decades. The Department of Defense committed $500 million.[^8] Combined with the ARPA-H Sprint's $100 million, total federal innovation commitment exceeded $800 million.[^9] ARPA-H launched the MOCS program — Making Obstetrics Care Smart — the first federal program explicitly integrating AI into obstetric care. The PCORI reauthorization in 2019 had secured $4.4 billion through 2029 in a Trust Fund structure insulated from annual appropriations volatility — one of the few pieces of infrastructure that would survive what came next.

## Trump 2.0: The Dismantlement

**PRAMS destroyed.** The entire PRAMS team was fired April 1, 2025, before releasing the 2023 data.[^1] Federal funding expires April 2026[^3], and multiple states have already reported disrupted data collection. 2023 is the last year for which we will have reliable, nationally comparable data on pregnancy outcomes. We have no idea what happened in 2024. We have no idea what is happening right now.

**Surveillance infrastructure systematically eliminated:**
- Women's Health and Fertility Branch — eliminated
- Contraceptive Best Practices Team — eliminated
- CDC STI surveillance labs — closed
- 8-person STI committee — dissolved
- ART surveillance — terminated
- Birth defects tracking — cut
- Safe to Sleep program — eliminated (April 2025)
- Over 200 CDC datasets removed or altered between January and February 2025
- Pages on gender-based violence, HIV, abortion access, LGBTQ+ health, and teenage depression — removed or redacted
- Nearly half of CDC surveillance databases: updates paused
- Terminology altered: "gender" replaced with "sex" across federal health documents
- OMB executive directive removed sexual orientation and gender identity data collection from all federally funded research — a permanent research blind spot on LGBTQ+ health
- HHS reduced from 82,000 to 62,000 staff; over 4,800 federal health workers fired

DOGE-directed CDC workforce reductions eliminated approximately 2,400 staff,[^10] with the Division of Reproductive Health and Division of Vital Statistics disproportionately affected. These were the researchers who tracked maternal mortality, infant mortality, and reproductive health outcomes. The capacity to monitor preventable deaths in real time — to detect a spike in sepsis from delayed miscarriage management, to track the geographic spread of congenital syphilis — was not automated. It depended on epidemiologists, data analysts, and public health officers who received buyout offers and termination letters.

**The scale of grant termination.** As of November 2025, HHS had terminated 977 NIH grant awards representing approximately $1.7 billion in obligated funding.[^2] NIH flagged more than 1,700 grants as "not consistent with Trump's directives." Terminated grants included intimate partner violence research — a leading cause of pregnancy-associated death — HIV prevention research, and adolescent health research. The NSF separately terminated over 400 grants for DEI non-compliance.[^10] AAU member institutions reported over $3 billion in total federal grant terminations[^11] by mid-2025. Clinical trials were interrupted. Minority-serving institutions had no bridge funding.

**NIMHD gutted — and the anti-DEI framing.** 77 grants terminated ($223.5 million), 29.6% cut, 61% fewer competitive grants[^12], targeted for elimination. The framing of this elimination as "anti-DEI" is politically useful. The functional outcome is defunding the only research infrastructure specifically designed to understand the patients who are dying the most. NIMHD is the primary federal funding source for research on Black maternal mortality, on why fibroids disproportionately affect Black women, on what social determinants are actually driving the maternal mortality crisis. Black women die at 3-4 times the white rate.[^6] The one institute whose entire purpose was to understand why and fix it was targeted for destruction — not because it was failing, but because its work was reclassified as ideology. Title X funding was simultaneously frozen under the guise of reviewing grantee compliance with anti-DEI executive orders.

**Fetal tissue research grants terminated.** 17 active grants worth $22 million were ended.[^13] These were not abstract bench science — they were clinical trials studying preeclampsia, preterm birth, and placental development. Preeclampsia is a disease of the placenta. You cannot do preeclampsia research without studying placental tissue. When you terminate those grants, you need years of re-enrollment, tissue collection, and rebuilt teams. This is generational damage. And no FDA-approved drug has ever been developed specifically for preeclampsia — despite over 70,000 maternal deaths globally per year.[^14] The drugs we use — aspirin and magnesium sulfate — are generics from the 1920s.[^14] A century-long R&D failure driven by short treatment windows, exclusion of pregnant women from trials, and a patient population that is disproportionately lower-income and non-white. BARDA has never prioritized preeclampsia. The fetal tissue terminations guaranteed this pharmaceutical void will persist.

The political history of fetal tissue research is itself a measure of the damage: Reagan moratorium (1988), Clinton lifted it (1993), Trump 1.0 severely restricted it (2019), Biden reversed it (2021), Trump 2.0 formally terminated it (January 2026). This 10-to-20-year structural instability ultimately drives researchers permanently out of the field.

**NIH consolidation — and the funding illusion.** The administration proposed an $18 billion NIH cut — 40%.[^15] Congress rejected it. The Consolidated Appropriations Act (H.R. 7148) appropriated $47.2 billion — a $400 million increase.[^10] On paper, protected. In practice, OMB imposed spending restrictions requiring every institute to submit detailed spend plans before releasing funds. Combined with the 43-day federal shutdown, grant awards are running at a fraction of normal pace. FY 2025 success rates dropped to approximately 17%.[^16] The money exists. It cannot be spent. Jay Bhattacharya was appointed single director of NIH and CDC simultaneously. A proposed consolidation would reduce 27 institutes to 5.[^17] The NIH Office of Research on Women's Health — which had just released its 2025–2029 Strategic Plan — faces consolidation, rendering its $55 million budget aspirational.[^4] ACOG withdrew from the CDC's Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices. Fifteen states sued.

**What the Women's Health Initiative produced — before it was terminated.** WHI was terminated in September 2025. Here is what it produced over its three decades — just the findings from 2024-2025 alone: a 2024 JAMA comprehensive review establishing that low-fat dietary patterns reduce breast cancer mortality; a June 2025 NIH finding that estrogen-only hormone therapy reduces breast cancer incidence by 14% in women under 55;[^4] a December 2025 finding that hormone therapy is NOT associated with increased breast cancer risk in women with BRCA1/BRCA2 mutations — the largest prospective analysis ever conducted; and a March 2026 study identifying a blood biomarker (phosphorylated tau 217) that predicts dementia risk 25 years before symptoms appear.[^16] Every one of those findings came from WHI data. Every one of them has the potential to save thousands of lives. The initiative that produced them has been eliminated.

**ARPA-H — the question mark.** ARPA-H — the Advanced Research Projects Agency for Health, established in 2022 within NIH with a venture-capital model — invested $113 million in women's health across 24 awardees[^6] in six months under the Biden administration. Its non-dilutive funding model allowed startups to retain equity — a structural innovation in federal health investment. As of March 2026, ARPA-H's operational status under the new HHS leadership is uncertain. Its budget is technically included in the NIH appropriation, but its independence is at risk in the 27-to-5 institute consolidation plan. The only federal agency specifically designed to fund breakthrough women's health innovation may be eliminated before it completes its first investment cycle.

**CDC bifurcation.** A proposal would split the CDC into an infectious disease entity and a "lifestyle-related" chronic disease entity. The diseases reclassified as "lifestyle" include reproductive health, maternal mortality, obesity, diabetes, and mental health. These are not lifestyle choices. Separating infectious disease from reproductive health surveillance means the next congenital syphilis epidemic gets tracked by an agency with no mandate to integrate pregnancy data. The next COVID maternal mortality surge becomes invisible by institutional design. The fragmentation is the point.

**The MMRC paradox.** Congress funded the Maternal Mortality Review Committees ($113.5 million through 2030).[^6] Idaho tried to disband its own MMRC to stop publishing data showing its abortion ban is killing women. The paradox: they can count how women die. They cannot fund the systems to prevent it. Surveillance without intervention is not a healthcare system. It is an autopsy.

## The Post-Dobbs Research Chill

The destruction of federal research infrastructure is happening alongside a second crisis: the legal environment created by state abortion bans is chilling reproductive health research. Researchers at Johns Hopkins and the Multi-Regional Clinical Trials Center have documented a measurable deterrence effect — IRB denials for abortion outcomes research, researcher departures from ban states, deterred clinical trial enrollment for reproductive-age participants. The ambiguity extends to documentation of pregnancy during trials, management of miscarriage complications, and criminal prosecution risk for trial staff.

This compounds a gender data gap predating the 1993 NIH Revitalization Act. Thirty years after that mandate, cardiovascular trials remain approximately 25 to 30 percent female.[^6] More than 67 percent of drugs prescribed to pregnant women lack adequate safety or dosing data.[^18] The research infrastructure is being dismantled from both ends — politically from above, legally from below.

## Health Data as a Weapon: Surveillance in the Post-Dobbs Era

RestoredCDC.org exists because researchers lost access to federal data — an independent mirror maintained by librarians and public health advocates, archiving federal data before it disappears. This is not normal. This is emergency data preservation. But the crisis in research surveillance is mirrored by a second crisis: the weaponization of health data against the patients it was designed to protect.

**Period apps, data brokers, and digital subpoenas.** The FTC settled with Flo Health after the app — used by over 100 million people globally[^8] — shared menstrual cycle data, pregnancy status, and health symptoms with Facebook and Google, even for users paying for premium privacy. Most period tracking apps are classified as "wellness" devices outside HIPAA. According to FTC estimates, 88 percent of popular health apps are not HIPAA-covered.[^1] After Dobbs, app deletions spiked more than 30 percent[^11] — women understood the risk before regulators acted. They were right: law enforcement in ban states has issued subpoenas and search warrants for menstrual app data, health search histories, and location data. At least three abortion prosecutions between 2022 and 2024 used digital health data as evidence.[^8] The Digital Defense Fund reported a fourfold increase in digital security assistance requests post-Dobbs.[^4] SafeGraph sold smartphone location data of abortion clinic visitors to anti-abortion groups for as little as $160 per dataset.[^19] Google responded by auto-deleting location history for visits to abortion clinics and fertility centers. A technology company implemented stronger reproductive data protections than the federal government.

**The Change Healthcare breach.** In February 2024, Russian-linked ransomware group ALPHV/BlackCat accessed Change Healthcare — which processes roughly one in three U.S. patient claims[^4] — through compromised credentials with no multi-factor authentication. The breach exposed protected health information of approximately 100 million Americans.[^8] UnitedHealth Group paid a $22 million ransom.[^13] It was the largest health data breach in U.S. history. The MOVEit vulnerability simultaneously compromised Medicaid data across at least ten states — Missouri alone, 3.2 million beneficiaries.[^20] These breaches disproportionately exposed BIPOC and low-income populations dependent on safety-net systems where 40 percent of critical access hospitals lack dedicated cybersecurity staff.[^15]

**The enforcement vacuum.** HHS's Office for Civil Rights — sole federal HIPAA enforcer — operates on $38 million annually to protect data for 330 million Americans,[^21] handling 60,000-plus complaints but issuing fewer than five civil penalties per year.[^21] Average breach-to-resolution: 2.8 years.[^21] Under the current administration, OCR staffing was cut 30 percent through DOGE.[^11] The FY2026 budget proposes cutting the FTC by over $100 million.[^8] The 2024 HIPAA rule prohibiting disclosure of reproductive health records for law enforcement has been delayed. Meanwhile, the health data broker industry generates $11 billion annually.[^4] IQVIA and Symphony Health aggregate over 90 percent of U.S. pharmacy transactions[^12], selling medication histories enabling inference-based surveillance — mifepristone identifies abortions, antiretrovirals identify HIV, naloxone identifies opioid use — without diagnosis codes. Retail loyalty programs predict pregnancy with 85 to 90 percent accuracy.[^22] One-third of top U.S. hospitals transmitted patient data to Facebook via embedded Meta Pixel tracking. Congress failed to pass comprehensive health data privacy legislation through 2025, leaving 33 states with no health-specific privacy law.[^1] Washington State's My Health My Data Act — enacted 2024 with a private right of action — remains an outlier.

The patients most surveilled are the patients least protected: incarcerated people whose medical records are accessible to corrections officers; adolescents whose EHR billing discloses reproductive care to parents; LGBTQ+ individuals whose care records exist in states that have criminalized them. And Black and Latina women, disproportionately prosecuted in pregnancy cases, whose health data exists in systems designed without their safety in mind.

## Innovation Without Equity

Innovation continues despite the destruction — femtech VC surged 55 percent to $2.6 billion in 2024[^17], shifting from healthtech apps toward biopharma. The FDA cleared the first AI-powered cervical cancer screening system (February 2024) and the first predictive AI for five-year breast cancer risk from standard mammograms (June 2025). Non-hormonal male contraceptives entered human trials. State innovation funding is emerging as a hedge — Massachusetts invested $3.7 million in women's health grants in 2025[^23], California committed $6 million[^4] through its Initiative for Precision Medicine.

But venture capital funds products for commercially insured patients in cities. All-female founding teams received approximately 2 percent of global VC in 2024[^6], with deal sizes of $5.2 million versus $11.7 million for male teams.[^24] Black women founders received less than 2 percent of femtech VC.[^4] Fertility prediction algorithms built on white-centric datasets show reduced accuracy for Black and Hispanic women. AI diagnostic tools trained on datasets where fewer than 5 percent of dermatology images show dark skin,[^4] where EHR records for Black patients contain twice as many missing fields, where VBAC prediction calculators systematically assign lower success rates to women of color — these tools do not correct disparities. They automate them.

Post-Dobbs philanthropic investment in reproductive health grew 50 to 100 percent, but remains below $500 million annually[^8] versus the multi-billion-dollar federal baseline. The Society of Family Planning launched a $10 million emergency fund.[^4] MacKenzie Scott contributed over $50 million.[^16] Private philanthropy cannot replicate the scale, continuity, or geographic reach of federal research. Rural women — 15 to 19 percent of the population — represent less than 5 percent of clinical trial participants.[^15] Only one in ten rural counties has an OB/GYN. The research desert maps onto the mortality crisis.

We will have better drugs and more sophisticated technology. We will have less understanding of why Black women die in childbirth at three times the rate of white women. Innovation without equity research is not progress. It is the widening of a gap.
