# Chapter 10: The Immigration Crisis

---

A physician who treats pregnant women learns, eventually, what it looks like when patients start disappearing.

It does not happen all at once. It happens appointment by appointment. A woman misses her twenty-eight-week visit. She had been coming reliably — every four weeks, then every two. Her glucose screen was abnormal; she needed follow-up. She does not reschedule. When the front desk calls, the phone rings and no one picks up. The chart stays open. The glucose stays unmanaged. The physician finds out what happened, eventually, from another patient who knows her — she went into labor at home because she was afraid to come to the hospital.

Physicians at Hennepin Healthcare in Minneapolis reported exactly this: patients with high-risk pregnancies laboring at home rather than coming in. The reason was not poverty. It was not distance. It was not cultural preference. It was ICE. On January 20, 2025, the Department of Homeland Security rescinded the sensitive locations policy that had, for decades, protected hospitals and clinics from immigration enforcement. The message was received instantly, and it was received by every undocumented pregnant woman in the country: the place you go to have your baby is now the place where they can come to take you.

In border regions, prenatal visits dropped 40 to 60 percent. Women called their clinics before appointments — not to reschedule, but to ask whether agents had been seen in the area. The clinical question was no longer "How far along are you?" It was "Is it safe to leave the house?"

The physician cannot treat a patient who does not come. Every missed visit is a missed diagnosis — gestational diabetes unmanaged, preeclampsia undetected, fetal growth restriction uncaught. Every missed diagnosis becomes a complication. Every complication becomes, in a system designed to deny these women coverage, an Emergency Medicaid presentation — the most expensive and most dangerous way to give birth.

## The Invisible Patients

There is no reliable count of how many undocumented pregnant women live in the United States. What is documented is this: undocumented women are categorically excluded from Medicaid in most states, cannot safely access clinics in the current enforcement climate, and cannot appear in the courthouses where protective orders are obtained without risking their own deportation. They exist in a healthcare system that simultaneously counts on their labor and refuses to count their deaths.

The reproductive health crisis for immigrant women does not begin at the border. It begins at the internal CBP checkpoints that create de facto internal borders, making it impossible for undocumented women in border-adjacent communities to travel to the nearest hospital, OB-GYN, or domestic violence shelter without passing through federal enforcement infrastructure. A pregnant woman in the Rio Grande Valley who needs specialty care in San Antonio must pass through a checkpoint. Many don't go. The resulting avoidance of prenatal care is not a personal choice — it is a rational survival calculation in a system that has made healthcare access and deportation risk inseparable. According to the Guttmacher Institute, an estimated 72 percent of undocumented women in abortion-restrictive states report an inability to travel to abortion-access states because of these interior checkpoints — a barrier that traps roughly 40,000 additional women per year inside states that have banned or severely restricted abortion care.[^2]

The chilling effect is now quantified. A 2026 analysis by the Center for American Progress found that 41 percent of federally qualified health centers in high-enforcement ZIP codes reported immigrant patient no-shows directly attributed to ICE fear.[^1] First-trimester prenatal care initiation among Medicaid-enrolled immigrants dropped 23 percent following the rescission of ICE's sensitive locations policy.[^1] Women are calling clinics to ask whether ICE has been spotted in the area before deciding whether to come in for a prenatal visit. This is the triage system the enforcement state has created — one in which the first clinical question is not "How far along are you?" but "Is it safe to leave the house?"

## The Erosion of the Immigrant Advantage

Epidemiologists have long documented an "immigrant advantage" — the paradox that newly arrived immigrants often have better birth outcomes than native-born Americans of the same demographic profile, despite lower income and reduced healthcare access. The advantage is attributed to strong social networks, healthier dietary patterns, and lower rates of substance use. The numbers are substantial: according to ACOG and data published in the American Journal of Obstetrics and Gynecology, the foreign-born overall maternal mortality ratio stands at 14.3 per 100,000 live births, compared to 22.7 for US-born women.[^3]

The advantage disappears with time in the United States — and it disappears fastest for the women who need it most. The longer an immigrant woman lives in this country, the worse her birth outcomes become, her risk profile converging with and eventually mirroring the disparities experienced by native-born women of her racial and ethnic group. Black immigrant women lose the healthy immigrant effect most rapidly, with a maternal mortality ratio of 31.2 per 100,000.[^3] The current enforcement environment is accelerating this erosion. In high-enforcement states, the advantage has not merely eroded — it has reversed. Undocumented Latina women in these states now have an estimated maternal mortality ratio of 26.4 per 100,000, exceeding that of US-born Latinas.[^3] The stress of immigration enforcement, social isolation produced by fear of public activity, and avoidance of institutions that could provide support are the mechanisms through which the advantage collapses. In the current climate, the advantage may not survive first contact with the American healthcare system at all.

The biological pathways are becoming clearer. A study published in JAMA Psychiatry found that 71 percent of pregnant undocumented women in enforcement-heavy communities screen positive for anxiety, 54 percent for depression, and 38 percent for prenatal PTSD — compared to 11 percent in the general perinatal population.[^4] Chronic immigration enforcement stress activates the HPA axis, elevating cortisol throughout pregnancy, creating measurable pathways to preterm birth and disrupted fetal neurodevelopment. Infants born to enforcement-exposed mothers show elevated cortisol at six months of age — the stress response dysregulated before the child can speak. A University of Michigan analysis found that preterm birth rates among undocumented Latina mothers have risen to 11.4 percent, up from 8.9 percent in 2022[^5], with county-level ICE enforcement intensity used as a natural experiment for causal identification. The healthy immigrant effect in birth outcomes is now reversed for undocumented Latinas as a population.

## Mixed-Status Families and the Arithmetic of Fear

The numbers begin with children. According to the Migration Policy Institute, 4.4 million US-citizen children live with at least one undocumented parent.[^6] These children are American citizens whose healthcare is held hostage by their parents' immigration status. When an undocumented mother avoids a clinic, her citizen child also misses well-child visits, vaccinations, and developmental screenings.

Mixed-status family structures reshape reproductive decision-making itself. Nearly half — 48 percent — of mixed-status couples report that immigration anxiety directly affects their reproductive decisions.[^6] A third have delayed planned additional children to reduce household vulnerability.[^6] Nineteen percent report using contraception more consistently specifically because of enforcement fear[^6] — not as reproductive autonomy, but as threat mitigation. Some families move in the opposite direction: 22 percent of DACA recipients surveyed by the National Immigration Law Center chose earlier childbearing than planned, hastening pregnancy before anticipated deportation.[^7] Fifty-eight percent of DACA recipients — now aged 26 to 38, in their peak reproductive years — changed or delayed reproductive health decisions because of program uncertainty.[^7] Thirty-one percent discontinued long-term birth control to "prepare for pregnancy before deportation."[^7] This is reproductive planning conducted under duress, family formation distorted by the state.

The birthright citizenship executive order issued in January 2025 intensified the crisis. Thirty-seven percent of pregnant undocumented women surveyed reported changing their plans about prenatal care enrollment in response[^8], and Medicaid prenatal enrollment for undocumented women dropped 19 percent following the announcement[^8] — despite the order being immediately challenged in court. Twenty-four percent of women surveyed considered delivering outside the United States entirely.[^8] The chilling effect does not require a policy to take effect. The announcement is sufficient.

## Detention Conditions

In May 2025, CBP eliminated protections for pregnant detainees — stripping away requirements for water, food, formula, and diapers that had been established under prior administrations. The consequences are now documented in government data obtained through FOIA. As of February 16, 2026, 86 pregnant women were being held in ICE detention, including 9 in their third trimester.[^9] Between January and September 2025 alone, 16 miscarriages occurred in ICE custody.[^10] In the fourteen months from January 2025 through February 2026, 363 pregnant, postpartum, or nursing immigrants were deported.[^10] Women reported being shackled and restrained while actively miscarrying. One woman was deported 10 days after being diagnosed with a missed miscarriage — without receiving treatment in the United States — and required emergency care in Honduras.[^9]

Eighty-nine percent of pregnant asylum seekers report receiving no prenatal care during CBP detention, where the median custody time for pregnant women reached 18 days in early 2026.[^11] The maternal complication rate in custody is estimated at three to five times the general population rate.[^11] Sworn testimonies document pregnant women experiencing miscarriage in detention cells without medical attention. ICE detention facilities are not bound by the same healthcare standards as state or federal prisons — and those standards, as this report documents in its chapter on incarceration, are already inadequate.

The intersection of immigration detention and reproductive health produces a unique category of harm: women who are simultaneously subject to carceral health standards and immigration administrative processes, with no guaranteed access to prenatal care, no clear pathway to abortion where it remains legal, and no advocate in the system whose mandate is the health of the pregnant detainee rather than the enforcement of immigration law. ACLU documentation found 18 cases of detained immigrant women denied timely abortion access in 2023-2024, with an average delay from request to access of 22 days.[^12] Six women exceeded gestational limits due to the delay.[^12] Four were transferred to more restrictive states while their abortion request was pending.[^12]

The Irwin County Detention Center case has continued to expand. The 2020 nurse whistleblower who reported non-consensual gynecological procedures on detained immigrant women exposed a pattern that, by 2026, has grown to more than 40 identified women who received procedures — including hysterectomies and oophorectomies — without adequate informed consent.[^13] Non-English-speaking women signed consent documents they could not read. Fourteen active lawsuits are now pending.[^13] The contractor physician lost his medical license. This is not a rogue actor. It is the predictable outcome of a system that concentrates power over reproductive bodies in facilities with no independent medical ethics oversight.

## The Coverage Architecture

The 5-year bar on Medicaid eligibility for legal immigrants — even green card holders — means that women who have followed every legal process available to them are still denied the coverage that funds prenatal care, delivery, and postpartum mental health treatment. Emergency Medicaid covers the delivery itself in most states, creating a system in which the government will pay for the most expensive moment of a pregnancy (the birth) but refuses to fund the prenatal care that would have prevented complications making that birth more expensive. The data confirms the absurdity: according to the Urban Institute and Health Affairs, 44 percent of undocumented births are now Emergency Medicaid-only.[^14] Emergency C-section rates for these births run at 31 percent, compared to 22 percent for births with full Medicaid prenatal coverage.[^14] Hemorrhage rates are double — 18 percent versus 9 percent.[^14] ICU admissions run four times higher.[^14] The average hospital cost for an Emergency Medicaid birth is $14,200, compared to $8,400[^14] for a birth preceded by full prenatal care. The system pays more to produce worse outcomes.

States vary in their coverage of immigrant women. Some have extended state-funded coverage to undocumented pregnant women; others have not. The result is a postcode lottery layered on top of an immigration status lottery — a pregnant woman's access to care depends on which state she lives in, which documents she holds, and whether she can reach a clinic without passing through an enforcement checkpoint.

The public charge rule has weaponized even the coverage that exists. After the Trump administration expanded the rule in 2025 to include Medicaid, SNAP, and other public benefits as factors weighing against green card applications, the Kaiser Family Foundation found that 34 percent of low-income immigrant women withdrew from or declined Medicaid.[^15] Twenty-nine percent stopped using Title X-funded family planning clinics.[^15] Eighteen percent skipped prenatal visits entirely.[^15] The original 2019 public charge rule announcement — before the rule even took effect — drove 13.8 percent of immigrant adults in mixed-status families to disenroll from Medicaid and prenatal care. Hispanic and Latino families were hit hardest, with 15.2 percent avoiding benefits. The rule was eventually blocked and withdrawn, but the chilling effects persisted for years. Fear, once introduced into a community's relationship with its healthcare system, does not retract on the same timeline as the policy that created it.

## DACA, TPS, and the Contraception Collapse

Two hundred fifty thousand women hold Temporary Protected Status[^16] for countries including El Salvador, Honduras, Haiti, and Venezuela. As the Trump administration terminates or fails to renew TPS designations, these women — many of whom have lived legally in the United States for 15 to 25 years — lose work authorization, employer-sponsored insurance, and with it, access to contraception and prenatal care. The National TPS Alliance found that 29 percent of TPS-holding women of reproductive age discontinued hormonal contraception following the loss of employer insurance tied to work-authorization lapse.[^16] Thirty-eight percent changed their reproductive plans entirely.[^16]

The contraception collapse extends beyond TPS holders. According to the Guttmacher Institute and the National Latina Institute for Reproductive Justice, the unintended pregnancy rate among undocumented immigrant women rose 34 percent between 2022 and 2025 — from 47 to 63 per 1,000 women.[^17] LARC uptake dropped 41 percent[^17], specifically because LARCs require a provider visit for insertion, making them the most avoidance-sensitive method. Multiple mechanisms converge: clinic avoidance from ICE fear, Title X clinic closures, public charge deterrence, and community isolation. Planned Parenthood closed 32 clinics in 2026 across ten states.[^18] Eight hundred seventy-nine Title X clinics — 24 percent of the national total — had their funding withheld pending a so-called civil rights review by HHS, even after Congress kept Title X funding steady in the February bipartisan budget.[^18] The infrastructure that provided contraception to immigrant women is being systematically dismantled.

## The "Abortion Trafficking" Framework

Multiple states have introduced or enacted "abortion trafficking" laws that criminalize assisting minors — and in some proposed versions, any person — in traveling across state lines for abortion care. For immigrant women in border states with complete abortion bans, this legal architecture creates a double bind: the nearest legal abortion may be across a state line that new legislation aims to make uncrossable, while the nearest international border leads to a country whose healthcare system may be more accessible but whose crossing triggers re-entry risks. Before Dobbs, a 2018 border community study found that 22 percent of immigrant women were already crossing into Mexico to access reproductive healthcare services including abortion and contraception[^19] — a shadow cross-border system driven by US insurance exclusions and cost barriers. Dobbs made the shadow system the only system.

Immigrant women in abortion-ban states are 3.1 times less likely to access out-of-state abortion compared to US-born women in those same states.[^19] Sixty-eight percent of undocumented women in ban states who sought abortion in 2023-2024 could not access it.[^19] Sixty-one percent of immigrant women in ban states who had previously accessed abortion did so via medication abortion — a pathway now eliminated or severely restricted in 21 states.[^20]

The chilling effect extends beyond abortion. Community health centers that serve immigrant populations report declining utilization across all services — not just reproductive health — as fear of data sharing between health systems and immigration enforcement agencies suppresses healthcare-seeking behavior broadly. Cervical cancer screening rates among undocumented immigrant women stand at 54 percent, compared to 83 percent among US-born women.[^21] Late-stage diagnosis runs at 48 percent versus 29 percent.[^21] These are women dying of a cancer that is almost entirely preventable through routine Pap smears — screenings they are too afraid to seek. Women who avoid clinics for fear of deportation are not only forgoing prenatal care. They are forgoing cancer screening, STI treatment, contraception, and well-child visits for their U.S.-citizen children.

## Separated Families, Invisible Children

Mass deportation operations do not pause for postpartum status. In the first quarter of 2026, 47 documented cases emerged of postpartum women deported within eight weeks of delivery.[^22] Twelve US-citizen newborns were left with relatives after maternal deportation.[^22] Breastfeeding cessation occurred in 100 percent of deportation-separated mother-infant pairs.[^22] Two deported mothers died from unmanaged postpartum complications in their countries of origin. Forced separation disrupts neonatal bonding, postpartum mental health, and the basic biological relationship between a mother and her newborn.

The children who remain face their own crisis. Over 37,000 unaccompanied minors were in ORR custody in fiscal year 2023.[^23] Thirty-one percent of girls in ORR custody reported sexual assault during their migration journey.[^23] Access to Plan B and emergency contraception was blocked in 43 percent of ORR facilities — many of which are operated by faith-based contractors that refuse reproductive healthcare on religious grounds. Abortion access was denied or severely delayed in all documented cases. These are girls who were raped during the most dangerous journey of their lives, housed by a government that will not provide them emergency contraception.

Meanwhile, 130,000 unaccompanied minors entered the workforce, with 66 percent working full-time as of 2022.[^24] Child labor violations surged 88 percent since 2019, reaching 5,792 children in violation in fiscal year 2023.[^24] The pipeline is seamless: children arrive unaccompanied, are placed in ORR custody where their reproductive health needs are ignored, are released to sponsors where oversight collapses, and enter exploitative labor where their bodies are consumed by an economy that refuses to acknowledge they exist.

## The Sanctuary Evidence

The data makes the case for sanctuary. A Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health analysis published in Health Affairs found that immigrant women in sanctuary jurisdictions are 2.3 times more likely to initiate prenatal care in the first trimester compared to women in non-sanctuary areas.[^25] Emergency-only obstetric presentations are 47 percent lower.[^25] Late-stage cervical cancer diagnoses are 31 percent lower.[^25] The effect persists after controlling for socioeconomic status and insurance coverage. Sanctuary policies do not create a parallel healthcare system. They create the conditions under which immigrant women can use the system that already exists.

## What the Administration Is Doing

The Trump administration's immigration enforcement posture and its reproductive health policy agenda are producing compounding effects that no prior administration has generated at this scale. The same government that eliminated pregnant detainee protections is the government that reinstated the Global Gag Rule as a "Mega-Gag" applying to $30 billion in foreign assistance — affecting the reproductive healthcare infrastructure of the countries these women fled. The same government that operates internal checkpoints preventing access to care is the government that banned undocumented children from Head Start, producing a 20 percent enrollment decline in some counties and removing the early childhood infrastructure that serves the U.S.-citizen children of undocumented parents.

The policy is coherent: restrict reproductive access for immigrant women at the border, in detention, within the country's interior, and in the countries of origin that would have provided care had these women been able to stay. There is no point in this system where an undocumented pregnant woman encounters a policy designed to help her. Every policy she encounters is designed to exclude her — from coverage, from care, from data, and from the count that would make her visible.

## The Other Invisible Patients: Women Veterans

Over 900,000 women veterans are now enrolled in VA healthcare — the fastest-growing segment of the veteran population, with 50,000 new enrollees in a single year (2023-2024).[^26] In fiscal year 2024, a record 741,259 women veterans received VA compensation payments totaling $20.4 billion in benefits.[^26]

On December 22, 2025, the Trump administration implemented a near-total ban on VA-provided abortion services.[^26] The Biden-era policy had allowed VA abortion care in cases of rape, incest, or threat to the life or health of the veteran. The new policy, based on a DOJ memo, now permits abortion only in narrow life-saving circumstances — effectively eliminating coverage for abortions resulting from rape or incest. The ban also prohibits VA providers from offering abortion counseling.

The cruelty is specific: the women most directly affected are those who experienced military sexual trauma — the same population the VA was designed to serve. A woman veteran who was raped during active duty, who is enrolled in VA healthcare, cannot access abortion care through the healthcare system that exists because of her service. She cannot even receive counseling about her options from her VA provider. The ban does not merely restrict a procedure. It silences the providers who might help her navigate a situation that the military itself created.
