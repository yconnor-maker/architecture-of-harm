# Chapter 3: Funding and the Safety Net — What Was Cut and Who Loses

*The money that kept the system alive.*

## The Baseline: The Cost of Survival

The United States was already the most expensive country in the world in which to have a baby — and simultaneously the most dangerous wealthy country in which to have one. The U.S. maternal mortality rate of 13.9 per 100,000 live births in 2023 remains two to four times higher than peer nations — Canada at 7.5, the United Kingdom at 9.3, Germany at 6.2. For Black women, the rate is 37.3 per 100,000. For American Indian and Alaska Native women, it is 26.2. According to the CDC, eighty percent of these deaths are preventable. Long before the current administration began dismantling the safety net, the financial architecture of maternity care was failing American families.

Medical debt was the leading cause of personal bankruptcy, accounting for over 60% of all filings. The cruelest data point within that statistic: two-thirds of those bankruptcies were filed by people who *had* health insurance at the time of the medical event that bankrupted them. Childbirth systematically pushed middle-class families into poverty. 

For the 52% of pregnant women covered by private insurance, the average total cost of childbirth amounted to $20,416, with an average out-of-pocket burden of $2,743. For families enrolled in high-deductible health plans (HDHPs) — a growing proportion of the privately insured population — costs were catastrophic. Because of these out-of-pocket burdens, 24% reported unmet healthcare needs during pregnancy, and 60% reported healthcare unaffordability. 

The "financial toxicity" of maternity care is not just an economic issue; it is a clinical one. Nearly 25% of perinatal women report that current debt or concerns over future costs significantly worsen their mental health. When complications arise, the costs multiply. Hospital costs for commercially insured patients with severe maternal morbidity (SMM) are 111% higher than for standard deliveries. For Medicaid patients with SMM, the increase is 175%. The cost of a delivery associated with a maternal death is six times higher than a standard delivery — $2,802 versus $480.

Unlike every other wealthy nation, the U.S. has no guaranteed federal paid family leave. A mother in New Jersey might receive 85% of her wages while recovering from a cesarean section; a mother in Alabama must return to a minimum-wage shift two weeks post-surgery to avoid eviction. Women hold 64% of all U.S. student loan debt (approaching $1 trillion), with Black women carrying twice the educational debt of white women. And the childcare infrastructure functions as a complete market failure: working families span 8.9% to 16.0% of their median income on full-day care for a single child, well above the HHS affordability threshold of 7%.

This was the system *before* anything went wrong. This was the baseline structural fragility that the new administration inherited — and then aggressively dismantled.

## Dobbs as an Economic Multiplier

When the *Dobbs* decision removed reproductive autonomy, it acted as a massive economic multiplier on this already fragile baseline. The Turnaway Study — the most rigorous longitudinal study of pregnancy denial ever conducted — established the economic consequences with devastating precision.

Women denied a wanted abortion were:
- **Four times more likely** to fall below the federal poverty line ($15,060 per year).
- **Three times more likely** to be unemployed six months later.
- Highly vulnerable to financial ruin: 81% experienced increases in bankruptcy, evictions, and tax liens.
- 78% had past-due debt within the first year.

Crucially, 60% of the women in the study *already had children* at the time they were denied care. Denial did not affect one person. It destabilized entire families — the existing children absorbed the economic devastation, the housing instability, the violence. Women were also more likely to remain in contact with abusive partners, tethered to relationships they could not afford to leave.

Scaled nationally, the Institute for Women's Policy Research (IWPR) estimates a **$133 billion annual economic cost** across the 16 restriction states. This includes 556,042 women suppressed from the workforce. The brain drain is accelerating: over 129,000 residents fled the 13 ban states post-Dobbs, disproportionately prime-age, college-educated workers with the highest taxpayer value. Eight of the ten lowest-GDP states now have total or severe abortion bans.

For those who travel for care, a $500 procedure in a protection state costs $1,250 to $3,000 or more when factoring in last-minute flights, hotel stays, childcare, and lost wages. Average travel time quadrupled from 2.8 hours to 11.3 hours. Over 100,000 additional hours of travel are now required annually. ACA premiums in restriction states jumped 114%, from $888 to $1,904.

## The Dismantled Safety Net: Trump 2.0

If *Dobbs* was the economic shock, the policies of 2025 were the systematic dismantling of the institutions that could absorb it. The administration did not merely target individual programs. It restructured the federal health apparatus itself — collapsing HHS from 28 operating divisions into 15, eliminating approximately 20,000 positions, and absorbing SAMHSA, HRSA, and the Office of Head Start into a new entity called the Administration for a Healthy America. The workforce reduction was staggering: HHS went from roughly 82,000 employees to 62,000 in a single year. Across CDC, NIH, and FDA, 4,800 federal health workers were fired, with 20,000 more planned. The FDA alone lost a net 2,000 full-time employees after courts reinstated some of the 3,500 probationary staff initially terminated. These were not abstractions. These were the epidemiologists tracking maternal deaths, the scientists running clinical trials for safer contraception, the program officers overseeing grants that kept rural clinics open.

Within this reorganization, the administration targeted the three core pillars of the maternal health safety net: Medicaid, Title X, and WIC — and then moved beyond them.

### The Medicaid Unwinding and the OBBBA

Medicaid is the absolute foundation of maternal health in America. It covers 41% of all births — including 64% of births to Black mothers and 58% to Latina mothers. Nearly half (47%) of all rural births are covered by Medicaid, and nearly half of all rural hospitals are already operating at a loss.

The *One Big Beautiful Bill Act* (OBBBA), signed July 4, 2025, fundamentally rewrote this foundation. It instituted **$900 billion in proposed Medicaid cuts** over a decade. The reconciliation reduced the ACA's federal medical assistance percentage for expansion populations from 90% to 87% and eliminated provider tax financing mechanisms worth approximately $16 billion per year — the fiscal tools states use to draw down their federal match. The Congressional Budget Office projects these changes will produce 10 to 12 million Medicaid disenrollments over the coming decade.

The most immediate threat within OBBBA is the mandate for Medicaid work requirements (80 hours monthly) for expansion enrollees aged 19-64, effective January 2027. Work requirements are projected to cause **5 million people to lose coverage** — mostly due to bureaucratic hurdles, not clinical or financial ineligibility. The evidence is unambiguous. When Arkansas piloted work requirements in 2018, 18,164 people lost Medicaid coverage in four months. No significant increase in employment was documented. When Georgia launched its PATH program in July 2023, only 6,634 of an estimated 500,000 potentially eligible residents managed to enroll. The administrative machinery of work requirements does not promote employment. It promotes disenrollment. Total projected coverage loss across all cuts ranges from 4.6 to 8.6 million people, including 2.1 million women of reproductive age.

The Medicaid continuous enrollment unwinding had already set the stage. Between April 2023 and June 2025, approximately 25 million total disenrollments occurred. Sixty-nine percent were procedural — people lost for paperwork errors, not because they were ineligible. Among those disenrolled, 4.6 million were children, disproportionately Black and Hispanic, in states like Florida, Texas, and Georgia. These children did not become ineligible. Their parents missed a form.

Furthermore, CMS guidance limiting states' ability to use provider taxes to draw down federal Medicaid matching funds is a kill shot for rural obstetrics. Over 120 Critical Access Hospitals — operating at a median margin of 0.4% — face solvency risk from these cuts. The bill also penalizes states that use their own funds to cover undocumented pregnant women — reducing a state's federal Medicaid allotment if they attempt to provide prenatal care regardless of immigration status.

Maternal mortality is already 35% higher in states that refused to expand Medicaid. During COVID, maternal deaths surged 46% in non-expansion states versus 21% in expansion states. The 48-state postpartum extensions — one of the few genuine recent improvements — are now at risk of rollback as states face budget pressure from these new federal rules. As of March 2026, 49 states plus the District of Columbia had adopted 12-month postpartum Medicaid coverage. Only Arkansas and Wisconsin retained the 60-day federal minimum. That near-universal achievement now sits directly in the path of $900 billion in cuts.

### The Deliberate Collapse of Title X and ACA

Title X, the federal grant program for comprehensive family planning and related preventive health services, was not merely frozen. It was methodically strangled. Its $286.5 million appropriation had been stagnant for eleven consecutive years, unadjusted for inflation. Then, in March 2025, HHS withheld $65.8 million from 16 grantees serving nearly 900 clinics across 23 states — 24% of the entire Title X network — affecting 834,000 patients. The Office of Population Affairs, which administers Title X, was gutted from approximately 50 staff to one. The FY2026 presidential budget proposed eliminating the entire $286 million program. When continuation grant guidance was finally released in March 2026 — one week before expiration — it had been stripped of the "Quality Family Planning" standards and all equity mandates that had defined the program's clinical framework. 

Planned Parenthood was simultaneously defunded from Medicaid through the OBBBA's exclusion of "prohibited entities," eliminating approximately $400 million in annual Medicaid reimbursements to roughly 600 affiliates. An estimated 1.6 million patients lose access to PP-provided services. According to organizational data, Planned Parenthood prevents 822,000 pregnancies annually through contraceptive services alone. Its defunding projects more than 300,000 additional unintended pregnancies per year.

The safety valve for many women losing Medicaid — the Affordable Care Act (ACA) marketplace — was also severed. ACA marketplace enrollment had reached a record 24.3 million in plan year 2025, driven by enhanced premium tax credits that saved enrollees an average of $800 per year. Those enhanced subsidies expired on December 31, 2025. In the first quarter of 2026, **over 3.2 million women dropped their marketplace insurance plans** due to premium shock as average monthly premiums jumped 44%. The ACA's contraceptive mandate — requiring zero cost-sharing for all FDA-approved methods — was further undermined when the administration suspended enforcement in March 2025, placing 62.4 million women with employer-sponsored insurance at risk of losing no-cost contraceptive coverage. An expanded moral exemption rule allowed an additional 200,000 women to lose zero-cost coverage, on top of the 130,000 already exempted.

### Billing Frictions and Nutritional Cuts

Effective January 1, 2026, the administration replaced the global maternity bundled payment model with itemized billing for prenatal and postpartum care. Under a bundled payment, a single fee covered all prenatal visits, delivery, and postpartum care. Itemized billing generates a separate claim — with a separate copay and coinsurance — for *every* encounter. The result is intense financial friction at every appointment, precisely disincentivizing the two categories of care most proven to prevent maternal death. The women with the least financial margin will skip the visits; the skipped visits will become missed diagnoses; the missed diagnoses will become preventable deaths.

Finally, the Special Supplemental Nutrition Program for Women, Infants, and Children (WIC) faced severe cuts. The Cash Value Benefit for fruits and vegetables was slashed from $26/$52 down to $10/$13. Combined with government shutdowns and the administration's refusal to utilize available USDA contingency funds, this created immediate household food insecurity, forcing families to redirect rent money to pay the full cost of specialized infant formula.

### The Gutting of Federal Health Infrastructure

The destruction extended far beyond Medicaid and Title X. The administration executed a systematic dismantlement of the federal agencies that generate the evidence, train the workforce, and fund the programs on which maternal survival depends.

The CDC's budget was proposed for a **44% cut** — from $9.2 billion to $5.2 billion. The agency lost approximately 25% of its total workforce. Two of three branches of the CDC Division of Reproductive Health were eliminated. The CDC's STI surveillance laboratory was shut down at a moment when syphilis cases had reached an 80-year high of 207,000 in 2023 and congenital syphilis was at 3,882 cases — ten times the 2012 rate. Over 200 CDC datasets were removed or altered; 46% of the agency's regularly updated public surveillance databases had unexplained pauses, according to a study published in the *Annals of Internal Medicine*. CDC pass-through grants to state and local health departments were cut approximately $500 million, forcing program cuts in 37 states and putting 5,000 to 8,000 state and local public health jobs at risk.

The Pregnancy Risk Assessment Monitoring System (PRAMS) — 38 years of continuous pregnancy surveillance covering 81% of U.S. births — was suspended on January 31, 2025. The entire PRAMS team received reduction-in-force notices on April 1, 2025. No replacement was announced. Funding expires April 2026. Both House and Senate Appropriations Committees reinstated PRAMS funding in the FY2026 bill — Congress kept the money. HHS continues to execute the shutdown.

At NIH, the administration terminated 977 grants worth approximately $1.7 billion. NIH deployed automated keyword scanning to flag grants containing "disparity," "equity," "structural racism," and "social determinants" for termination — disproportionately eliminating cancer disparity research studying outcomes for Black and Hispanic women. The National Institute on Minority Health and Health Disparities — the only NIH institute dedicated to minority health, with a $223.5 million budget — saw 77 grants terminated, a 29.6% funding cut, and 61% fewer competitive grants. The institute itself was targeted for elimination. The NIH Office of Research on Women's Health, which enforces sex-inclusion mandates in clinical research, lost core senior staff and had discretionary funding frozen. The NIH FY2026 budget was initially proposed for a 40% cut to $27.5 billion before Congress restored it to $47.2 billion — but the year of grant freezes and stop-work orders had already driven early-career scientists from the field.

The Office of Health Equity and the Office of Minority Health — the two federal offices most directly responsible for producing race-stratified health data and policy guidance — were both eliminated under the anti-DEI executive order framework. The HHS Office on Women's Health was downgraded: its budget reduced approximately 40%, staff cut from 72 to 38, and its Deputy Assistant Secretary position eliminated. HHS instructed federal grant applicants to remove the word "Black" from applications — one of approximately 200 banned words and phrases. Researchers studying Black maternal mortality must now remove the racial descriptor to remain eligible for federal funding.

DOGE-directed audits of HHS identified programs with any gender-specific, race-specific, or equity-framing language as candidates for elimination. The SAMHSA Women's Mental Health Initiative — $27.5 million, the first major targeted allocation for women's behavioral health — was eliminated. The CDC's Racial and Ethnic Approaches to Community Health program (REACH), funded at over $100 million per year, was targeted. HRSA maternal health equity grants totaling over $150 million were affected. In January 2026, nearly $2 billion across 2,706 SAMHSA grants were terminated in a single administrative action. The terminations were reversed within 24 hours under bipartisan outcry — but the signal was unmistakable: any program could be killed by keystroke.

The Trump FY2026 budget proposed cutting the **Title V Maternal and Child Health Services Block Grant by $800 million** — from $1.25 billion to approximately $450 million. Title V is the primary federal funding stream for state maternal and child health infrastructure. It funds home visiting programs, prenatal care coordination, maternal mortality review committees, and programs for children with special health care needs across all 50 states, reaching approximately 35 million mothers, infants, and children annually. The proposed cut would eliminate or severely curtail these programs in every state. Home visiting programs serve predominantly low-income families of color — over 60% of participants are Black, Hispanic, or American Indian/Alaska Native. Defunding maternal mortality review committees would halt the race-stratified data collection that drives intervention.

Community Health Centers faced an equally devastating threat. The FY2026 budget proposed over $1 billion in cuts to the Community Health Center Fund. Federally Qualified Health Centers serve approximately 30 million patients at over 14,000 sites — 63% of patients are racial and ethnic minorities, and 94% of FQHCs provide reproductive health services. Budget reconciliation would cut an additional $10 billion from Section 330 funding over ten years. The AHRQ — the agency that produces the annual National Healthcare Quality and Disparities Report — was proposed for complete elimination before Congress restored $300 million.

Meanwhile, the Indian Health Service remains funded at 67 cents per dollar of comparable federal health spending. IHS per capita spending is approximately $3,900 — compared to $13,000 at the VA. The workforce vacancy rate exceeds 30%. American Indian and Alaska Native women have a maternal mortality rate of 29.7 per 100,000, the highest of any racial group with sufficient data. And in January 2025, IHS facilities were prohibited from providing abortion services even in cases of rape and incest — despite AI/AN women having the highest lifetime prevalence of sexual violence of any racial group at 84%.

### The Chilling Effect on Immigrant Care

DHS rescinded its sensitive locations policy on January 20, 2025, removing protections that had prevented ICE enforcement at hospitals and clinics. The clinical consequences were immediate and measurable: studies in high-ICE-activity counties documented a 20 to 28 percent decline in prenatal visits among Hispanic women. Federally Qualified Health Centers serving immigrant populations reported appointment no-shows increasing 30 to 40 percent in the weeks following the policy change. Pregnant immigrant women now face an impossible calculation — attend the prenatal appointment and risk deportation, or skip the appointment and risk dying.

## The Arithmetic of Destruction

The math underlying the destruction of the safety net is irrational. For every $1 spent on Medicaid, the system saves $7.09 in downstream costs. HRSA's Alliance for Innovation on Maternal Health — expanded to all 50 states by 2024 — demonstrated a 14 to 21 percent reduction in severe maternal morbidity in participating hospitals. Oregon's Medicaid doula program showed a 22% reduction in cesarean sections and a 3.7-fold improvement in birth outcomes for Black Medicaid enrollees. These programs work. They are being destroyed not because they fail, but because they succeed at keeping women — particularly Black women, Indigenous women, immigrant women — alive and independent.

The goal of these cuts is not fiscal responsibility. The goal is the structural dismantling of the mechanisms that support women's independence and survival.
