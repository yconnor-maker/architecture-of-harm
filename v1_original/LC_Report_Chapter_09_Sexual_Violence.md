# Chapter 9: Sexual Violence — The Scale Nobody Will Say Out Loud

## The Prevalence Numbers

54.3% of women in the United States have experienced contact sexual violence in their lifetime. That is the CDC's National Intimate Partner and Sexual Violence Survey. More than half of all women. 26.8% have experienced completed or attempted rape. 443,000 or more people aged 12 and older are sexually assaulted or raped each year. 75% of those assaults go unreported. At least 225,000 rape kits sit untested in evidence rooms across the country. And the forensic infrastructure to respond is collapsing — as documented later in this chapter.

79% of female rape survivors were first raped before age 25. 40% before age 18. The perpetrator is known to the victim in over 80% of cases. Less than one-third report to police. The CDC estimates the lifetime economic cost at $122,461 per survivor — totaling over $3.1 trillion annually.

Nearly 20 people per minute are physically abused by an intimate partner — more than 10 million women per year. The CDC estimates over 2,300 intimate partner homicides of women annually; 3,991 female IPV homicide victims were documented between 2018 and 2021 alone. 66.6% involved firearms. 98.5% had a male suspect. 68% occurred at the victim's residence. Sexual violence precedes 70% of femicides. There is no federal femicide tracking database.

## The Racial Disparity in Victimization and Justice

The numbers are not race-neutral. Black women experience sexual violence at 35% higher rates than white women, according to the CDC's NISVS. Black women aged 25 to 44 are six times more likely to be murdered than white women of the same age. American Indian and Alaska Native women report the highest lifetime rape prevalence of any racial group — 34.1%, compared to 22% for Black women, 18.8% for white women, and 14.6% for Latina women.

But the disparity does not end at victimization. It compounds at every stage of the response. Black survivors of sexual violence are 40% less likely to have their cases prosecuted than white survivors — a disparity that persists after controlling for assault type, location, and relationship to perpetrator. Researchers have documented a "Black woman believability gap" across criminal legal systems: police are less likely to classify reports from Black women as founded, district attorneys are less likely to bring charges, and juries are less likely to convict. Cases with Black suspects, by contrast, are prosecuted at higher rates regardless of the survivor's race.

Latina survivors face compounding barriers of a different architecture. Familismo norms, deportation fear, language access gaps, and police mistrust drive formal service utilization 50% lower than for non-Latina white survivors. Seventy percent of Latina survivors report family pressure not to seek services. Undocumented Latina women face the most extreme version of this trap — seeking justice means risking deportation. The U-visa program, designed to protect immigrant crime victims, has a backlog of over 350,000 applications against a statutory cap of 10,000 approvals per year. Survivors wait five or more years in unauthorized status, vulnerable to re-victimization the entire time.

## Homicide Is the Leading Cause of Maternal Death

Pregnancy-associated homicide exceeds all leading clinical causes of maternal death — hemorrhage, hypertension, infection, cardiomyopathy — by more than two times. The rate is 3.62 per 100,000 live births. Black women aged 18 to 24 die from pregnancy-associated homicide at 8 per 100,000 — four times the national average. According to a JAMA Network Open analysis, 76% of pregnancy-associated homicides involve firearms.

These are not clinical failures. These are violence failures. Most maternal health policy focuses on clinical causes of death. The thing that actually kills more pregnant women than any single clinical cause — their partners — is absent from the policy response. States that prohibit divorce finalization during pregnancy exhibit significantly higher rates of pregnancy-associated intimate partner homicide. The law traps women in the marriage; the marriage traps women in the violence.

Non-fatal strangulation — the signature of sexual domination within intimate partner violence — is a direct predictor of lethality. A survivor of non-fatal strangulation by an intimate partner is 7.5 times more likely to be killed by that partner. Strangulation precedes 25% of femicide cases, often leaves no visible marks, and causes traumatic brain injury in an estimated 50% of cases. The TBI itself can impair the survivor's ability to testify coherently, which then undermines the prosecution. Thirty-five states have enacted strangulation-specific felony statutes. But statutes without enforcement are words on paper.

## The Dobbs-Violence Connection

NBER and Tulane researchers documented a 7-10% increase in intimate partner violence in ban counties compared to non-ban counties, producing an estimated 9,000 to nearly 14,000 additional domestic violence incidents nationally — with an estimated $1.24 billion in social costs. Each additional state abortion restriction correlates with a 3.4% increase in the intimate partner violence homicide rate. When a woman cannot leave a pregnancy, she cannot leave the relationship. The pregnancy becomes a tool of control. The violence escalates. This is measured, published, peer-reviewed research. The UCSF Turnaway Study confirmed the mechanism longitudinally: women denied the abortion they sought stayed in contact with violent partners at significantly higher rates and experienced more subsequent intimate partner violence than women who received the care they needed.

Fourteen states with near-total abortion bans include no rape or incest exceptions. Guttmacher estimates 64,000 rape-related pregnancies occur annually in ban states, with 32,000 additional rape-related pregnancies forced to term in the first year after Dobbs alone. Survivors in ban states travel an average of 700 miles to reach an abortion provider. In six states, a police report is required to access even a nominal rape exception — but nationally, 75% of sexual assaults go unreported. The exception is structurally inaccessible to three-quarters of the women it claims to protect.

The geographic concentration of Black women in ban states compounds every disparity. Black women in Florida, Texas, and Georgia account for 44% of Black women in ban states and one-quarter of all Black women of reproductive age nationally. All three states have above-average maternal mortality rates, the strictest bans, and the most aggressive safety-net dismantlement.

## Reproductive Coercion and the Control Architecture

Reproductive coercion is not an edge case within intimate partner violence — it is one of its primary mechanisms. Traffickers use forced pregnancy as a control tactic in 60% of sex trafficking cases reviewed by the Polaris Project. In 15 states without rape or incest exceptions, trafficking survivors are denied abortion access entirely. The documentation requirements that exist in other states — police reports, identity verification, immigration status — are precisely the instruments a trafficker controls. The survivor cannot access the exception because the exception was designed for someone who is not being trafficked.

Healthcare providers identify fewer than 1% of trafficking victims, despite the fact that 88% of trafficking survivors have contact with a healthcare facility during their exploitation — an average of 4.5 encounters before anyone asks the right question. The training gap is not incidental. It is the difference between a clinical encounter and a rescue.

Emergency contraception, which prevents 89% of pregnancies when taken within 24 hours and 52% within 72 hours, could prevent an estimated 25,000 rape-related pregnancies annually. But conscience clause expansions in 2025 allow hospitals to refuse EC to assault survivors without a transfer mandate. Advocacy surveys report that 15% of ERs in abortion-ban states are declining to provide emergency contraception to sexual assault survivors. In rural areas where the ER is the only acute care option, refusal is tantamount to forced pregnancy.

## Child Marriage: The Legalized Pipeline

Child marriage remains legal in 34 U.S. states as of mid-2025. Between 2000 and 2021, at least 315,000 minors were legally married in the United States. 86% were girls, disproportionately married to adult men under parental and judicial consent statutes. Four states have no statutory minimum marriage age when all exemptions — parental, judicial, pregnancy, religious — are applied. A child of any age can be legally married.

The reform trajectory is real but slow. Complete bans on child marriage increased from two states in 2018 (Delaware and New Jersey) to 16 by mid-2025. The Federal Child Marriage Prevention Act of 2024 has not advanced through Congress. There is no federal marriage-age floor. And the geography of reform maps almost perfectly onto the geography of reproductive rights: 15 of the 16 states that have banned child marriage also preserve abortion access. Missouri is the sole exception. In the states where a minor rape victim can be forced to carry a pregnancy to term, she can also be legally married to her rapist.

## The Justice Gap: IPV Impunity and the Criminalization of Survival

The criminal justice system's response to intimate partner violence fails women at every stage. Only approximately 14% of reported IPV cases result in criminal conviction. 53% of IPV incidents are never reported to police. Nine states still retain marital rape exemptions or lesser penalties for spousal rape. The men who kill pregnant women are almost never held accountable before the homicide occurs.

When women act to protect themselves, the system reverses. 94% of incarcerated women have experienced domestic violence. 74% of women imprisoned for murder or manslaughter had experienced intimate partner violence within a year of the offense that led to their conviction. At least 30% of women serving time for murder or manslaughter — including those with life sentences — were acting in self-defense or protecting a child during the incident. Most state laws do not provide meaningful opportunity to assert self-defense during prosecution or to present abuse history as a mitigating factor in sentencing.

The incarceration rate for women is growing twice as fast as that for men. Black, Latina, American Indian, and Alaska Native women are disproportionately represented. The system does not prevent the violence, does not prosecute the men, and imprisons the women who survive.

## Campus and Military Sexual Violence

25.9% of female undergraduate students experienced nonconsensual sexual contact according to the Association of American Universities campus climate survey. The CDC's 2025 Youth Risk Behavior Survey found that 12% of female high school students reported forced sex in the past year — rising to 22% for LGBTQ+ youth. Title IX protections were expanded in 2022 to include LGBTQ+ protections and updated investigation standards. In January 2025, those expanded protections were fully rolled back, restoring the 2020 DeVos-era rule that narrowed the definition of sexual harassment and reinstated live hearing cross-examination of survivors — a procedural mechanism that research consistently shows re-traumatizes victims and reduces reporting. Students of color face compounded barriers, with documented racial disparities in campus adjudication outcomes.

Military sexual assault affects an estimated 134,000 service members per year when unreported cases are included, according to the DoD's own SAPRO data showing a 73% non-reporting rate. Female service members are 3.8 times more likely to be sexually assaulted than male peers. The VA reports that 42% of women veterans screen positive for military sexual trauma, with MST-related mental health treatment utilization increasing 28% in a single year. The VA abortion ban, enacted December 2025, contains no exception for rape or incest — affecting 460,000 women veterans of reproductive age, including survivors of military sexual trauma. Over 2 million women total receive VA healthcare.

## Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women

In 2024, 10,248 reports of missing Indigenous persons were filed with the FBI's NCIC. Murder is the third leading cause of death for Indigenous women and girls, at rates 10 times the national average. 84% of Indigenous women have experienced violence in their lifetime; 56.1% sexual violence specifically. They are 2.5 times more likely to experience sexual violence than any other racial group, with 96% of perpetrators being non-Native — and until 2013, tribal courts lacked jurisdiction over non-Native offenders.

VAWA 2022 expanded tribal jurisdiction, and Savanna's Act improved NCIC reporting of AIAN missing persons by 23%. But implementation has been incomplete. The Indian Health Service — the only federal healthcare system serving these communities — has been targeted for $900 million in cuts; twelve IHS facilities have closed since March 2025. The Urban Indian Health Institute maintains its own independent data because federal databases cannot be trusted. And the children who witness this violence carry it forward: Native children have PTSD rates three times the general population, shaped by intergenerational trauma from the boarding school system and ongoing exposure to domestic violence.

## Disability, Deafness, and Sexual Violence

The populations least visible in sexual violence data are often the most victimized. People with disabilities experience sexual violence at two to ten times the rate of non-disabled populations. Among women with intellectual disabilities, the lifetime prevalence reaches 83%. Dependency on caregivers, communication barriers, and power differentials create compounding vulnerability. Compulsory sterilization persists: 31 states still permit court-ordered sterilization of adults with intellectual disabilities, with approximately 600 sterilizations ordered annually.

Deaf and hard-of-hearing survivors experience sexual violence at 1.5 to 2 times general population rates, yet only 5% of SANE programs offer ASL-fluent providers for forensic exams. Sixty-two percent of SANE programs lack non-Spanish interpreter services entirely, and only 18% offer real-time interpretation for forensic interviews. Language access is the difference between evidence that holds up in court and evidence that does not.

## The FACE Act Pardons and the New Enforcement Reality

In January 2025, 23 individuals convicted under the FACE Act for violence against reproductive health clinics were pardoned. In June 2025, HIPAA reproductive privacy protections were vacated in Purl v. HHS. EMTALA emergency abortion guidance was rescinded. A pregnant woman walking into a hospital now faces risks that did not exist three years ago: her medical records can be subpoenaed, her physician may be legally obligated to report her, the clinic has no federal protection from violence, and the DOJ has dismissed pending FACE Act cases protecting abortion clinics while simultaneously prosecuting church intrusions under the same statute. Violence against reproductive healthcare facilities is no longer a federal enforcement priority.

## Immigration Enforcement as a Maternal Health Threat

Since the January 2025 executive orders expanding immigration enforcement, prenatal visits dropped 40-60% in border regions after ICE removed the sensitive-locations buffer. Physicians at Hennepin Healthcare in Minneapolis reported patients with high-risk pregnancies going into labor at home rather than coming to the hospital. The chilling effect extends to crime reporting: undocumented crime reporting dropped 40% in high-ICE-enforcement jurisdictions in 2025. An undocumented sexual assault survivor who shows up to testify may be detained at the courthouse. DHS OIG documented 14,340 sexual abuse complaints at immigration facilities over a five-year period, with 90% not properly investigated and complainants held 16% longer than non-complainants.

Trump 1.0 ended presumptive release for pregnant detainees in 2017 — resulting in over 4,600 pregnant women detained by 2018, documented miscarriages, and reported shackling. Biden reversed the policy. Trump 2.0 reinstated it. The 2025 detention death toll reached 33 — the highest since ICE began publishing records — with 95% documented as preventable.

## The SANE Shortage: No One to Collect the Evidence

The forensic nursing infrastructure is failing sexual assault survivors at scale. Fewer than 25% of U.S. hospitals have a trained Sexual Assault Nurse Examiner available — roughly 970 SANE programs across 6,146 hospitals. Only 39% of hospitals with any SANE capacity have 24/7 availability. In rural areas, certified SANEs are present in only 16.7% of counties, according to Penn State University research. An RTI International analysis found rural jurisdictions take three times longer to submit rape kits for testing, and 45% of rural law enforcement agencies lack written protocols for evidence collection. Rural sexual assault cases are 50% less likely to result in arrest. There are an estimated 3,000 SANE vacancies nationally.

A rape survivor in much of the country faces a choice: drive hours to a hospital with SANE capability, or go to the nearest ER where no one is trained to conduct a forensic exam — meaning the evidence that could lead to prosecution is never collected.

The shortage is compounded by funding. SANE programs rely on a patchwork of federal VAWA and state grants. The Violence Against Women Act reauthorization in 2022 expanded funding, but the Trump FY2026 budget proposes a 40% cut to VAWA grant programs — $258 million slashed from the $644 million FY2025 Office on Violence Against Women allocation. That funding supports approximately 1,600 rape crisis centers and SANE training programs. The proposed cut would force closures and eliminate forensic nursing positions in the communities that need them most. Meanwhile, sexual assault kit backlogs remain massive — at least 225,000 untested kits sit in evidence rooms, with Black and Latina survivors overrepresented in backlogged jurisdictions concentrated in under-resourced urban labs. The evidence exists. No one is processing it.

## The Sextortion-to-Pregnancy Pipeline

Sextortion — the online blackmail of minors using sexual images — has exploded to 26,718 reports to NCMEC by 2023. CSAM reports to the CyberTipline surged from 16.9 million in 2019 to 36.2 million in 2024 — a 114% increase, with AI-generated content now accounting for over 20% of reports. Financial sextortion has been linked to at least 20 documented youth suicides. The pipeline extends into reproductive health: sextortion victims are disproportionately adolescent girls simultaneously losing access to sex education, contraception, and abortion care. An adolescent coerced into sexual activity through digital exploitation, in a ban state with abstinence-only education and no contraceptive access, faces a forced pregnancy originating from sexual violence — with no legal recourse and potential prosecution. The sextortion crisis is the entry point of a pipeline that feeds directly into the maternal mortality statistics documented in Chapter 1.

## The Service Gap: Demand Without Capacity

National domestic violence and sexual assault programs turned away 11,105 service requests in a single 24-hour census count in 2023. Seventy-three percent were for emergency shelter. Sexual assault hotline calls have increased 23% since 2020. Housing instability and sexual violence form a bidirectional spiral: 38% of homeless women report sexual assault as the direct cause of their homelessness, while one in three homeless youth experiences sexual assault within 48 hours of their first street episode.

Sexual violence survivors have three times the PTSD rate, four times the suicide attempt rate, and three times the depression rate of non-survivors. They are 13 times more likely to abuse alcohol. These are chronic conditions requiring sustained treatment — treatment the system is simultaneously defunding. Forty-two percent of low-income survivors cite cost as the primary barrier to services. Crime victim compensation covers medical costs but not lost wages in 30 states. The system demands that survivors absorb the economic consequences of someone else's crime.
