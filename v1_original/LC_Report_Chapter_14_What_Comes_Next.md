# Chapter 14: What Comes Next — The Projections

## The Data Blackout

Within the next twelve to eighteen months, the United States will lose the capacity to comprehensively track what is happening to women and babies during and after pregnancy. PRAMS funding expires in April 2026. Many states cannot sustain Maternal Mortality Review Committee operations independently. CDC STI surveillance is already gone. Birth defects tracking is gone. The ART surveillance system is gone. The Safe to Sleep monitoring infrastructure is gone.

2023 is the last year for which we will have reliable, nationally comparable pregnancy outcome data. Everything after that is estimation, proxy measurement, and inference. The instrument that measured the damage was destroyed while the damage was accelerating.

## What the Trend Lines Tell Us

This report has documented what is happening. This section documents what we know is coming — not through speculation, but through the extrapolation of trajectories that are already in motion, accelerating, and no longer subject to the corrective mechanisms that would have slowed them.

### Projection 1: Maternal Mortality Will Rise — And We May Not Be Able to Measure It

The maternal mortality rate in states with abortion bans is already 62 percent higher than in states preserving access — 28.8 versus 17.8 per 100,000. A total national ban would produce an estimated 140 additional maternal deaths annually: a 21 percent overall increase and a 33 percent increase for Black women.

The factors documented in this report compound simultaneously:
- The behavioral health workforce is on a trajectory to be short 77,050 addiction counselors, 99,780 mental health counselors, and 99,840 psychologists by 2038. Overdose — the leading medical cause of maternal death — depends entirely on this workforce for its reversal.
- OB-GYN flight from ban states is accelerating (see Chapter 2 for the full infrastructure collapse analysis). Idaho has already lost 22-43% of its OBGYNs and most of its maternal-fetal medicine specialists. The same trajectory is occurring in Alabama, Texas, and Oklahoma.
- L&D closures have eliminated obstetric care in communities that cannot attract replacement providers. Fifty-five percent of U.S. counties already have no OB-GYN. These closures accelerated post-Dobbs and will continue accelerating as malpractice premiums rise and provider exodus compounds.

The PRAMS destruction means that by 2027, we will have no nationally comparable data on pregnancy outcomes. Maternal deaths will continue to occur. They will not all be counted. The political utility of the data blackout is not incidental — it is the design.

### Projection 2: Congenital Syphilis Will Become Endemic in the South

Congenital syphilis increased 700 percent over 10 years *with* surveillance intact — from approximately 500 cases in 2013 to 3,882 in 2023. The STI surveillance committee has been eliminated. The CDC's budget faces proposed cuts of 54 percent. The Bicillin shortage continues. The prenatal care infrastructure that would screen for syphilis — Title X clinics, community health centers, rural obstetric practices — is closing.

A disease increasing at double-digit annual rates will not reverse itself when you eliminate the screening, surveillance, and treatment infrastructure simultaneously. In the states with the highest rates — South Dakota, Mississippi, Arizona — congenital syphilis is on track to become endemic within this decade. This is a preventable disease — prevented by a $30 injection of penicillin administered during a prenatal visit that may no longer be available.

### Projection 3: The Uninsured Birth Rate Will Spike in January 2027

The Medicaid work requirements in the One Big Beautiful Bill Act take effect January 2027. CBO projects 5 to 10.3 million people will lose coverage. Medicaid funds 42 percent of all births in the United States. Maternal mortality is already 35 percent higher in states that never expanded Medicaid.

The women who will lose coverage are disproportionately low-income women of reproductive age — the same women already affected by the Medicaid unwinding, which stripped coverage from 25 million people (69 percent for paperwork failures). The work requirements will layer documentation of 80 hours of monthly employment onto a population that already could not navigate the administrative complexity of renewal.

The 12-month postpartum Medicaid extension is meaningless if a woman loses eligibility before becoming pregnant. The work requirements will strip coverage before pregnancy, guaranteeing later entry into prenatal care and higher rates of every complication this report has documented.

All ten states that have refused Medicaid expansion are controlled by Republican legislative trifectas — forgoing more than $11 billion annually in federal funding that would cover the 2.2 million uninsured adults trapped in the coverage gap. Black and Latino women in Texas, Georgia, Florida, Alabama, and Mississippi face the highest maternal mortality rates *and* the highest Medicaid gap rates in the country. The decision not to expand is political, made by legislatures whose composition is determined by the voter suppression documented below.

### Projection 4: Violence Against Pregnant Women Will Increase in Ban States

The NBER documented 9,271 to 13,998 additional intimate partner violence incidents in ban states. Each additional TRAP law correlates with a 3.4 percent increase in the IPV homicide rate. These are not projections — they are measured outcomes of existing policy.

The mechanisms are structural: women trapped in violent relationships by pregnancies they cannot terminate; a SANE infrastructure that covers fewer than 25 percent of hospitals; VAWA-funded victim services facing appropriations threats while the FACE Act pardons signal enforcement priorities. The pregnancy-associated homicide rate of 3.62 per 100,000 is based on pre-Dobbs data. The post-Dobbs rate in ban states will be higher.

### Projection 5: The Children Being Damaged Now Will Become the Maternal Deaths of 2040

This is the projection that matters most. The ACEs pipeline has been quantified at every link: 4 or more ACEs produce an odds ratio of 3.06 for preeclampsia. Each additional ACE increases preterm delivery risk by 13 percent. An ACE score of 6 reduces life expectancy by 20 years.

The children accumulating ACEs *right now* — in this specific administrative period — include:
- 4.6 million children who lost Medicaid in the unwinding (70 percent for paperwork)
- 65,000+ children in Head Start programs that closed during the October 2025 government shutdown
- Children in Head Start programs that lost 20 percent enrollment when undocumented children were banned
- Children in ban states experiencing the documented increase in anxiety, depression, and excess births among adolescents
- Children of the estimated 9,271–13,998 additional domestic violence incidents in ban states
- Children of imprisoned mothers being permanently separated under ASFA timelines
- Children born with neonatal abstinence syndrome in treatment deserts where MAT access is shrinking

These are not future risks. These are ACEs being loaded into children today, on a timeline that will produce measurable health consequences in their pregnancies between 2035 and 2050. The maternal mortality crisis of 2040 is being built right now, by policies being enacted right now, in children whose names we do not yet know.

### Projection 6: Female Labor Force Participation Will Decline

The economic pipeline is as measurable as the clinical one. The cost of raising a child through age 17 exceeds $310,000. Childcare prices surged 22 percent between 2020 and 2024, consuming up to 35 percent of income for low-wage families. Only 5 percent of the lowest-wage workers have paid family leave. The Institute for Women's Policy Research documented a $133 billion annual GDP drag from abortion restrictions. The Turnaway Study found women denied abortions four times more likely to fall below the poverty line. The fertility rate is at an all-time low not despite these policies but because of them — women who can choose are choosing not to have children in this environment. The women who cannot choose — low-income, young, rural, incarcerated, undocumented, trapped — bear the births and bear the consequences.

## The Democratic Infrastructure Under Siege

The projections above assume that the political conditions producing them will persist. They will persist because the mechanisms that would reverse them — elections, representation, policy change — are themselves under coordinated attack. This is not coincidence. It is architecture.

### The Voter Suppression–Maternal Mortality Feedback Loop

The Voting Rights Lab and the Brennan Center for Justice have documented a structural correlation that should be central to every maternal health conversation in this country: states with the most restrictive voting access have maternal mortality rates 2.4 times higher than states with the most expansive voting access. The correlation persists after controlling for income and urbanicity. The same policy regime — anti-democratic, anti-reproductive-rights — produces both outcomes simultaneously.

The mechanism is self-reinforcing. Communities with the highest maternal mortality — disproportionately Black and Latino — are the same communities targeted by voter suppression. With diminished political power, they cannot elect representatives who would expand Medicaid or reverse abortion bans. The policy environment worsens. More women die. The cycle continues.

According to the Sentencing Project, 4.6 million Americans are disenfranchised due to felony convictions — Black Americans at 3.7 times the rate of non-Black Americans. Women account for approximately 20 percent of the disenfranchised as female incarceration rises. Thirty-seven states count incarcerated people at prison location rather than home community for redistricting, and the Prison Policy Initiative documents approximately 1.6 million people mislocated — phantom constituents inflating the political weight of rural, predominantly white districts at the expense of the urban Black and Latino communities where those people's families live.

### The Callais Cascade

On April 29, 2026, the Supreme Court ruled 6-3 in *Louisiana v. Callais* that a second majority-Black congressional district in Louisiana was an unconstitutional racial gerrymander — effectively allowing states to use partisan affiliation as a proxy for race in redistricting.

Within three weeks, four Southern states — Louisiana, Tennessee, Alabama, and Mississippi — initiated mid-decade redistricting targeting majority-Black districts. Louisiana postponed its congressional primary to accommodate a new map eliminating one of two majority-Black districts. Tennessee split majority-Black Memphis — 64 percent Black — into three Republican-leaning districts, projecting a 9-0 Republican delegation. Alabama petitioned the Supreme Court to dissolve the *Milligan* injunction. Mississippi announced plans to eliminate its lone majority-Black district before 2027.

The combined effect threatens the elimination of three to four Black congressional representatives in states where Black populations range from 17 to 38 percent — the same states with the highest maternal mortality, the most restrictive abortion bans, and the deepest refusal to expand Medicaid.

### The New Barriers to the Ballot

The SAVE America Act, which passed the House in February 2026, requires documentary proof of citizenship presented in person for federal voter registration. According to the Brennan Center, approximately 21 million voting-age citizens cannot readily access such documentation, and 3.8 million lack any form entirely. Birth certificate replacements cost $10 to $60 with weeks-long processing — a de facto poll tax. Three states enacted their own proof-of-citizenship laws within a single week in spring 2026: Utah created a two-tier voting system restricting citizens without proof to federal-only ballots; Mississippi's SHIELD Act mandates annual voter roll purges; Florida's HB 991 requires flagged voters to produce birth certificates or passports or lose registration.

The SAVE Act would eliminate online voter registration in 42 states and terminate the community registration drives that registered 2.4 million voters between 2022 and 2024. Native Americans on reservations face compounding barriers — no accepted street addresses, DMV locations requiring 50-mile travel, and gaps in pre-1960s birth records.

The Trump DOJ has halted enforcement of Voting Rights Act Section 203 bilingual requirements in at least five states, leaving 30 million limited-English-proficient citizens without ballot access. The John Lewis Voting Rights Advancement Act has failed the Senate filibuster in four consecutive Congresses. Fourteen formerly covered states have enacted over 1,000 discriminatory voting changes since *Shelby County v. Holder* in 2013.

## The States That Are Fighting

The data does not only document destruction. It documents resistance — and resistance that is working.

### The Direct Democracy Bypass

When gerrymandered legislatures enact abortion bans that contradict majority public opinion, the ballot initiative is the corrective. In August 2022, Kansas — a deep-red Republican trifecta — became the first post-Dobbs test: 59 percent of voters rejected an anti-abortion constitutional amendment, with over 900,000 casting ballots in a typically low-turnout primary. That result became the template. In 2024, voters in seven of ten states approved abortion-rights ballot measures. Florida's received 57 percent but failed a legislature-imposed 60 percent supermajority requirement. Since 2022, nine state constitutions have been amended to explicitly protect abortion rights, winning at margins of 55 to 73 percent.

Eleven states have retaliated by restricting initiative processes — raising signature thresholds, requiring geographic distribution, and imposing supermajority requirements of 60 to 67 percent. As of May 2026, abortion rights advocates are pursuing ballot measures in at least eight states for November, including second attempts in Arizona and Nebraska.

### The Protective State Models

Democratic trifecta states have built legal architectures — shield laws, statutory codification, state-level EMTALA protections — that serve as templates for reconstruction. Critically, they have linked reproductive rights to democratic access.

Twenty-one states plus D.C. allow same-day voter registration. These states show 5 to 7 percent higher turnout and 8 percent higher participation among young women and women of color. Zero same-day registration states have total or six-week abortion bans. California's automatic voter registration at Medicaid enrollment offices alone added 2.1 million voters. An estimated 8 million unregistered eligible voters in ban states could be reached through healthcare-based registration if adopted nationally.

New Mexico and Colorado achieved female legislative majorities in 2025. Women hold a record 33.4 percent of state legislative seats nationally. Women of color hold 13.7 percent — up from 9.8 percent in 2019 but below the 20 percent population share. In anti-abortion trifecta states, women of color hold only 7.2 percent. The representation gap and the maternal mortality gap are the same gap.

## The 2026 Midterm Stakes

The November 2026 midterm election is not an ordinary election for women's health. It is the election that determines who controls the redistricting maps drawn after the 2030 Census — maps that will govern political representation through 2041. Every state legislature elected in 2026 will participate in drawing those maps. The EMILY's List 2026 cycle has endorsed 187 state legislative and 43 congressional candidates — all explicitly pro-choice — with $45 million raised by May 2026 and a strategic focus on state legislatures that will control 2031 redistricting.

Thirty-four Senate seats are contested, with abortion determinative in at least seven competitive races. The administration has confirmed 60 Article III federal judges in the first 120 days of its second term — lifetime appointments that will shape reproductive rights litigation for 30 to 40 years. The Senate filibuster has blocked the Women's Health Protection Act four times since Dobbs.

State supreme court elections are equally critical. Since 2022, pro-choice justices have won 23 of 34 contested seats — a 2-to-1 ratio. The 2023 Wisconsin race drew a record $51 million, flipping the court to a liberal majority and effectively nullifying the state's 1849 abortion ban. Anti-abortion dark money spent $43 million in 2024 state attorney general races alone, outpacing pro-access spending 3-to-1.

The mobilization infrastructure is building. The Black Women's Roundtable targets 1.2 million Black women voters in eight Southern states, framing disenfranchisement and forced birth as parallel mechanisms of control. Young women are registering at twice the rate of young men in ban states, though youth turnout declined to 42 percent in 2024 as a widening gender gap within Gen Z reflects young men's rightward shift.

The DOGE-directed staffing reductions at the Census Bureau compound the stakes. The 2020 Census undercounted the Black population by 3.3 percent, the Latino population by 4.99 percent, and Native Americans on reservations by 5.6 percent. A degraded 2030 Census will lock those undercounts into place for a full decade.

## The Legal Resistance Landscape

ACOG, the AMA, and over 75 medical professional societies have filed amicus briefs in post-Dobbs cases, escalating to direct lobbying and physician political mobilization. Federal lawsuits challenging proof-of-citizenship laws are active in Florida, Utah, and Mississippi.

The post-Dobbs legal strategy has shifted from federal constitutional defense to building state constitutional protections through ballot initiatives and litigating their enforcement through favorable state courts. It requires winning judicial elections, which requires voter mobilization, which requires defeating voter suppression. The circularity is the point. Breaking the cycle at any link creates the opening to break it at the others.

## The Information Suppression Architecture

This is not an information deficit problem. It is an information suppression problem. Over 200 CDC datasets removed or altered. PRAMS destroyed. Seventy-five percent of sexual assaults unreported by design. CPS referrals dropped 18 percent when mandated reporters disappeared during school closures. Seventeen consecutive search failures documented when querying federal public health data on sexual violence affecting children. The data is being hidden, filtered, removed, and defunded at every level simultaneously.

Since official surveillance systems have been dismantled, the Labora Collective is building alternative measurement capacity — hospital ICD codes, 911/EMS records, poison control data, hotline volumes, NCMEC CyberTipline data, insurance claims patterns, RestoredCDC.org, direct database access, academic partnerships. We are archiving everything, because the alternative is silence, and silence in the face of this data is complicity.

## The Reconstruction Agenda

The evidence base is not the problem. The political will is the problem, and political will is a function of political power. The reconstruction agenda is therefore a power-building agenda:

**Restore Medicaid.** Reverse the work requirements. Expand in the ten holdout states. Make the 12-month postpartum extension permanent and federally mandated. Medicaid covers 65 percent of births to Black mothers. It is the single largest lever for reducing maternal mortality, and it is under deliberate, documented attack.

**Protect the ballot.** Defeat documentary proof of citizenship requirements in court and at the polls. Adopt same-day registration, automatic voter registration at healthcare settings, and ranked choice voting — which FairVote research shows produces 25 to 30 percent higher win rates for women and candidates of color. Restore federal preclearance under the Voting Rights Act. End prison gerrymandering. Restore felony re-enfranchisement.

**Win the maps.** The 2026 state legislative elections determine who draws the 2031 redistricting maps. Every pro-choice state legislative seat won in November is a seat at the table when congressional districts are drawn for the next decade. The Callais decision made winning state legislatures more important, not less — because it means future majority-minority districts can only be drawn by legislatures that want to draw them.

**Win the courts.** State supreme court elections are now the primary venue for reproductive rights litigation. Since 2022, pro-choice justices have won at a 2-to-1 ratio. That ratio must hold. Every state judicial election is an abortion election.

**Rebuild the data.** The surveillance systems destroyed in this period took decades to build. PRAMS took 38 years. The alternative measurement infrastructure we are building cannot replace what was lost, but it can ensure that the damage is documented, that the trends are tracked, and that when the reconstruction begins, the baseline exists.

## The Verdict

Through two terms in office, a pandemic, and the judicial transformation of the American legal system, Donald Trump and the movement he leads have produced the most devastating period for women's and children's health in modern American history. Not through a single dramatic act. Through simultaneous, documented federal actions — each one deniable in isolation, each one devastating in aggregate — that systematically dismantled the infrastructure protecting, monitoring, treating, and counting the most vulnerable people in the country.

The administration that did this claimed it was protecting families. The data tells a different story. Every metric for women got worse. Every metric for children got worse. Every system designed to help them was defunded, fired, frozen, or eliminated. The money was redirected. The power was consolidated. The instruments that would have measured the damage were destroyed.

The trends documented in this report are not reversible on short timelines. PRAMS took 38 years to build. A single OB-GYN takes 12 years to train. The surveillance data for 2024 and 2025 will never exist. The children accumulating ACEs cannot un-accumulate them.

But the tools of reversal exist — the ballot, the bench, the map, the data. The states that have used them are building an architecture of protection that did not exist before Dobbs. What can be done — what must be done — is to measure it, document it, publish it, make it impossible to deny, and then vote as though your life depends on it. Because for the women and children in this report, it does.
